LinkedIn Post: Imagination

Full Post

Tesla claimed he could run entire machines in his imagination for weeks, then mentally disassemble them to check for wear on imaginary components. When he built physical prototypes, they functioned exactly as his mental models predicted.

This sounds like inventor mythology until you consider: Tesla holds 300 patents, and his AC motor design from 1888 remains essentially unchanged in modern use.

I wrote Imagination to investigate the question: is the capacity to see what does not yet exist a trainable skill?

The neuroscience says yes. Brain imaging studies show that vivid mental rehearsal activates the same neural regions as physical execution. People who imagined playing piano developed the same motor cortex changes as people who physically practiced. The brain does not fully distinguish between a vividly imagined experience and a lived one.

Three findings from the book that changed how I approach creative and professional work:

  1. Imagination and fantasy are different skills. Fantasy drifts and entertains. Imagination is directed -- it holds a model of something that does not exist long enough for the conscious mind to examine, test, and refine it. The distinction determines whether mental effort produces outcomes or just pleasant feelings.

  2. Mental models are the operating system of imagination. First Principles, Inversion, Second-Order Thinking -- these are not academic concepts. They are lenses that determine what you see and therefore what you can build. The person with better models sees more clearly and builds more effectively.

  3. The imagination economy is real and measurable. The World Economic Forum ranks creativity as the third most important workforce skill, projected to reach first by 2030. As AI handles more execution, economic value migrates toward the human capacity to envision what does not yet exist.

The book covers Da Vinci, Tesla, Edison, Jobs, and Einstein -- not as historical trivia, but as case studies in a method that can be practiced: vivid mental simulation, rapid prototyping, iteration with memory.
