The Infinite Game

"There are at least two kinds of games. One could be called finite, the other infinite. A finite game is played for the purpose of winning, an infinite game for the purpose of continuing the play." — James P. Carse, Finite and Infinite Games, 1986

I want to tell you about two companies that launched in the same year, in the same market, with the same amount of funding.

The first one grew fast. It optimized for user acquisition, burned through capital on paid growth, hit impressive numbers in its first eighteen months. The metrics looked extraordinary on a slide deck. The founders talked about market dominance, about winner-take-all dynamics, about the race to scale. They raised a Series B at a valuation that made the tech press write profiles.

The second one grew slowly. It focused on a small group of users and made the product remarkably good for them. It didn't pursue virality. It pursued depth. The founders rarely gave interviews. They were too busy talking to customers.

I won't name either company, because the ending is still being written. But here is what I know so far: the first company's growth has stalled. The users it acquired cheaply turned out to have no loyalty — they came for the promotion and left when the next one appeared. The metrics that looked extraordinary on a slide deck turned out to measure activity, not value. The company is now trying to figure out how to retain users it never really had.

The second company has 4,200 paying customers, near-zero churn, and a revenue growth curve that looks like a straight line drawn with a ruler. It has never appeared in TechCrunch. Its founders could not tell you their company's valuation because they've never needed to calculate it.

The first company is playing a finite game. The second is playing an infinite one.

I. The Two Games

James Carse published Finite and Infinite Games in 1986, and the book has aged like good architecture — the structure still holds because it was built on something true.

A finite game has known players, fixed rules, and an agreed-upon objective. You play to win. When someone wins, the game ends. Chess is a finite game. A funding round is a finite game. A product launch is a finite game. Market share in a single quarter is a finite game.

An infinite game has known and unknown players, changeable rules, and no defined endpoint. You play to keep playing. The objective is not to win but to continue — to remain viable, to keep learning, to stay in the game long enough for the game itself to change in your favor. Evolution is an infinite game. A career is an infinite game. Building a body of work is an infinite game.

The distinction matters because the strategies that win finite games often lose infinite ones.

Optimizing for a funding round — a finite game — might mean inflating metrics, overpromising capability, spending faster than you earn. All of those strategies can win the finite game of getting funded. All of them make you more fragile in the infinite game of building something that lasts.

Optimizing for market share in a single quarter — a finite game — might mean underpricing, over-hiring, or copying a competitor's features instead of building your own. All reasonable finite strategies. All corrosive to the long-term positioning that infinite players depend on.

I've watched the AI industry play an extraordinary number of finite games in the past three years. Model benchmarks — finite game. Funding announcements — finite game. User count milestones — finite game. Each one produces a burst of attention, a moment of apparent victory, and then the game resets and the next round begins.

The builders I keep coming back to in this book — Danny Postma, Seth Kramer, Pieter Levels, the woman I called Sarah — are playing infinite games. They're not trying to win. They're trying to keep building. The distinction sounds subtle. It changes everything.

II. The Practice

There's a word I've been avoiding throughout this book because it carries connotations I don't intend, but I think it's the right word for this chapter: practice.

Not practice in the sense of rehearsal — doing something over and over to get better at it, though that's part of it. Practice in the sense that a doctor has a practice, or a lawyer has a practice. An ongoing body of work organized around a set of principles, sustained over years, accumulating expertise and reputation and relationships that compound.

A practice is not a product. A product can fail. A practice absorbs failure. A product has a lifecycle — launch, growth, maturity, decline. A practice doesn't decline. It evolves. The doctor who's been practicing medicine for thirty years is not doing the same thing she did in residency, but it's all one continuous thread of work.

I think the most important shift a builder can make is from thinking in products to thinking in practice.

When you think in products, every launch is a judgment. The product succeeds or it fails, and you succeed or fail with it. The emotional exposure is total. I've watched talented builders quit after a single product failure — not because they lacked skill, but because they'd invested their identity in a finite outcome.

When you think in practice, every launch is an experiment within a larger body of work. The product might succeed or fail, but the practice continues. The knowledge you gained, the audience you built, the technical skills you developed, the relationships you formed — those are assets of the practice, not the product. They persist regardless of any single outcome.

Marc Lou ships a new product roughly every month. Some work. Some don't. His practice — the accumulated knowledge of what markets respond to, what technical approaches scale, what marketing strategies convert — grows with every attempt. A product failure costs him two weeks of work and some hosting fees. It earns him data points that make the next product more likely to succeed. That's the math of practice: every input compounds, even the failures.

III. Optimizing for Learning

In 2019, a researcher named Celeste Kidd gave a talk at a machine learning conference that I still think about. Her topic was curiosity — specifically, the computational mechanics of how humans decide what to pay attention to and what to learn next. Her core finding was that humans are most engaged — most curious, most attentive, most likely to learn — when the information they're encountering is neither too predictable nor too surprising. Too predictable and the brain disengages. Too surprising and it can't integrate the new information with existing knowledge.

She called this the "Goldilocks zone" of learning. And what struck me about her work was how directly it maps to the experience of building in the AI era.

The builders who are thriving are the ones who've found their Goldilocks zone — working at the edge of their current capability, building things that stretch them without breaking them. They're not building the same thing over and over. They're not attempting things so far beyond their skill that they produce nothing usable. They're in the space between — each project slightly harder than the last, each one requiring them to learn something they didn't know.

This is what optimizing for learning looks like in practice. It's not reading papers or taking courses, though those have their place. It's building things that require you to learn in order to finish them. The learning is embedded in the work. It can't be separated from it.

I'll tell you what this looks like for me. When I started building with AI tools seriously, my projects were simple: blog posts assisted by language models, basic automations, straightforward prompts. Each project taught me something. The blog posts taught me how to direct AI output without losing my voice. The automations taught me how to chain AI capabilities together. The prompts taught me the difference between instructions that produce mediocre output and instructions that produce something genuinely useful.

Over time, the projects got more complex. An operating system with seventy-five skills. A research hub tracking seventeen domains. A music production workflow across eight genres. Each one was built on top of everything that came before — not just the code and content, but the judgment. The accumulated understanding of what works, what doesn't, and why.

That's not a product trajectory. It's a learning trajectory. And the learning trajectory is the thing that compounds.

IV. The Compounding Advantage

Albert Einstein may or may not have called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. The attribution is probably apocryphal, but the insight is real: systems that compound — where each period's growth builds on the previous period's total — produce outcomes that are dramatically nonlinear over time.

Financial compounding is the version everyone knows. But the version that matters most for builders is skill compounding.

Here's what I mean. In year one of building with AI tools, you learn the basics. How prompting works, what models are good at, what they're bad at, how to structure workflows. Your output is decent. You can build things that work.

In year two, you're building on top of year one's knowledge. You're not re-learning the basics — they're internalized. You're learning the second-order things: how to combine tools, how to build systems that improve themselves, how to design for edge cases, how to ship consistently. Your output is meaningfully better. The gap between you and someone starting from zero is widening.

By year three, the gap is enormous. You have pattern recognition that can't be taught in a course. You've encountered and solved hundreds of specific problems. You know which tools to use for which situations without thinking about it. Your judgment — the thing that AI can't replicate and that no tutorial can teach — is refined by thousands of micro-decisions.

This compounding is invisible on any given day. On any given Tuesday, the difference between a year-one builder and a year-three builder might be marginal — one makes a slightly better architectural choice, the other produces a slightly more effective prompt. But over the course of a year, those marginal advantages stack. The year-three builder ships faster, at higher quality, with fewer dead ends. The compound effect shows up in the portfolio: more products, better products, more satisfied users, more revenue, more options.

The builders who optimize for winning a single game — getting one product to succeed, hitting one revenue target, closing one funding round — miss this compounding effect. They're playing for the snapshot. The builders who optimize for learning — who treat every project as an investment in their practice — capture it. They're playing for the trajectory.

V. The Personal AI CoE

I've described the Personal AI Center of Excellence in different contexts throughout this book — as a framework, as a product, as a philosophy. In this final chapter, I want to describe it as what I think it actually is: an infinite game made concrete.

The six pillars of an enterprise AI CoE — Strategy, Governance, Talent, Technology, Data, Ethics — are not a checklist to complete. They're dimensions to develop. You don't finish your AI strategy. You refine it as your understanding deepens and the landscape changes. You don't complete your governance framework. You evolve it as you encounter new situations that your previous framework didn't anticipate. You don't stop developing talent — yourself, your team, your collaborators. The learning never ends because the field never stops moving.

This is what makes it an infinite game. There is no state of completion. There is no point at which you've won. There is only the ongoing practice of getting better — more capable, more efficient, more ethical, more creative — at working with intelligence systems that are themselves getting better.

When I built the Personal AI CoE framework and made it freely available, the response that meant the most to me came from a teacher in Portugal. She wrote to say that she'd used the framework to organize her approach to integrating AI into her classroom. She hadn't bought a product. She hadn't hired a consultant. She'd taken a framework designed for Fortune 500 companies, adapted it to her context — a public school with thirty-two students and a limited technology budget — and built something that worked. Her six pillars looked different from Oracle's six pillars. But the structure held.

That's the vision I want to end this book with. Not a world where a few thousand AI engineers build systems that everyone else uses passively. A world where millions of people — teachers, doctors, artists, small business owners, researchers, parents, creators — have their own intelligence infrastructure. Their own strategy. Their own governance. Their own continuously developing capability.

VI. What the World Looks Like

Imagine it's 2030. Four years from now.

The Personal AI CoE is not a niche concept anymore. It's how competent professionals organize their work with AI systems, the way filing systems organized work with paper and spreadsheets organized work with numbers. The six pillars have been adapted to dozens of domains — education, healthcare, legal, creative, scientific, agricultural. Each domain has its own conventions, its own best practices, its own community of practitioners sharing what works.

A pediatrician in Accra has a Personal AI CoE that helps her diagnose rare conditions by cross-referencing symptom patterns against a global database of case studies. Her AI strategy is focused. Her governance framework ensures that patient data stays private. Her technology stack costs $30 per month.

A furniture maker in Kyoto has a Personal AI CoE that handles customer communication, supply chain optimization, and design iteration. He speaks to his AI systems in Japanese. They understand the specific terminology of woodworking. His business has grown 40% since he implemented the framework. He still makes every piece by hand.

A sixteen-year-old in Sao Paulo has a Personal AI CoE that she built herself, following a tutorial translated into Portuguese by another teenager. She uses it to produce music, write stories, and learn computational biology. She has published three research papers as a co-author with her AI system. She is not exceptional. She is one of thousands of students in her city doing similar work.

A retired engineer in rural Montana has a Personal AI CoE that helps him manage a small ranch, optimize irrigation, track cattle health, and write the novel he's been thinking about for twenty years. He tells people he has "a staff of twelve" and laughs when they visit and see that he lives alone.

None of these people are AI researchers. None of them are software engineers. They are people with specific needs, specific knowledge, and access to intelligence infrastructure that meets them where they are.

This is not utopia. The problems of the world in 2030 are still substantial — climate, inequality, conflict, the ongoing negotiation between human agency and machine capability. The Personal AI CoE doesn't solve those problems. But it distributes the tools of problem-solving more broadly than they've ever been distributed before. And the history of human progress suggests that broad distribution of powerful tools — literacy, electricity, the internet, now intelligence — tends to produce outcomes that concentrated control does not.

VII. The Only Game That Matters

I started this book with Matt Shumer's observation that something big is happening. I've spent ten chapters trying to describe the shape of that something — the economic shift, the leverage change, the builder's opportunity, the intelligence layer, the ethical obligations, the practical mechanics.

But here, at the end, I want to make a simpler point.

The great transition is not a destination. There is no moment when it will be complete, no finish line to cross, no state of arrival. The people who are waiting for the dust to settle before they engage will wait forever. The dust doesn't settle. It's not that kind of transition.

What it is, instead, is an infinite game. A game that rewards the players who keep playing — who keep building, keep learning, keep adapting, keep shipping. Who optimize for the trajectory, not the snapshot. Who build practices, not just products. Who carry the ethical weight of their work and don't set it down because it's heavy.

The finite players will have their moments. They'll win funding rounds and hit milestones and land on magazine covers. Some of them will build things that matter. But the builders who will still be here in ten years, in twenty, in thirty — the ones whose work will compound into something that reshapes their domain — are the ones playing the infinite game.

They're the ones who understood, early, that the point was never to win.

The point was to keep building.
