A reader asked which AI tools to use for market research and I realized the answer is less interesting than the order. The mistake most retail investors make is reaching for the most powerful tool first. The right move is to use the cheapest tool to ask the dumbest question, then escalate only when the answer matters.

## The stack, in order

- **Perplexity Pro** — first pass for any question that touches news from the last 72 hours. Citations are honest and current. ~$20/mo.
- **Claude Opus 4.7** — second pass for analysis on documents you paste in. 10-K, earnings transcript, the actual primary source. Don't ask it about live prices.
- **TradingView with Pine Script** — third tool for testing any hypothesis you formed in the first two steps. Cheap if you already use TradingView.
- **A spreadsheet** — final step. If a thesis can't survive a column of plain numbers in front of you, it isn't a thesis.

## Why this order matters

Most retail investors burn the most expensive tool first. They ask Claude or GPT to analyze a stock — it hallucinates a price, a multiple, or a fact from 2023. The first job of the stack is to ground the question in current reality, which is what Perplexity does well and what foundation models do badly.

The 10-K analysis step is where the real edge sits. A foundation model reading a 200-page filing in two minutes and surfacing the three risks buried in footnote 14 — that's a 2026 capability that did not exist usefully 18 months ago.

> The edge is not in the model. It is in knowing which document to feed it.

## What I cut from my own stack

Stock screeners with AI-generated "buy/sell" calls. Newsletter aggregators that summarize earnings. Anything that produces a recommendation without showing you the reasoning trail you'd need to disagree with it.
