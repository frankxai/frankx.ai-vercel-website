## What I actually do for Oracle

At Oracle EMEA I helped Europe’s largest enterprises stand up AI Centers of Excellence. The scope, on paper, is six pillars: Strategy. Governance. Talent. Technology. Data. Ethics.

The work, in practice, is the same 90 questions every time, asked in a different order:

- *Strategy:* What's the AI bet that matters in the next 18 months, and what's the bet that's a distraction?
- *Governance:* Who decides what ships, and who's accountable when it goes wrong?
- *Talent:* Which roles get rewritten by agents, which roles get amplified, and which new roles haven't been named yet?
- *Technology:* What runs in our own cloud, what runs in the model provider's cloud, and what runs on the user's device?
- *Data:* What can we use, what must we exclude, and what's the audit trail when the regulator asks?
- *Ethics:* What does this system refuse to do, and how does it refuse cleanly?

A CoE engagement runs 6-18 months. Total cost — internal time, external consulting, software, change management — sits between €150k and €2M depending on company size. The output is a working framework + a small team trained to extend it + governance docs that survive audits.

## Then I got home and ran the same framework on myself

In late 2024 I realized something obvious in retrospect: the questions don't care about the budget.

If I'm using AI to ship music, write articles, run a workshop business, build products — the six pillars still apply. Strategy still asks what bet matters. Governance still asks who decides. Talent (in solo mode) asks which parts of me get rewritten and which get amplified.

I built ACOS — the Agentic Creator OS — as my own CoE. Same six pillars. Different scale.

The total cost wasn't €200k. It was the time to read a few papers, write down my own answers, and codify them as agents + commands + skills. The framework was identical. The implementation was 1/5000th the spend.

That's the Inner Circle thesis. Not "here are 10 prompts." Not "here are the tools." The thesis is: **the framework that costs €200k at enterprise scale costs zero when you're the only employee — but only if you know which questions it actually asks.**

## Walking one pillar end-to-end

Let me show one pillar in full so you can see what the rest of the framework feels like. I'll pick **Governance** because it's the one most solo operators skip and the one that creates the most regret later.

Enterprise version (what Oracle clients pay for):
- An AI Steering Committee — usually CIO + CTO + General Counsel + a business owner per major use case
- Use-case intake form: every AI initiative submits a 1-page brief before any work starts
- Risk classification matrix: low/medium/high based on data sensitivity + decision authority + reversibility
- Pre-deployment review gate: legal + ethics + ops sign off before any agent goes to production
- Quarterly model review: each shipped agent gets re-audited against the latest version of the framework

Solo version (what I run):
- The Steering Committee is me + one durable spec doc (one paragraph per pillar)
- The intake form is a paragraph in CLAUDE.md that says *"before any new agent: write its purpose, its data scope, and what it refuses to do"*
- The risk matrix is two questions: *"can this fail loudly?" and "can I roll it back in under 5 minutes?"* If both are no, it doesn't ship.
- The pre-deployment gate is `@integrity-guard` — the same 5-gate quality check Issues 1 and 2 referenced
- The quarterly review is `/palace` on Sunday plus `/chronicle` rolling up the month

Same questions. Same architecture. 1/5000th the spend.

The trap is that solo operators look at the enterprise version and conclude "that's not for me — I'm too small." It IS for them. It's exactly for them. The enterprise version is just the framework + the people + the politics. Strip the people and the politics and you're left with the framework, which costs nothing to apply.
