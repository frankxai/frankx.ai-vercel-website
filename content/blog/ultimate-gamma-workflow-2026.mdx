## What is the best Gamma setup for serious work?

The difference between a forgettable deck and an investor-ready one is set before you ever click "Generate." Configure these four things first.

| Step | What to set | Why it matters |

## How do you go from a prompt to a pitch deck?

The cold-start path, for when you have an idea but no written material yet.

1. **Write a structured prompt, not a topic.** Instead of "AI for dentists," write: "A seed-stage pitch deck for an AI scheduling tool for dental clinics. Cover problem, solution, market size, traction, business model, team, and the ask. Tone: confident, data-led." Gamma respects structure — give it the section logic.
2. **Generate at 10–12 cards.** Pitch decks don't need 20. Constrain it.
3. **Read it as a stranger would.** The first generation is a draft, not a deliverable. Note which cards are weak.
4. **Refine by chat** (next section). This is where the deck becomes yours.

The prompt-to-deck path is fast but generic on its own. It only becomes investor-ready after the refinement loop.

## How does the edit-by-chat refinement loop work?

This is the workflow most people skip, and it's the one that separates a usable deck from an AI artifact. Instead of manually editing card by card, you direct the Gamma Agent in plain language.

A real refinement sequence:

- "Card 4's market-size claim is vague. Rewrite it with a clear TAM/SAM/SOM breakdown and add a chart."
- "Make all card headlines shorter and more declarative."
- "The tone on cards 6–8 is too soft. Make it confident and specific."
- "Add a competitive-landscape card with a 2x2 positioning matrix."
- "Re-theme the whole deck to match the brand guide I just uploaded."

The Agent makes edits across all cards at once for consistency, which is the part that saves real time — you're not fixing the same font or tone problem fourteen times. Treat it like directing a junior designer: specific instructions, one change at a time, review after each.

The discipline that matters: don't accept the first generation as final, and don't over-prompt either. Two or three sharp refinement passes beat ten vague ones.

## How does the document-to-deck workflow compare?

If you already have the content — a strategy memo, a research doc, a transcript, a long email — this is the faster and better path. You're not asking Gamma to invent an argument; you're asking it to design one you already have.

1. **Paste or upload the source.** Gamma handles large inputs (roughly 100,000 tokens).
2. **Set textMode to `condense`** so it summarizes faithfully instead of rewriting from scratch.
3. **Let it structure the deck**, then use the same edit-by-chat loop to tighten.

Consultants and solution engineers should default to this path. Your decks should come from your real analysis, not from a model's guess about your topic. Doc-to-deck keeps your substance and adds the design — which is exactly the division of labor you want.
