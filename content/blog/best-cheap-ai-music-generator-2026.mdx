## Can you sell AI-generated music commercially?

Usually yes — **if you're on the right paid plan and the license actually transfers rights to you.** Three separate questions hide inside "can I sell this":

1. **Does the plan permit commercial use?** Most free tiers say no. Most paid tiers say yes.
2. **Who owns the copyright?** Some tools (AIVA Pro, Riffusion paid, Soundraw) give you ownership or a clean perpetual license. Others (Suno) assign you their rights in the output but explicitly do not guarantee copyright vests at all — because purely AI-generated work may not be copyrightable in the first place under current US Copyright Office guidance.
3. **Is the *training data* clean?** This is the question the lawsuits are about, and it's the one that can follow you downstream.

That third point is why 2026 is a strange year to buy. Let me cover it directly.

## How do the Suno and Udio lawsuits affect commercial use?

The major labels sued both Suno and Udio in 2024 over training on copyrighted recordings. As of June 2026 the picture is split, and it matters for anyone monetizing output:

- **Udio settled with Universal Music Group (October 2025)**, creating the first major-label licensing template for AI music. Reporting indicates a per-generation royalty structure and content-identification requirements for catalog similarity. Sony and Warner litigation against Udio continued past that deal.
- **Suno settled with Warner Music (late November 2025)** and signed a licensing deal, but is still fighting the broader case on fair-use grounds, with a summary-judgment hearing reported for July 2026. Sony remained the last major label actively litigating both companies into 2026. Suno also raised $400M at a reported $5.4B valuation in mid-2026 even as the suits continued.

What this means practically: the platforms are increasingly licensed at the *training* level, which reduces — but does not zero out — the risk that a future ruling forces changes to existing output. I am not a lawyer, and none of this is legal advice. If you're building a commercial catalog, read each tool's current terms yourself and watch the July 2026 docket. For a deeper head-to-head on these two specifically, see my [Suno vs Udio 2026 breakdown](/blog/suno-vs-udio-2026).

## Which AI music generator has the best free tier?

It depends on what "free" needs to do for you.

- **Most generous for pure experimentation:** Riffusion — unlimited generation, no login, no credit card. The catch is personal-use only.
- **Most full-songs-per-day:** Suno's free tier gives 50 credits/day (~10 songs) with vocals. But Suno has been tightening free-tier downloads — free users are moving toward streaming/sharing rather than file downloads, so treat it as a try-before-you-buy, not a production pipeline.
- **Best free tier that *almost* lets you ship:** Udio's free plan generates roughly three full songs/day but keeps them public and blocks high-quality download.
- **Avoid for free commercial work:** Mubert's free "Ambassador" tier explicitly prohibits commercial, branded, and ad use. AIVA's free plan keeps copyright with AIVA.

The honest read: no free tier in this category is a real commercial foundation. Free tiers are auditions. The moment you intend to monetize, you're choosing a paid plan — so choose on the license, not the sticker price.

## What is the cheapest plan that actually clears commercial use?

Ranked by monthly cost for a license you can build a business on:

1. **Suno Pro — $10/mo** ($8 annual). Commercial rights on everything made while subscribed. Cheapest real commercial entry for full vocal songs.
2. **Soundraw Creator — ~$11/mo**. Every track royalty-free and commercially cleared, trained only on in-house music — the lowest *legal-risk* option in the table, because there's no label training dispute hanging over it.
3. **AIVA Standard — €15/mo**. Limited commercial license (YouTube, Twitch, TikTok, Instagram). Note: full *copyright ownership* requires Pro at €49/mo.
4. **Mubert Creator — ~$14/mo**. Royalty-free for promoted social content without attribution.
5. **Udio Pro — $30/mo**. Full commercial rights, high audio fidelity — but 3x the price of Suno Pro for a comparable license.

If you want the cheapest path to *owning* what you make outright, Soundraw and AIVA Pro are clearer than Suno, whose terms assign rights without guaranteeing copyright vests.

## When should you pay for the more expensive tier?

Pay up when the license gap, not the credit count, is the bottleneck:

- **Pay for Udio Pro over Standard** the day you decide to monetize — Standard's non-commercial restriction makes its $10 price irrelevant for a business.
- **Pay for AIVA Pro over Standard** when you need genuine copyright ownership and WAV/stems for film, game, or sync work, rather than a limited monetization license.
- **Pay for Suno Premier ($30/mo, 10,000 credits + Studio)** only when 2,500 Pro credits stops covering your output and you need the Studio editing features. For most creators, Pro is the right tier for years.

A note on Suno's retroactivity: subscribing does **not** automatically grant commercial rights to songs you made on the free plan. If you want to monetize a track, make it *while* subscribed. This is the single most expensive mistake I see new creators make.

## FAQ

**Is there a completely free AI music generator I can sell music from?**
Not cleanly. Riffusion is free with no login, but its free output is personal-use only. The open-source Riffusion model (MIT license) can be run locally with far fewer restrictions if you're technical. For everyone else, monetizing means a paid plan — the cheapest being Suno Pro at $10/mo.

**Does Suno give me copyright to my songs?**
Suno's paid plans assign you Suno's rights in output you create while subscribed, which permits commercial use. But Suno explicitly does not guarantee that copyright vests in AI-generated output — because under current US Copyright Office guidance, purely AI-generated work may not be copyrightable. You can sell it; you may not be able to stop others from using it. Verify Suno's current terms before building a catalog.

**What's the real difference between Suno Pro and Udio for commercial use?**
Price and clarity. Suno Pro is $10/mo with commercial rights included. Udio's commercial rights require its $30/mo Pro plan — its $10 Standard plan is non-commercial. Udio is often praised for audio fidelity; Suno wins on cost-to-license.

**Can I monetize music from the free tiers of these tools?**
Almost never. Suno, Udio (free), AIVA (free), Riffusion (free demo), and Mubert (free Ambassador) all restrict free output to personal, non-commercial use. Mubert's free tier specifically blocks ads and branded content. Treat free tiers as auditions.

**Will the Suno and Udio lawsuits make my existing songs illegal to use?**
Unknown, and nobody can promise otherwise. As of June 2026, Udio settled with UMG and Suno settled with Warner, reducing training-level risk, while Sony litigation and a July 2026 Suno hearing were still pending. This isn't legal advice — read each vendor's current terms and watch the rulings if you're building a commercial catalog.

**Which tool has the lowest legal risk overall?**
Soundraw. It trains only on music its in-house producers write and record, so it sits outside the major-label training dispute entirely, and every plan ships a worldwide perpetual commercial license.
