## How do you set up Spaces so Perplexity knows your context?

Spaces are where casual users stay casual and serious users get leverage. A Space carries context between sessions so you stop re-explaining yourself.

Build one Space per recurring research lane — not per question. A market analyst might keep "Competitor Intelligence," "Sector Macro," and "Earnings Prep." Inside each:

1. **Write custom instructions.** State your role, the output format you want, the source types you trust, and the ones you reject. Example: "I'm an AI architect evaluating vendors. Prefer primary docs, pricing pages, and engineering blogs. Skip listicles and SEO content farms. Default to a comparison table plus a one-line recommendation."
2. **Add grounding sources.** Upload the PDFs, CSVs, and docs that define your context — a spec, a pricing sheet, last quarter's notes. Link the sites you trust. Now every search inside the Space reasons against that baseline.
3. **Keep the thread.** Run related questions inside the Space instead of starting fresh chats. The accumulated context makes each answer sharper than the last.

Spaces passed 5 million created by March 2026 because this is the feature that turns a chatbot into a project-aware assistant. Set up two or three and the rest of the workflow clicks into place.

## What is the discover→ground→reason workflow?

This is the chain that wins. Most people run one search and accept the first answer. The instrument-level move is three deliberate passes.

**Discover.** Open with Pro Search to map the terrain. You're not looking for the final answer yet — you're finding the real questions, the key players, the terms of art. Ask broad: "What are the current approaches to X and where do they disagree?" Read the citations, not just the summary.

**Ground.** Now go deep on the questions discovery surfaced. Run Deep Research on the specific decision: "Compare A, B, and C on cost, latency, and lock-in for a mid-size team, with sources." Deep Research returns a structured, cited report — and since the March 2026 update, it can generate the deliverable directly: a slide deck, a spreadsheet, a dashboard, or a one-page site from the same prompt.

**Reason.** This pass is yours. Perplexity gives you grounded material; the judgment is your job. Read the primary sources behind the claims that matter. Decide. The tool's value is that it gets you to the reasoning faster — it doesn't do the reasoning for you, and pretending otherwise is how people ship confident nonsense.

Run this chain inside a Space and each pass compounds on the last. That's the whole system.

## How do you keep citation discipline so you can trust the output?

Perplexity's edge over a raw chatbot is that every claim ships with a source. That edge is worthless if you don't use it.

The discipline is short:

- **Open the citations on any claim you'll act on.** The summary is a convenience, not the evidence. A linked source can still be a blog summarizing a misreading of a paper.
- **Check the source date.** "Latest" in a generated summary can quietly mean 2024. For anything pricing- or version-sensitive, confirm the date on the page.
- **Trace to primary when stakes are real.** If a number goes in a deck or a decision, follow the chain to the original doc, not the secondary source that quoted it.
- **Treat a missing citation as a missing fact.** If Perplexity asserts something with no source, that's a flag, not a finding.

This is the same standard you'd hold a junior analyst to. The tool makes it fast — it doesn't make it optional.

## How do you avoid the common Perplexity mistakes?

Five failure modes account for most of the disappointment people report.

- **One-shot searching.** Asking once and accepting the answer wastes the instrument. Run the chain.
- **Skipping Spaces.** Without grounding and custom instructions, you re-explain context every session and get generic answers.
- **Trusting the summary.** The citations are the product. Open them on anything you'll act on.
- **Wrong mode for the job.** Quick Search for a fact, Pro Search to map, Deep Research for a report. Using Deep Research for a one-line lookup wastes your daily quota; using Quick Search for a real decision wastes the decision.
- **Treating it as a Google replacement.** It's a reasoning-and-research tool. Judged as a search engine, it underdelivers. Judged as an instrument, it's in a different category.
