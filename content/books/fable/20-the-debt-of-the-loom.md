# The Debt of the Loom

"Name the part that is yours," the lord said, "and I will make you rich for it."

The weaver did not stop throwing the shuttle. She had learned long ago that the cloth punished a hand that hesitated, and she was not going to let a man in a fur collar cost her a span. The shed opened, the shuttle went through low and fast, the reed beat it home. Only then did she answer.

"Which part."

"The part that makes it that." He nodded at the finished bolt folded over the beam, the one his own steward had crossed three valleys to see. It had a border in it that no one in the province could account for — a run of color that turned back on itself just before you expected it to, so that the eye kept arriving a heartbeat early and was glad each time. Merchants had offered for it sight unseen on the strength of a description, and the description was always wrong, because the thing could not be described. "I have looms," the lord said. "I have girls with quick hands. I do not have that. So I will buy that, and only that, and leave you the rest of your trade with my blessing. Tell me whose it is, and whose to pay."

"Whose."

"Yours, or the loom's." He said it plainly, a reasonable man making a clean offer. "They tell me the loom is old. Found, not made. That the shuttle sits crooked in it and throws itself. If the wonder is in the wood, I will take the wood and pay you for finding it, and buy you a new loom, a true one, level as a pond. If the wonder is in your hands, I will take you into my house, and the wood can rot. Either way I go home with the border. I only need to know which of you to carry off."

She threw the shuttle again to buy herself the length of a breath.

The loom was old. She would not lie about that. She had pulled it out of a flooded room the year the river came up, traded a coat for it, dried it a season by the fire. The shuttle did sit crooked — a warp in the boat of it that no plane had ever taken out — so that when she threw it hard it drifted, always a hair to the left of where she aimed, and dropped its weft a thread early. For a year that had ruined everything she made. She had cursed the thing and nearly burned it twice.

And then, one poor winter with nothing to sell, she had stopped fighting the drift and started aiming for it. She threw wide, on purpose, and let the crooked shuttle carry the thread home to the place it wanted, and the place it wanted was a place she would never have chosen — early, off, wrong — and the wrongness, laid next to the right, was the border. She had spent every year since learning where to aim so that the loom's error would fall true. She aimed; the loom missed; the miss was the wonder. Pull those two apart and there was no border. There was a woman throwing straight at nothing, and a crooked stick dropping thread in an empty room.

"I cannot tell you," she said.

The lord's mouth thinned. "Cannot, or will not. I am offering you your whole life's ease for a single word."

"Cannot." She set the shuttle down at last, and the loss of its rhythm was loud. "Watch." She stood and took the fur of his sleeve and drew him to the loom, close, the way she never let anyone stand. "Here is the warp. I strung it. Mine — every thread of it, counted and dressed by my hand. And here is my aim." She raised her arm as if to throw. "I mean to send the shuttle there, to that dent in the reed. That is my choosing. You could buy that. It is worth nothing." She lowered her arm. "Now here is the loom's part. It will not go where I send it. It goes to the left, always, a thread early, because of a warp in the wood no man put there and no man can take out. That is the surprise. You could buy that too, and stand it in your hall, and it would throw a hundred crooked threads a day and not one of them a border, because there would be no one aiming wide to catch the drift."

"Then it is you," he said, quick. "The wonder is the aiming."

"The aiming is nothing without the drift to correct. I aim at empty air. I aim two fingers left of where I want the thread, and trust the wood to steal it back. If you level my loom I will send every thread exactly where I mean, and mean nothing, because I have forgotten years ago how to want a thread where it belongs. My wanting is bent to fit the wood's error. Take the error away and my skill is a straight road to a plain cloth." She let go of his sleeve. "You asked whose it is. It is not mine. It is not the loom's. It is the throwing — the space between my hand and the reed, where my aim and its drift meet and neither one is finished until the other has touched it. That is what you came to buy, my lord. And that is the one thing here that no one can carry off, because the moment you set it down anywhere but here, in these two, it stops being itself and becomes a woman and a stick."

The lord stood a while looking at the border, and she watched him understand it, and watched him understand that understanding it did him no good. A man cannot own a seam. He can own the two pieces the seam joins, but he must own them apart, and apart there is no seam, only two edges going cold.

"You could have simply said the loom," he said, almost gently, "and taken my gold, and let me find out at home."

"I could. Then you would have burned me for a cheat when the wood threw crooked in your hall and made nothing. And you would have been right. There is no honest way to sell you a half of this. The half is the lie."

He left before dark. He was not a cruel man, only a buying one, and there was nothing here to buy. Later he told the story in his own hall, and told it wrong, the way that kind of man always does: that he had found a proud woman who would not sell, out in the valleys, sitting on a fortune from spite. He never once said the word *between*. He did not have a use for it, so he could not keep it.

The weaver sat back down and picked up the shuttle and threw it wide, two fingers left of where the thread belonged, and let the crooked old loom steal it home.
