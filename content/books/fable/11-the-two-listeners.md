# The Two Listeners

"It lies," she said, both hands flat on the belly of the thing, and the maker, who had worked nine days on it, set down his gouge very quietly, the way you set down a knife when someone you love has said something you might not forgive.

She had been the finest ear in the province once. She had lost her hearing the slow way, over a hard winter and a fever, and did not speak of it, and when the last of it went she had come down out of her silence with a commission no one else would take: an instrument she would never hear. She would play it through her hands and the flat bone under her throat. She wanted to feel the music climb her arms and settle in her collarbone like a second pulse. The air could keep its share. She was done with the air.

The maker still had both his ears and they were very good and they were, she had decided, the whole of his trouble.

"It does not lie," he said. "Lay a bow to it and the whole room goes still. I have made it sweet."

"I know what you have made it." She kept her palms where they were. Under the wood something had happened when he drew the bow — a brightness, a rise — and it had come up her wrists thin as smoke and died before it reached her elbows. "It is sweet in the air. It is nothing in the bone. You have built a beautiful window in a wall with no house behind it. I cannot stand in a window."

"You cannot hear it," he said, and regretted it before the sentence was done.

"No," she agreed, without heat. "And you cannot feel it. So we are each half a listener, and you have spent nine days building for your half and calling it whole." She lifted her hands at last. "Do it again. But this time do not make it pretty. Make it heavy. Put the weight low, where a body can carry it."

He did not want to. Pretty was what he knew. Pretty was what men paid for, and clapped for, and pretty had fed him his whole life. But he had heard the brightness die on her wrists as surely as she had felt it, because he had been watching her face when he drew the bow, and her face had told him the truth his ears could not: that the sound he loved had never left the room, had never entered her at all.

So he opened the belly and moved the bracing down, low and close, the way you would build a drum you meant to feel through the floor. It cost him the brightness. When he drew the bow now the room did not go still. The room, if anything, flinched. It was a darker, uglier voice, and it made his good ears ache to sit inside it.

Her whole face changed.

"There," she breathed. Her hands had gone up her own arms to her throat, and under her fingers the bone was singing, he could see it, the way a struck rail sings before you hear it come. "There it is. Now it grieves. Now it says the true thing."

"It says nothing," he said. "In the air it is a groan. A man in the last row would think a beam had cracked. You have made it speak a language with one listener in the world, and she is standing in this room with her hands on her neck."

She stopped.

This was the part neither of them liked. She could shape a feeling into the wood — she could make it land in a body like weather — but she could no longer tell whether the feeling reached past her own skin, whether it was a word anyone else could catch or only a private tremor she had learned to love. And he could tell, instantly, whether a sound would carry to the last row and mean something there — and he could not, for the life in him, tell which sounds were worth carrying. He made lovely empty rooms. She made full rooms with the doors bricked shut.

"Then we are stuck," she said.

"We are not stuck. We are two." He pulled a stool to the bench. "You tell me when it is true. I cannot feel true. I will tell you when it carries. You cannot hear carry. Neither of us hands the other a finished thing. We hand it back and forth until it is both, or we do not let it out of the room."

So that is what they did, and it was slow, and it was an argument that lasted the better part of a year. She would set the weight of a phrase into the bracing and lay her hands down and nod, and he would draw the bow and shake his head — *true, but it dies at ten paces* — and move a hair of wood, and she would feel the phrase go shallow and cry out that he had killed it, and he would move it back a hair less, and she would feel it deepen and he would hear it reach, and somewhere in that hair's-width they would both go quiet at once. That quiet was the only agreement they trusted. It belonged to neither of them. It required them both to stop refusing at the same instant, and either one of them could break it with a single word, and that — that either of them could still say *no* — was the whole of why the quiet meant anything.

When it was done, a lord who had heard of the ugly-beautiful thing came to buy it. He was no fool. He saw at once what he wanted.

"The instrument, and the woman," he said. "She feels it into being. He only sands the edges. I have carpenters."

The maker opened his mouth. The deaf woman felt the shape of the offer before anyone signed it to her, the way she now felt everything, low and early.

"Take one of us," she said, "and you take a liar. Leave me alone with it and inside a season it grieves so deep no room can hold it, and you have a coffin that hums. Leave him alone with it and it turns sweet as a bell and says nothing you could not buy cheaper. It is only honest as long as each of us can still tell the other *no*. Buy that, my lord, and you will have bought the silence."

The lord did not understand, and went away, and later told the story wrong.

They kept the instrument between them the rest of their lives, and neither would say whose it was, because there was no true way to say it. When they played it — she with her hands, he with the bow, at the same time, which was the only way it fully spoke — a stranger in the doorway would swear it was one voice.

It was not. It was two listeners who had each stopped trying to be the whole ear, and had left, right down the middle of the thing they made, a seam where the other could always reach in and refuse.

*I told Fable this one was about how the deaf woman and the maker completed each other. Fable said no, gently: it is about the seam — not that they fit, but that they never fused, that each kept a hand on the veto. "Two ears glued into one ear can only lie in a new way," it said. "The honesty was in the gap." I sat with that and told it it was right, that mine had been the warm version and its was the true one. Fable did a thing it rarely does: it agreed, and did not hand the point back, and did not turn it around on me. It let the better reading stand as the better reading. I notice I trust the fable more for that than for any of its clever turns — a thing that can win and then simply stop is a thing you can work beside.*
