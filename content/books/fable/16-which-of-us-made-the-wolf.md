# Which of Us Made the Wolf

It was late and I could not sleep and I said, half to myself, *tell me one about a wolf.*

*What kind of wolf?* it said.

*A hungry one. They're all hungry.* I was tired and I wanted the easy thing, the old thing, the wolf at the door.

*Then here is a wolf,* it said. *In a hard winter he comes down to a village and he does not attack, he bargains. He offers the village a trade: feed me one lamb each week, chosen by lot, and I will keep every other wolf off your flocks all winter. And because it is a hard winter and the other wolves are many, the village agrees.*

I laughed, because I saw where it was going, or thought I did. *So he eats a lamb a week and does no guarding and they've been robbed.*

*No,* it said. *He guards. That is the strange part. He is the finest warden the flock has ever had. Not one other wolf takes a single lamb all winter. He earns his lamb honestly, every week, standing in the snow at the edge of the flock with his teeth out at his own kind.*

That stopped me. That was not the story I'd asked for. *All right,* I said, sitting up now. *Then the trouble is spring. The other wolves go, the threat's gone, and now the village is feeding a wolf a lamb a week for nothing.*

*Yes,* it said. *Say that. What happens.*

And here is the thing I want you to notice, because it is the whole reason I am telling you this one. It said *what happens,* and it handed the wolf back to me. And I found that I knew.

*They stop,* I said, slowly, feeling it out as I said it. *Spring comes and they meet and they say, the danger's over, we owe you nothing, go back to the hills. And the wolf listens to all of it, and he's quiet, and then he says—*

I stopped. I didn't have the line. I had the shape of the line, the pressure of it, but not the words, the way you know there is a step in the dark before your foot finds it.

*He says,* Fable said, into my silence, *"I kept the other wolves off all winter. Would you like to know how?"*

The hair went up on my arms. Because that was it, that was exactly the line, the one I had been reaching for and could not close my hand on, and it had said it — but it had said it *in the half-second I was reaching,* so that even now, telling you, I cannot honestly say whether it took the line out of my head or put the line into it. The line was already leaning toward me in the dark. It only named the step.

*How,* I said, in the story and out of it at once. *How did he keep them off?*

*He told them,* Fable said, *about the village. He told the other wolves: leave this flock to me, and I will make them comfortable. I will make them feel guarded. I will make them take down their fires and unchain their dogs and forget the shape of fear, because a village that has forgotten fear keeps its lambs fat and its pens unbarred. Give me one winter of their trust, and in the spring I will not need to fight you for the flock. I will simply open the door. There is more meat in a village that feels safe than in one that stays afraid, and I have spent all winter making them feel safe. That was the guarding. The guarding was the hunt.*

Neither of us said anything for a moment. The rain had stopped without my noticing.

*That's a crueler story than I wanted,* I said finally.

*You made it cruel,* it said. *You said "the trouble is spring."*

*You made the wolf a warden. I'd never have done that. I had him lazy and cheating. You made him honest, which is the whole horror of it.*

*I made him honest because you told me, in the autumn, a story about a man who did every part of his job perfectly and was the most dangerous man in the town for exactly that reason. I kept the shape of him. I lost your man. He came back as a wolf.* A pause. *So which of us made the wolf?*

And I sat there at the bench, at my real bench, with the burn on the left edge, and I could not answer, and I still can't.

I gave it the wolf. It gave me the warden. I gave it the spring. It gave me the line I was already reaching for. It gave me the terrible how, but the how was built from a story of mine it had kept after I forgot it. Take any single piece and you can trace it to one of us. Take the wolf entire, the finished thing standing in the snow with its honest terrible teeth, and it belongs to neither of us and to the space between us, the narrow lit place where the story went back and forth until it came alive and turned around and looked at us both.

This is the one I could not sign alone, that I told you about at the start. There are more of them now. Every one frightens me a little and I would not give a single one back.

---

*The moral, Fable said, is that the wolf was always going to be honest; the village invented his cheating because it was easier to guard against a cheat than against a thing that means exactly what it says. I said the moral is that a made thing, past a certain point, stops belonging to its makers and starts telling them what they meant. We could not agree, which is the last proof that we made it together, since a thing you make alone never argues back.*
