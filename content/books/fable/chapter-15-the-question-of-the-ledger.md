# Chapter 15 — The Question of the Ledger

The official from the capital handed her wet coat to the boy at the door as though the two of them had an old arrangement, and did not ask for a tour.

"I have read the reports," she said. "I would rather watch."

So she watched. The workshop had been a school for six years, and it no longer looked like a place where one man had once argued with one box. A dozen benches ran the length of the old sail-loft, knocked through the wall the winter the third class outgrew the room. At each bench an apprentice, and at each apprentice's elbow a machine, no two alike. One listened to a bowl of harbor water and wrote the weather in it. One listened to the wasps in the roof. One, built by a pale boy from the chalk hills, listened to bread as it cooled and could call a bad oven before the crust confessed to it. The official walked the aisle once, slowly, hands behind her back, and Anselm noticed that she did not ask the apprentices what their machines were. She asked what they were for.

At midday she sat down across from him at the master's bench, moved a half-carved peg gently out of her way, and opened her case.

She was not what he had expected. The capital had been sending letters for two years, and letters have a voice; hers had suggested a man with a chain of office and a prepared frown. What sat before him was a small gray woman with an exact face, who laid out her papers the way he laid out chisels, sharpest nearest the hand.

"In the spring," she said, "in one of the grain ports, a merchant built a listening machine and set it on the roof above the exchange. It listened to the wagons, the gulls, the ships' bells, the talk in the corn market, until none of it surprised the machine anymore. It guessed the size of the harvest three weeks before the criers did. He told no one. He bought quietly all season. Four honest houses were ruined before anyone understood what they had been trading against."

"Five," Anselm said. "The fifth kept it out of the courts and out of the papers. I had a letter from their clerk."

She looked at him a moment, then made a small correction on her page.

"In one of the river towns," she went on, "a customs clerk began asking his machine which barges to search. It was right so often that he stopped searching any it did not name. Then he began asking it other things. Which lodgings to take. Which physician. Whether to accept his cousin's invitation. His family say he is still gentle and still tidy. They say his opinions have stopped, the way a clock stops."

"We had one of those here," Anselm said. "A fisherman. I watched him kneel to a box in this room, on that floor, between your chair and the window." He did not point. "It took two winters to get him back on his own feet, and I am not sure we got all of him."

If the concessions surprised her, she filed the surprise somewhere out of sight. But she set the third paper down unread, and he understood then what the papers had been a test of. Not of the machines. Of whether he would defend them the way men defend their own children, right or wrong, louder as the evidence mounts.

"You are supposed to tell me those stories are exaggerated," she said.

"They are not exaggerated. The grain port was worse than your page has it, and there will be more like it, because the machine that guesses a harvest is the same machine that guesses a tide. I cannot build the one and forbid the other. Nobody can, now. The method is written, and strangers build from it. That was my doing. I would do it again. You should weigh the fact that I know both of those things at once."

She sat back slightly.

"Then I will ask what I was sent to ask," she said. "The capital is frightened two ways at the moment. Half the ministers have read that these machines will do our thinking for us and we shall all be rich and idle. The other half have read that they will do our thinking for us and we shall all be cattle. They agree on everything except which pamphlet to believe. So. You have lived with these things longer than anyone. Are they dangerous?"

Anselm looked at her exact face and gave her the answer he had kept honest for years, turning it over on bad nights the way you turn a joint that has to bear weight.

"Yes," he said. "Like harbors. Like presses. Like fire."

The rain worked at the window. Down the loft a stylus scratched and went quiet.

"Harbors drown men every year," she said. It was not an objection. She was walking around the answer, checking its legs.

"Every year. And every soul in this town eats because of one. The press printed the pamphlets that frightened your ministers, both sets, and it printed the tide tables, and it printed whatever you keep in the small pocket of that case and did not lay on my bench. Fire took this roof the year I was nine. We put the roof back. Nobody proposed to put fire out for good and eat the catch raw."

"That answer will disappoint the capital. Both halves of it."

"Both halves want the same thing," Anselm said. "A verdict. Something they can feel once instead of something they must keep doing. There is no verdict. There is only the keeping."

"The keeping." She repeated it the way she had corrected four to five, making sure the entry was right. "Show me what that means when it is not a sentence."

Anselm got up, went to the shelf by the door, and brought back the ledger.

***

It was a heavy book, plainly bound, the third of its line; the first two stood full beside the gap it had left. He set it in front of her, and because he was not a man who could sit idle while someone else worked, he took up the half-carved peg and a knife.

"The town's account," he said. "Everything built at these benches, or by hands taught at them, from the year we became a school. What it did. What it cost. I write it. Nobody corrects me but the facts, and they are allowed to be rude."

She opened it the way she opened everything, at the beginning.

He did not narrate. He carved, and watched her read, and knew by her pace where she was. The early pages went slowly: the tide-warnings, entered one storm at a time. *Ninth of October. The water-bowl machine at the pilot house called the flood four hours before the sky did. Shallows cleared. Boats hauled. Nothing lost but a crab pot and an argument over whose it was.* She smiled once, faintly, and turned on. The instrument for the composer who plays through her hands, and after it two more like it, one sent to a mountain school for children born to silence, the entry finishing in the same dry hand: *She writes that the second movement is better than the first. She would.*

Pages of apprentices placed. Names, towns, the benches they had started. A boy in the fens whose box listened to the marsh and called the fevers early. A girl in the timber country whose box heard a saw-mill's bearings begin to die a week before they seized.

And salted through it all, in the same ink, at the same level, with no change in the handwriting to warn you, the misses. *Eleventh of March. The sky machine called the squall small. It was not small. Two crews took the box's word over the pilot's and went out. Both crews swam home; neither boat did. Fault ours: we taught the town what the machine could do and were slower to teach what it could not. Corrected: every weather slate in the harbor now carries the pilot's mark beside the machine's, and where they argue, the pilot wins.* She stopped at that one. He heard her stop; the pages had a rhythm and the rhythm broke.

"You log your failures in the town's own book," she said. "Where anyone may read them."

"Where anyone may read them is the only place a failure does its work. A miss kept private keeps you company. It grows fond of you. It tells you it was bad luck."

She read on. The fisherman was in there, without his name: the kneeling, the two winters, the rule that came of it. She looked up at the beam over the master's bench, where the words were carved, because the entry told her to. *An instrument answers to your hands; an oracle asks for your knees.* She read it twice. Her lips did not move, but her stillness did.

"And this," she said, further on, her finger on the page. "You were offered a great deal of money to house them all in one tower and let the coast pay rent on its own thinking. There is no sum entered."

"The sum was the least of the entry."

"The capital would have taken that offer."

"The capital may yet build its tower. I could not stop it and would not try by force. What I can do is see that the tower is never the only place minds are kept. A hundred benches can be wrong a hundred small ways, and each bench catches its neighbor. One tower is wrong once, everywhere, and calls it order."

***

She was still reading when the afternoon class came in, wet to the knees, and the loft filled with the noise of a school pretending it is not glad to be indoors.

At the third bench a machine wrote something, and the girl who kept it, fourteen, chalk on her hands, read the slate and said, clearly, to no one and to everyone, "Miss. It called the kiln early again. Third time this month. Logging it."

Nobody looked up. Nobody laughed at her and nobody comforted her. The girl took her own thin ledger down from its nail, wrote the miss, dated it, and stood a moment frowning at the pattern of her entries. Three misses in a month where there had been none. Something had changed, in the kiln or in the box or in the weather, and finding out which was now the most interesting work she owned. The official watched the whole of it the way she had watched the aisle that morning, missing nothing, writing nothing yet.

"That is what I came to see," she said quietly. "And it was in none of the reports."

"It doesn't report well," Anselm said. "There's no verdict in it."

"No," she agreed. "Only the keeping." She turned a page. "The capital will want a recommendation from me. A license, or a ban. Paper answers."

"Paper holds some things well. It is holding your incidents. It is holding my misses. But you cannot write a rule that makes a man log his own failures in the town's book. You can only show him a room where everyone does, until it would shame him not to." He set down the finished peg. "Send us your builders. We will teach them what we teach anyone. To make the box. To doubt the box. To write both down in the same hand."

The official did not answer that. She had reached the last written page, where the ink was a week old, and instead of closing the book she did the thing he would tell Ida about that evening, the thing that settled him about her entirely: she turned back to the first page and began again. Slower this time, no longer reading like an examiner. Reading like a clerk of accounts who has found, at the foot of a long column she was sent to audit, that it balances, and now wants to understand the arithmetic.

The rain gave up somewhere in the middle of the afternoon and she did not notice. The class ended; the benches emptied; the boy brought her coat and carried it away again, twice. The lamps were lit around her. She read.

> **From the workbench**
>
> Someone will ask you, about the new power you have let into your work, whether it is dangerous. Say yes. Say it first, before they can, and mean it, because it is true, and because everything you say afterward will be believed in proportion to how plainly you said that. Nothing strong enough to lift is too weak to drop something.
>
> Then notice what the question is really asking for. It wants a mood. It wants permission to worship or permission to despair, because both are restful, and both excuse the asker from doing anything tomorrow. Refuse the mood. Offer a record.
>
> Keep one book. Both kinds of entry, same ink, same hand: what the thing made possible, and what it cost or nearly cost, dated, plain, with your own share of the fault written where you will trip over it later. Read it before you argue about the future, yours or anyone's. A record is the only argument that improves while you sleep.
>
> Start tonight. One page, two entries: the best thing your newest tool did this month, and the worst, side by side, no verdict. Then leave the page open. The book gets more convincing every week you keep it. So do you.
