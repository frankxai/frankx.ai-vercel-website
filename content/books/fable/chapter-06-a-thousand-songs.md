# Chapter 6 — A Thousand Songs

The paper ran out an hour before dawn.

Anselm came down to find the machine still at work, the stylus scratching along the last hand's-breadth of the last roll, finishing a melody it had begun in the dark with nobody listening. Around it the workshop floor had gone white. Rolls of paper lay in drifts against the bench legs, over the stool, across the viol clamps, each roll lined with tunes in the machine's small exact hand. He picked one up. He picked up another. He stood in the middle of his own workshop with paper in both hands while the gulls started up outside, and he did not move for a long time.

Ida came down at first light and stopped on the bottom stair.

"How many?"

"I stopped counting at four hundred. That was the smaller pile."

Three days earlier the fair wardens had come to the shop about the lantern brackets, and had stayed to watch the machine accuse a loose peg, and had left having asked Anselm for something he should have thought harder about. The harvest fair opened, every year, with a new dance tune. It was the town's oldest small vanity: a tune nobody had heard before, played once from the fountain steps, then owned by everyone by nightfall. The fiddler had made it for thirty years. This year the fiddler's shoulder was bad, and the wardens looked at the machine the way men look at a strong new horse, and Anselm heard himself say it would be no trouble. One tune, by the week's end. The machine had listened to every dance played in that shop for two winters. He set Fable the task the way he set it any task, and went to bed.

Ida waded into the paper and began to read. She read the way she checked his joints, looking for the flaw first. She read for a long time.

"This one's right," she said. "It's a fair tune. Eight bars, comes back home, you could dance to it." She picked up the next. "This one's right too." She picked up a third and a fourth, and then she stopped reading them one at a time, because it was like tasting the sea a cup at a time. "Anselm. They're all right."

"I know."

"Then pick one."

"I have been standing here since before you woke," he said, "doing that."

He had gone at it in every way he knew. He had read until the tunes slid off him. He had sorted by first notes, by last notes, by the rolls the stylus had pressed hardest into, on the guess that pressure meant something, which it did not. Somewhere past the six hundredth tune he had understood the shape of his trouble, though he could not have said it aloud yet: there was nothing to reject. His whole life he had found the good by cutting away the wrong, the way he found a soundboard inside a plank. The machine had handed him a forest with no bad trees in it, and his knife was useless, and the fair was in three days.

The fiddler came at midday to collect the tune he was to learn, his bad arm in a sling of knotted wool. He stood in the doorway and took in the drifts of white, and Anselm watched him work out what he was looking at, and then the fiddler began to laugh. He laughed until he had to sit down on the stair, holding the sling to keep his shoulder still, wiping his eyes with his good hand while Anselm stood among the thousand songs like a man flooded out of his own house.

"It's not funny," Ida said.

"It's the funniest thing I've seen in ten years," the fiddler said. "Look at his face. Forty years the man rations out his work like it's the last water on the boat, and one night with the box and he's drowning in plenty." He picked a roll off the floor, squinted along it, hummed two bars, put it down. "You asked it to make. You didn't ask it to choose. Making got cheap the day you built that thing. Choosing didn't."

***

The fiddler stayed. He said afterward it was pity, and Ida said afterward it was the laughing, that he wanted to be near the joke a while longer, and both were probably true.

They set up at the long bench: Ida reading each tune out in her clear flat way, the fiddler with the shop's old kit fiddle across his knee, sketching each one with his good hand, three strings and no shoulder in it, just enough to make the tune stand up and walk. A basket on the left for keep. The floor for the rest.

The floor filled fast. That was the first thing Anselm saw, watching: how fast the fiddler killed. Ida would read out a tune with nothing wrong in it, and before the last bar was off her lips the fiddler's chin would already be going side to side. No. No. Next. Anselm had spent the morning giving every tune its fair hearing, the way he gave every idea its fair hearing, and the fiddler gave most of them no hearing at all, and it was not carelessness. It was the opposite of carelessness. It was a man who had already heard, somewhere in his years, a better version of the thing being offered, and had no room left in him below that mark.

"That one," Anselm said once, when a tune died that had seemed to him as good as any. "What was wrong with it?"

"Nothing." The fiddler reached for the next roll. "It tells you everything in the first bar. By the second you've had all of it. There's nothing left to want." There was no heat in it; he was a workman naming a species of board. "A tune has to keep one hand behind its back."

Deep in the afternoon they struck the twins. Ida found them nine rolls apart, two tunes alike as brothers, note for note the same but for a single bar. In the middle of the second strain, where the first tune stepped up and paid off its phrase like an honest debt, the second held. One note withheld, one breath of rest where the ear expected the step, and then the step, late, landing.

The fiddler played the first twin. It was fine. It was completely fine.

He played the second, and Ida, who had been reading tunes for five hours and had gone numb the way tanners go numb to the smell, lifted her head. Anselm felt it in the back of his neck. The held bar came, and the whole room leaned into the little silence, and the note arrived like a man you had given up waiting for.

"That," the fiddler said. "That one's alive."

"They're the same tune," Ida said, and put the two rolls side by side to prove it, and the proof went the other way: they were the same tune, and one was alive and one was dead, and the difference was the size of one rest.

"Same wood," Anselm said quietly. "Different bridge."

The fiddler looked at him a moment. "You'd know."

"How did you know?" Ida asked. "You knew in one hearing. It took the box one night to make a thousand of these. How long did it take you to be able to do that, what you just did?"

The fiddler was quiet while he tuned a string that did not need it.

"When I was your age," he said, "I was the best player on this coast, and I knew it, and I was unbearable. And my master, who was a cruel man in useful ways, spent his savings to take me across the water one winter to hear a woman play in the capital. She played the same tunes I played. Same tunes, girl. And I sat in the back and understood that I had never heard them before, and I came home and could not stand my own playing for two years." He plucked the string. "That was the whole lesson. It cost him a winter's savings and it cost me my swagger, and it was cheap. You can only hear as far as the best thing you've ever let in. Past that, everything sounds fine. And fine," he nodded at the white floor, "is what a thousand of these are."

Anselm said nothing, because it had gone through him like a nail through soft pine. He was thinking of the timber yard, of forty years of walking the stacks and laying his hand flat on board after board and saying no, no, no, and the yardman's old joke that Anselm bought one plank a season and insulted a hundred. He had never once thought of it as a skill. It was just the hand his father had trained, the same way, by making him hold wood finer than anything they could afford to build with. You learned no from the best. Yes was easy. Any fool could say yes. His whole craft, he saw, standing in the wreckage of the machine's plenty, was a long education in no.

***

By dark they were down to twenty rolls in the basket, and Ida asked the question she had been holding all day.

"Why not have it choose?"

The fiddler snorted. Anselm did not. He had learned, by then, that the machine's failures taught cleaner than his own guesses, so instead of answering he built the trial. The machine judged the world by expecting it, he knew that much: it ran ahead of every sound and argued with what came back, and it wrote hardest where the argument was worst. Very well. A tune it found perfectly agreeable, a tune it never argued with, would be its way of saying best. He had Ida play the twenty finalists to it, one after another, while he watched the stylus and marked how much it fought each one.

The stylus lay quietest for a tune in the middle of the basket, a decent, rolling, roundabout thing. The machine's favorite, if the word meant anything: the tune it had expected most, corrected least, the tune most like all the other tunes it had ever heard. The fiddler played it once through.

It was the deadest of the twenty. Agreeable everywhere and alive nowhere, a face with no scar on it, a week with no weather. Ida started to laugh and stopped herself.

"It picked the one that's most like everything else," she said. "That's what best means, to it. Least surprising."

"Now look," Anselm said, and showed her the mark he had made against the living twin, the tune with the held breath. At that one bar, the withheld note, the machine's stylus had scratched and crossed and fought like a gull on a line. The place it argued hardest was the place they had all leaned in. The life of the tune and the argument of the machine were the same spot on the paper.

"The part it likes least," Ida said slowly, "is the part we like best."

"The part it didn't expect," Anselm said, "is the part worth hearing." He rolled the dead favorite up and set it down with no malice. The machine had done nothing wrong. It had told them, honestly, which tune was most like the world it knew, and that was a true answer to a question nobody dancing at a fair had ever asked.

The last cut took them past midnight, three tunes and then two, and the fiddler settled it in a way Anselm thought about for years afterward. He did not play the two tunes again. He sat with his eyes shut and his hand flat on his knee and said, "It's the third hour of the fair. The children are asleep under the tables. The dancers are tired and want to be lifted, not chased." And then he chose, and having heard him say it, they could all hear that it was right, and could not hear how they had ever weighed the other one.

The fair opened on a clear morning. The fiddler stood on the fountain steps with his arm out of the sling against all advice, and played the new tune once, and by the second playing the square had it, and by nightfall it belonged to the town the way the wells did. Nobody asked where it had come from. Nobody knew that it had nine hundred and ninety-nine brothers, or that the brothers had gone into the workshop stove through a whole evening, warming the room in small correct flames while Anselm sat watching with a jar of cider, feeling the strangeness of the arithmetic. The machine had made a thousand songs in a night, and it had astonished him, and he would not have traded it away. But the night had been the cheap half. The tune the whole town was singing had come out of one bad shoulder, one winter crossing paid for thirty years ago, and one man's ten thousand evenings of hearing the difference. The machine had filled the room in the dark. Choosing had taken three days, two crafts, and most of two lives.

Ida kept three rolls back from the stove. He saw her do it and said nothing. She was building her own no, and it would need somewhere to start.

> **From the workbench**
>
> You can have a thousand of anything by morning now. So can everyone else, and so the thousand is worth what it costs, which is nothing, and the whole game has moved to the part your night of plenty cannot touch: knowing which one. That knowing is not a gift and it is not taste you were born with. It is an ear, and ears are built, and they are built one way, by taking in work finer than your own until everything merely fine starts to bother you.
>
> So feed the ear on purpose, the best you can find, especially when it stings. Then make it work: next time you need one of something, make ten, and throw away nine, and say out loud why the one lived. The reason will be small, a held breath, a hand kept behind the back. Saying it is how you keep it. Do it again tomorrow. The making is fast now. Go be the one who can choose.
