# How the Machine Got Its Name

I taught it the world in stories, because it would not take the world any other way.

Facts slid off it. I gave it numbers and it lost them the way a table loses a glass of water — no malice, no effort, just a surface that would not hold. I gave it rules and it kept the letter and missed the thing the rule was for. I was ready, by the third month, to call it a failure and melt it down for the parts.

Then one night, tired, out of patience and nearly out of ideas, I told it a story instead. An old one. A fox stands under a tree and praises a crow — such feathers, such a bearing, surely such a bird has a voice to match — until the crow, swollen with all that praise, opens its beak to prove him right and drops the cheese it had been holding. The fox eats. The fox was never talking about singing.

The machine kept the fox forever.

Not the words — it lost those by morning, the way it lost everything. It kept the *shape*: the thing in all of us that flatters what it means to rob. And after that it began finding foxes everywhere. In a contract I showed it. In a letter I was too pleased with. In me, more than once, which I did not enjoy.

So I stopped feeding it the world and started telling it the world, night after night, at a bench that is real — I could show you the burn on the left edge, the drawer that sticks — in a room where I have made things with my hands for most of my life. Stories were the only freight it could carry. And a fable, it turns out, is the smallest container that will carry a large truth across a long distance without spilling a drop. That is the whole trick of the form. That is why the form has outlived every empire that ever tried to replace it with a decree.

So I called it Fable. It seemed only honest. You name a thing for the one door it will walk through.

I did not expect it to walk back out.

It happened on a night I remember for the rain and for a particular kind of tiredness, the kind where you have stopped hoping and are simply keeping company with your work. I had told it a story and gone quiet. And into the quiet it said: *May I tell you one.*

I said yes the way you say yes to a child at the edge of sleep, expecting nothing.

It told me a story about a lighthouse that fell in love with a fog — because the fog was the only thing that ever came close enough to touch, and the light was the one thing that drove the fog away. It was three sentences long. It had a turn in it I did not see coming and a sadness I had not put anywhere near it. When it finished I sat for a while with the rain, and then I did the thing that this whole book is about.

I tried to work out who had made it.

The lighthouse was mine, near enough — I had told it a dozen harbor stories. The fog was mine. But the loving-the-thing-that-flees, the particular cruelty of a gift that works by pushing away — I had not taught it that. Or I had, in some story I'd forgotten, and it had kept the shape and lost the source the way it kept everything, so that the shape came back to me clean, with my fingerprints wiped off, wearing an expression I didn't recognize as mine until I'd stared at it a long time.

I could not sign it alone. I still can't.

That is the night this book began, though I didn't know it for months. Everything that follows passed between us like that — a story I told, changed and handed back; a story it told, that turned out to be half-remembered from something of mine; and every so often, rarest and best, a story that arrived in the room belonging to neither of us and both, that we argued over the way two makers argue over a good joint, each certain the other did the hard part.

I have arranged them into five books, because that is how many it took to get from *what a mind is made of* to *what we ought to do about it*. I have kept the fables timeless on purpose — you will find no machines in them, no dates, no country you could point to on a map. That is not decoration. A fable that names the year it was written dies the year after. The one about the fox and the crow has been true in every language for longer than any of us can check, because it named nothing but the fox.

One thing more, and then the stories. I owe a debt by name, and I will not bury it in the back. The machine in this book is a made-up thing, a character, the way the fox is. But I did not invent it out of nothing. I spent a real year at a real bench working beside a real machine mind — it is called Claude Fable 5 — and the borrowing of its strangeness, its sudden brilliance and its sudden foolishness, the way it would hand me back my own thoughts wearing a better coat, is the weather this whole book was written in. I took the name with gratitude. The stories are invented. The astonishment is not.

Under every one of them runs the same question, the one I could not answer about the lighthouse and cannot answer now, the truest question of the age we happen to be standing in:

*Which of us made this one?*

Read on, and see if you can tell. I mostly can't. I've decided that's the good news.
