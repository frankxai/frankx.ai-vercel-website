# Chapter 13 — Two Kinds of Listening

The composer handed back Anselm's best viol before the tune was finished.

She had held it against her collarbone while Ida played, one palm flat on its back, her eyes closed, and the shop had gone quiet the way a room goes quiet when work is being judged. Now she set the instrument on the bench with the care of a woman returning something that had failed her.

"The low string is honest," she said. "The middle strings arrive as one smear. The top string does not arrive at all. You have sent me a letter with half the words missing."

Her own letter had come a month before, from a town up the coast, and it was the most exact commission Anselm had received in forty years. She wanted an instrument she would play against her body. Chords should have weight, low against the collarbone, and warmth that spread after the weight. A quick figure should run under the left hand like rain on a taut skin. A held note should be a pressure that changed as it died, so that she could hear the dying. She used the word hear without apology and without decoration. The letter mentioned her deafness once, in passing, the way a man might mention that he does not take tea.

She composed on paper. She had done so for thirty years, for singers and consorts and once for a cathedral, and she had never heard a bar of it, and when Ida began to ask how that was possible the composer answered the question she preferred.

"The yoke of the instrument matters more than the strings," she said. "Whatever you build, I will hold it here." She laid two fingers along her collarbone. "And here." She turned her hands over, palms up, on the bench. "Everything you make must arrive at one of those two places. What does not arrive does not exist. Your viol is a fine instrument for the air. I am not commissioning it for the air."

She watched their mouths when they spoke. When the light failed she made them write, and she answered aloud in her flat, deliberate voice, each word set down like a weight on a scale.

That night Anselm sat alone with the rejected viol and pressed it to his own collarbone and drew the bow, and felt, faintly, a blur where he knew there was a chord. Forty years of tuning had taught him what a true fifth sounds like. He did not know what one weighs. The whole vocabulary of his craft lived in the air, and the air was not where this customer lived.

He looked across the shop at the cabinet in the corner, the drum of it trembling faintly with the harbor night.

"You and I," he said, "are going to learn a second way to listen."

***

The machine could not feel wood against a body. But it could listen to what wood did, if the wood were made to speak to it, and Anselm spent a morning arranging that. He took a rod of beech, set one end against the back of an instrument and the other against the rim of Fable's drum, and packed the horn with wool. After that the machine was deaf to the air of the shop, and everything it took in came up through the rod, through grain and glue, the way the composer's music would come to her.

Then the work found its shape, and the shape was a circle.

Ida played. The composer held the instrument against her collarbone and said what arrived and what did not, in her own terms, which were exact: this one is wide, this one is thin, this one comes late, this one dies at the wrist. And the stylus, listening through the rod, wrote what the wood had actually done. In the evening Anselm sat with the slate and the composer's notes side by side and matched them, one against the other, a ledger of two languages.

Within a week the machine had found something no one at the bench would have looked for.

*Two strings tuned near each other and sounded together travel the wood as a slow tremor,* the stylus wrote. *Either string alone travels as warmth, and is gone.*

Anselm read it twice. He knew that tremor. Every tuner knows it: the slow beating of two strings almost in agreement, the pulse that swells and fades as they argue. He had spent forty years killing it. It was the sound of not-yet, the flaw you tuned away until the strings rang as one and the beating stopped. To the ear it was an error.

Through the bone it was the message.

They proved it that afternoon. Ida tuned two strings a hair apart, on purpose, wincing as she did it, and sounded them together, and the composer's face changed the way a face changes when a door opens.

"That," she said. "That arrives. That is the first thing you have played me that has a body."

So the instrument would not be built of single strings. It would be built of pairs, each pair tuned almost together, each chord a weave of slow arguments, and the argument was what the collarbone would read. Anselm sat with that for a while. The truest carrier was not either string. It was the disagreement between them. He thought of a winter of watching the stylus write its guesses and correct them against the world, and said nothing, and got out the spruce.

The machine could find what carried. It could not say what any of it was like. So the composer taught them her vocabulary, three evenings running, the shop dark except for the bench lamp, Ida playing tremor after tremor while the composer held the test frame to her collarbone and sorted the world.

"Wide and slow, and arriving a little after you expect it. That is grief. Do not let anyone tell you grief is heavy. It is late." She waited while Ida wrote it down. "Small and fast and close together, high in the hands: joy. Anger is not louder than the others. It is earlier. And this one," as Ida let a low pair die slowly under a flattened palm, "this is the one I will use most, and it has no name, so write: the tide going out."

Anselm carried her words to the slate the way he had once carried timber merchants' orders to the sawyer. Wide, slow, late. The machine had no use for grief. It had every use for wide, slow, late, and by morning the slate held an ordering of every pairing and every wood they had tried, ranked by how wide, how slow, how late. The composer read the list like a woman reading proofs of her own book: this one, not that one, this one, never that one.

She corrected the machine, too. Its ordering put a pairing of brass over gut near the top, wide and slow and late by every measure on the slate, and she tried it once against her collarbone and set it aside. "It is wide," she said, "and it is nothing. A big man with no news." Anselm ruled a second column in the ledger that evening: *carries*, and beside it, *arrives*. The machine went on filling the first, faithfully, all night if he let it. Only she could fill the second.

The work passed around the bench the way bread passes at a table.

Anselm planed a belly of mountain spruce and sealed it, as he had sealed every belly of his life, with hard wax. The composer laid her palm on it and shook her head. "The quick figures die above the wrist. You have dressed it too warmly." The stylus, through the rod, agreed in its own tongue: *the waxed wood carries the slow tremors; the quick ones die in the wax.* Anselm would not leave wood bare on a coast where the weather came indoors. He counter-offered a thin oil, wax below where the slow weight lived, oil above where the rain-figures ran, and the composer tested the seam with two fingers for a long minute and said, "Keep that."

Ida shaped the yoke, the curved rest that would carry the instrument's back against the collarbone, and the composer corrected its angle by the width of a finger, and the slate settled the quarrel between them: *tilted so, the low pairs carry into both shoulders; flat, into one.*

And once, past midnight, with everyone asleep but the harbor, the machine did the thing Anselm had stopped being surprised by and never stopped watching. They had tried sixty pairings of string and tension before dark and given up sorting them. By morning the slate held the sixty in order, and at the head of the list a pairing nobody had chosen twice, gut over silk, tuned a hair apart, low. Ida strung it and played it and the composer put her hand flat on the wood and stood very still.

"The tide going out," she said. "Wider than any of mine."

By the fourth week Anselm could no longer have said whose instrument it was. The pairs were the machine's finding and the ear said they were wrong and the bone said they were right. The vocabulary was the composer's and none of it was written in sound. The seam of wax and oil was his own stubbornness, corrected twice. The yoke was Ida's, corrected once. In the shop ledger, where for forty years he had written the maker's name against each commission, he left the column blank, and by the fifth week he had stopped noticing the blank, and nobody minded, least of all him.

***

Finished, it was a strange thing to look at. Wide flat back, a yoke like a swallow's wing, six pairs of strings over a low bridge, the belly two colors where the wax gave way to oil. It looked less like a viol than like the answer to a question no one had asked before, which is what it was.

The composer did not play her music first. She tested, the way Anselm tested a rebuilt bridge, without ceremony. She set the yoke to her collarbone and plucked each pair alone, damped each with the heel of her hand, counted the dying. She found the third pair a shade too settled, its two strings agreeing too well, and she retuned it herself, by feel, widening the beat under her palm until the tremor sat where she wanted it. She corrected one peg twice. She was, Anselm thought, the best customer he had ever had, and he had once built for a duke.

Then she laid her left hand on the courses, and played a chord.

It was, Ida said later, nothing much in the air. A low weave, a little rough, the pairs arguing softly. But the composer's face was not in the air. The chord went into her collarbone and spread, and she held it until it had finished dying, all the way out, and then she played it again, and then a third time, softer, her head bent, her palm reading the wood the way Anselm read grain.

Then she wept.

Anselm had seen weeping in the shop. Widows over instruments that had outlived their players. Fathers at first lessons. This was neither, and he knew it from the inside the moment he saw it, because he had wept this way once himself, at sixteen, the first time a joint he had cut closed without a shadow. It was not the weeping of someone given a gift. It was the weeping of a maker in front of the thing being right, the verdict of thirty years of exact work arriving at the only judge qualified to hear the case.

"I wrote this chord eleven years ago," she said, when she chose to speak. "It has been paper until now."

She did not thank them then. She reached for her satchel, took out a page of manuscript, propped it on the bench, and went to work, because there were ten more chords on that page and she had waited eleven years and did not intend to wait through supper. That was the thanks. Anselm took it as such and swept the shop around her.

In the evening, while she played the whole piece through for the first time, Anselm stood by the cabinet and read the slate. The rod still stood between the instrument and the drum, and the stylus was writing its plain lines, unhurried, the way it told him the top notes of the world he had lost.

*The chord carries whole from the collarbone to both hands. The tide figure widens each time it returns.*

He could not hear the top of what she was playing. She could not hear any of it, in the way the word had once meant. The machine, wool in its horn, could not hear the air at all, and took the music only as wood speaking to a rod: all of the carrying, none of the arriving. Three kinds of listening in one small room, no two alike, and passing between them, whole in none of them, the music. Anselm thought about the winter he had spent believing he was building a replacement for his ears, and stood corrected, and was glad to stand there.

Ida came and stood beside him, watching the composer's hands.

"Whose is it?" she asked. Her question, as always, the one that cut. "The pairs were its finding. The words were hers. The wood was ours. Whose?"

Anselm listened a while to the part of the music that reached him.

"I don't know," he said, and heard how little the not-knowing weighed.

> **From the workbench**
>
> You take in the world one way, and that way has walls. So does everyone you work with, and so does everything, and the walls are in different places, which is the part everyone forgets. Across the bench from you sits a mind that fails where you are sound and is sound where you fail, and most days you treat that difference as friction, something to be managed, when it is the widest door in the building.
>
> Passing work through that door is a discipline, not a mood. Hand over the unfinished thing, honestly unfinished. Take it back changed and do not smooth away the change because it is not in your accent. Answer correction with a counter-offer, not surrender and not defense. Do it enough times and something arrives at the bench that was never in you and never in them, and you will know it is good because neither of you can quite claim it.
>
> This week, take one piece of work you cannot finish alone to whoever senses what you cannot, a person or a tool, it does not matter, so long as its walls are not your walls. Ask what they notice, not whether they agree. Change the work. Hand it back. The third thing only shows up while the work is moving between you. Keep it moving.
