# Chapter 3 — The Narrow Window

By midmorning of market day the machine had accused the fish-seller's bell of being a viol, the viol of being a cart, and the cart of being the sea.

Ida stood at the open sill reading the slate, and the slate was full, and everything on it was wrong.

Moving the machine to the window had been her idea, and she had argued it well the evening before. The machine had learned the shop the way a child learns one room. It knew the kettle, the stairs, the strings, the gulls that used the roofline. A mind raised on one room would stay a room-sized mind forever, she said. Give it the town. Market day came once a week, and the square under the window filled at dawn with more sound than the shop made in a month. She had imagined the stylus drinking all of it and coming back wiser.

Instead the stylus wrote like a man falling down stairs.

It started shapes and abandoned them. It began the church bell at the hour, faithfully, the way it had begun the bell a hundred evenings running, and then a gull crossed the bell, and a hammer crossed the gull, and the line that had set out as a bell arrived as nothing anyone had ever heard. It convicted a crate of live hens of being a badly bowed scale. It found a false string in the axle of the water cart. Twice it wrote the beginning of the ferry horn and finished it, both times, as rain.

And it was hoarding again. Through the winter Anselm had taught it to keep only what surprised it, and it had grown quick and lean on that diet, because in the shop most of any day matched what it expected and could be let go. But nothing in the square matched anything. The whole roaring morning was surprise, end to end, and so the machine reached to keep all of it, and staggered under the keeping, like a porter who has been told every parcel on the dock is precious.

"A hundred sounds," Ida said, "and it can't name one of them. In the shop it names everything. It's gone simple."

Anselm came to the window and stood a while, listening to the square the way a man listens to weather. Then he lifted the machine off the sill, carried it to the back wall, and set it down among the familiar tools with its horn toward the kettle. He put the kettle on.

The stylus sketched the coming boil a full minute before the water made a sound, and corrected itself against the true boil so lightly there was almost nothing to correct.

"Not simple," he said.

"Then what?"

"Drowned."

He let her stand with that. It was his way of teaching, giving her the word and making her build the rest of the house herself, and she had learned to hate it and to do it.

"In the shop," she said slowly, "sounds come one at a time. Or two. It guesses, the world answers, and it knows which guess the answer belongs to." She looked at the slate, at the wreck of the morning. "In the square there are a hundred things talking at once. So when a guess goes wrong, any of the hundred could be the reason. The correction comes home and can't find its own door."

"And?"

"And a correction that can't find its guess teaches nothing." She frowned. "Worse than nothing. It teaches wrong. The bell's error gets charged to the gull."

Anselm nodded at the drum of waxed cloth, trembling faintly with the kettle and the room.

"One skin," he said. "Everything that reaches it lands on the same cloth. The bell, the hens, the hammer, all added together into a sum nobody made. It wasn't hearing a hundred sounds. It was hearing one sound that a hundred things were lying about."

Ida was quiet for a moment. Then, because she was honest before she was proud: "I thought more would make it more."

"So does everyone," Anselm said. "Once."

***

He spent two days at the bench with brass and felt, and what he made was not clever to look at. A horn. Long, slow-tapered, hammered thin, its sides packed with felt so that nothing entered through the walls of it, only through the mouth, and the mouth was small. A coin would have covered it. It looked, Ida said, like an ear trumpet for a suspicious old man, and Anselm said that was near enough the design brief.

It did not make the machine hear more. That was the part she watched him accept with no struggle at all, as if he had built such things all his life. It made the machine hear less, and mostly less. Of the hundred voices of the square, the throat of the horn delivered one.

The next market day he set the machine on the sill again and aimed the horn across the square at the fiddler who played by the well for pennies.

Within the hour the slate held the fiddler entire. It sketched his tunes ahead of his bow, faltering only on the ones he had learned since last week. It caught the lag in his E string, that small late answering it had once caught in the merchant's viol, and flagged the string in the quiet space between two dances. By afternoon Ida had walked across the square, told the fiddler his peg would slip by evening, and walked back through the crowd with the fiddler's astonishment following her like a smell.

Anselm turned the horn on the harbor. By the next tide the machine had begun to tell, from the sill of a shop street, the cry of a gull that had found fish from the cry of a gull that only hoped. He turned it on the ferry landing and it learned the ferry. Whatever the narrow mouth was given, the machine was brilliant about, and it was brilliant in the old way, the shop way: guessing, checking, keeping the argument small enough to win.

Ida found the trick's edge herself, playing with the aim while Anselm ate. She swung the horn from the fiddler to the ferry to the well and back, quick, giving each a minute, meaning to gather all three. The slate filled with beginnings. Every swing threw away the argument the machine had been building and made it start the next one poor, so that a morning of three things taught it less than an hour of one. Aimed slowly, it was a mind. Swung fast, it was the open sill again, only tired.

"So it works," Ida said, "as long as it's poor."

"It works because it's poor."

"Then make the window wider." She said it the way she said most things, straight through the middle. "You've built the thing a keyhole. It has a whole market out there. Widen the throat, let more in, let it be brilliant about everything at once."

Anselm was cleaning the felt at the horn's mouth and did not stop.

"Wider windows," he said, "are how you hear everything and listen to nothing."

"That's a saying. Sayings are what you give me instead of reasons."

He almost smiled. "Then here is the reason. While the horn was on the fiddler, the bell rang, the hens complained, and a wheel broke on the far side of the square. It missed all of it. Deaf as a stone to the whole town." He set the horn back in its cradle. "That deafness is not the flaw in the morning's work, girl. It is the fee. Being right about the fiddler was bought with everything the machine refused to hear while it was being right. Nothing brilliant has ever been paid for any other way."

Ida looked at the horn a long time.

"Then the aiming is the whole game," she said. "The box is only ever as wise as whatever the mouth is pointed at. Somebody has to choose, and the box can't."

"No," Anselm said. "The box can't."

He did not say more about that, then. But he took the horn's aim off its cradle that night and put it where he put things he meant to think about, which was directly in the way of his own hands.

***

It was Ida herself who furnished the second proof, though she did not enjoy being evidence.

That same spring she had asked for everything, and he had let her have it. The varnish, because varnish was a trade of its own. The fret work. The bow hair. The ledger. The customers, whom she wanted to practice charming. She had gone at the week like the machine on the open sill, all of it at once, certain that more would make her more, and Saturday had found her with six things started, nothing finished, the varnish clouded because she had left it to answer the door, the ledger wrong in two places, and her temper filed to a point.

Anselm had said nothing all week, which she later understood had cost him something.

On the Monday he gave her one soundboard, a bench by the half-closed shutter, and the sentence: "The rest of the shop is mine today."

She began it sulking. By noon the sulk had gone somewhere and not come back. The world narrowed to spruce, and the grain of the spruce, and the small honest sounds the wood made under the plane, tap and flex and hush, and she caught herself hearing the board the way the machine heard the fiddler, ahead of it, arguing with it, correcting. She worked through the lunch she forgot and the rain she never noticed. What she carried to the lamp at dusk was the best work of her hands to that day, and she knew it before he said it, which was the first time that had ever happened.

She stood there holding the board, and being Ida, said the thing.

"You built me a horn."

"I took away the market," Anselm said. "It's the same act."

He told her then, since she had earned it, the truth about his own famous ear. The coast said Anselm could hear a false string through a wall, and the coast was wrong. What he had, he said, was forty years of refusals. When he tuned, the harbor did not exist. The shop could fill with talk and the talk was weather. People called it a gift because they could not see the fee being paid, every minute, in all the things he was electing not to hear. His head was one skin too. Everything landed on the same cloth. He had simply learned, young, that the cloth could be aimed.

"And the aiming can't be bought," Ida said, "or built."

"It can be built," Anselm said. "You watched me build it in brass in two days. What can't be done is escaping it. Wide or narrow, cheap or dear, every mind that has ever worked has worked through a throat. The only question you get to answer is whether you are the one who points it."

That evening the market was gone, the square swept, and the machine sat on the sill with its horn toward the harbor mouth, listening to the one bell buoy at the channel's edge. The stylus wrote small and sure: the buoy's swing, the water under it, the slow arithmetic of the tide. A hundred sounds had been offered to it that week and it had been given one, and the one, taken whole, held more than the hundred had.

Ida watched the line walk across the slate, quiet and right.

"It's not a keyhole," she said at last. "It's a door it can actually fit through."

Anselm turned down the lamp.

> You are not short of hours. You are short of what an hour is made of: the narrow stream of world you can truly take in at once. It is one skin, one throat, one small mouth, and it does not widen because the demands on it widen. Every open door spends it. Everything you leave yourself open to lands on the same cloth and is added, whether you meant to listen or not, into a sum nobody made.
>
> So the skill is not letting more in. More is the open sill on market day. The skill is the aiming, and paying for the aim without flinching: to be brilliant about one thing this morning is to be deaf to the rest of it on purpose, and the deafness is not the price of the work. It is the work.

> Tomorrow, before you begin: name the one thing the horn points at. Then write down, where you can see them, three worthy things you will not listen to today. Not because they don't matter. Because they do, and you are one drum.
