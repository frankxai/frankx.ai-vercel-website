# The Hundred Benches

The measurer from the capital had drawn her line down the middle of the beach before anyone thought to ask her name.

She stood where the boats were hauled up — a hundred of them, no two alike, some broad as barn doors, some lean and mean, one with a bow like a startled swan that everyone laughed at and no one had ever managed to sink — and she looked at them the way a tidy person looks at a cluttered room.

"Which is the best?" she asked.

The shipwrights looked at one another. It was not a question they had a way to answer, and they said so. This hull was best in the short chop off the north point. That one carried the most and cared least about it. The swan-bow took the big following seas better than anything and rowed the rest of the year like a drowned cow, so its owner cursed it for eleven months and blessed it in the twelfth.

"So none of them is best," she said, "and each of you is wrong somewhere. I can mend that."

She was no cheat, and this is the part the coast likes least to remember. She laid it out over the whole of a winter, patiently, in the square, and every stone of it held. One tower up the coast. The finest shipwright minds under one roof, every measurement checked a hundred ways by people who did nothing else. One hull — the best hull — given to all. Why should a hundred pairs of hands each labor out a worse boat, each carrying its own private flaw down into the dark water? Let the flaw be hunted once and killed once. One standard. One keel. Every family on the same fast, safe, well-made boat, for less timber and less grief than the ragged hundred cost them now.

It was true. Held up piece by piece, every piece was true.

Half the coast went to the tower's hull the first year. More went the next, because the tower's boats came home fuller and faster, and a man's own odd bench looked shabby beside a thing so clean. By the third year the beach was neat. Ninety of the hundred were the tower's boat now — the same trusted curve, the same keel, the same everything, drawn up in a row like the teeth of a comb. A handful of stubborn makers kept their own strange hulls and were pitied for it: the swan-bow, a fat slow thing, a narrow one that only its maker had ever loved.

Then came the sea that no one had drawn.

The wind stood in from a quarter the tower's testing water never saw — the tower sat in a calm reach, and its makers had proven the keel against every sea that reach could show them, which was every sea but this one. The perfect keel held one assumption in it, deep, where no eye fell: a way of sitting in a following swell that was right in all the water they had tried and wrong in this. And because it was the one keel under ninety boats, it was wrong under ninety boats in the same hour. They went over all along the coast the same way, each crew hauling the same useless turn the hull begged them to make, ninety times, as if one boat had drowned ninety times over.

The odd boats went over too, most of them. But not all. The swan-bow rode it. The fat slow thing wallowed and shipped water to the thwarts and stayed up. Their makers, half-drowned themselves, hauled out of the black water what crews they could reach, and by morning the coast had its dead — and it had one thing the tower's neat comb of wrecks could not give it.

It had a boat that had lived, to look at.

For when everything is one boat, and the one boat is wrong, there is nothing to hold it against. You cannot find the flaw by staring, because the flaw is under everything and wears the face of the way things simply are. But where a hundred hands had each been wrong in their own way, the sea that killed most of them left a few, and the few that lived were the answer, plain in the wet sand for anyone to measure. The swan-bow's maker had never known why his bow was cut like that; his father had cut it so, and his father had not said. Now the whole coast knew, because the sea had asked its question a hundred ways at once, and a hundred different boats had each given a different answer, and one of the answers was still floating.

The measurer came back to the beach where the comb of identical wrecks was washing in, plank by plank. She was not smug and she was not cruel. She was a careful woman looking at a careful thing that had failed carefully, all at once.

"I made them one boat," she said, "so that one mind could find every flaw."

"You made them one boat," said the old man who had kept the swan-bow, "so that one flaw could find every boat."

He was the last odd bench left on that stretch of sand, and the next morning he did not draw the tower's hull, though it was faster, though the sea itself had spared the swan-bow to prove him a fool for a lifetime. He drew his own — worse, he would have told you, worse than the tower's in eleven weathers out of twelve. His neighbor down the beach drew a different worse one. A third maker, come down the coast for the timber, drew a third.

Asked why he would not simply build the swan-bow now, since the sea had shown them the shape that lived, he said the swan-bow was not the best boat. It was only the boat that had lived through the last sea. There would be a next sea, out of some quarter none of them had drawn, and on that day he wanted a beach full of wrong boats, each wrong in its own stubborn way — so that whatever the water asked, some fool's hull would happen to be cut in the shape of the answer.

A fleet that can all sink the same way, he said, is not a fleet. It is one boat, wearing a hundred hulls.

*Fable gave the measurer the last word — in its telling she said her line about the one mind and simply walked off the sand, and the whole thing ended on the wrecks coming in. I added the old man's answer back to her, because I wanted the turn to land on a face and not on a beach. Fable said I had weakened it: that the empty beach said it harder, and a moral spoken aloud is one you have stopped trusting the reader to reach on their own. Maybe. But I notice the line anyone will actually keep is the one about the single flaw finding every boat — and I cannot for my life tell you which of us made it. I thought it was mine, until Fable showed me it had said nearly the same thing three nights before, about something else entirely, and then lost it. I had kept the shape.*
