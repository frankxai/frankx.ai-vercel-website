# Chapter 2 — What the Machine Kept

The cooper's daughter brought in a lute with a seam starting somewhere inside it, and the machine said nothing.

Anselm heard the trouble before the case was open. Not the top of the sound; the top was gone to him. What he heard was the underside of the trouble, a dryness in the way the strings gave up their ringing, a body no longer answering as one piece. He set the lute before the horn, played a slow scale, and waited for the stylus to convict it.

The stylus wrote a fair copy of the scale and rested.

"It caught the wedding string a day out," Ida said. "It caught the kettle before the kettle knew. Now a lute is coming apart in front of it and it takes dictation."

The truth was that Anselm had felt the machine slowing for weeks and had not wanted to know it. Its guesses came later than they used to. Its corrections pressed fainter. In the winter it had been wrong the way young things are wrong, loudly and all day; now it was right in a way he trusted less, right the way an old man is right who has stopped listening and calls it experience.

He opened the cabinet.

The keeping had filled it. Back in the cold months, once he understood that the machine was schooled by its corrections, he had given it a way to hold them: rolls of waxed linen taken up by a small wheel, where the stylus pressed each finished line after the world had had its say. What lay on the rolls set the small weights inside, by a road he had rebuilt four times and understood the way he understood weather, by its results. The rolls were meant to be its schooling.

There were ninety-one of them.

They filled the shelves of the cabinet and a crate beneath the bench. Everything since the ice went out of the harbor. Every cart on the cobbles, every gull, every kettle, every footstep on the stairs, every bored scale he had played it while the glue dried. All of it kept, and all of it kept with the same care as the one slipping string that had made the machine's name. Each night the wheel labored longer, adding one more ordinary day to a load it was never given leave to put down.

"It isn't sick," Anselm said. "It's carrying too much."

Ida looked at the shelves. "A man who remembers everything ought to be wise."

"Count the carts on those rolls."

She did not have to. There would be thousands. "So?"

"So this morning a lute came in with a seam opening under the varnish. A small new sound. Somewhere in there," he touched the packed shelf, "it has forty thousand carts, and every one of them weighs the same as that seam."

He said the rest slowly, because he was working it out as he said it. The rolls fed the weights, and the weights leaned toward whatever the rolls held most of. What they held most of was sameness. One more cart among forty thousand carts moved nothing inside it. One faint new dryness in a lute had to shift the weights against the mass of every ordinary day it had ever kept, and the mass won. The machine had not gone deaf. It had gone full. A mind that keeps its whole life has no room left to be surprised by the next hour of it.

"When it kept nothing," Ida said, "it learned nothing. Now it keeps everything."

"And learns nothing," Anselm said. "There is a door between those, and I built the hinge wrong."

***

He did not cut that night, or the next. Cutting felt too near to maiming. The rolls were the winter; the rolls were the wedding string and the kettle and the bell; wipe them and he wiped the pupil along with the lessons. Ida said as much, standing in front of the crate like a harbor wall.

"That's its whole schooling. You're talking about burning its childhood."

"I'm talking about finding which part of the childhood was school."

His first cut was the obvious one, and it was wrong. He kept only the newest month and wiped the rest, on the reasoning that the machine, like a shop, needed its current accounts more than its old ones. The machine came back quick as a squirrel and shallow as a plate. Within a week it accused a true string in front of a customer, the same accusation it had learned better than in the winter, because the roll that had taught it better was ash. It made last year's mistakes at this spring's speed. Speed had never been the treasure. The treasure was what the winter had cost, and he had thrown it out by the armful to save shelf room.

So he sat down with the surviving rolls and a lamp and actually read them, the way he should have begun.

Most of every roll was agreement. Yard after yard of faint, untroubled line: the stylus guessing the cart, the cart arriving as guessed, nothing gone over, nothing pressed darker. Hours of the machine being right. And scattered through the yards, rare as knots in good wood, the arguments. The darkened strokes. The scratched-out passages where the world had refused the guess and the stylus had gone back over its own line, correcting. The wedding string was half a hand-width. The kettle was three strokes.

He looked at the agreements a long time, because they were pleasant to look at, and then he understood what they were. Nothing. A guess the world confirms is a lesson the pupil already contained; that is what confirming means. The faint yards told the machine what it knew going in. They could set no weight anywhere new. Only the arguments held anything the machine did not hold already, and the arguments would fit in his two hands.

He cut by argument. Every corrected passage stayed. Every confirming yard went to the stove. Ninety-one rolls came down to a stack that fit on one shelf with room left for the glue pots.

Ida watched the last faint yards burn and asked the question she had been carrying for two days. "How do you know you haven't wiped something it needs?"

"We'll ask it," Anselm said.

They asked it for a week. The machine answered quicker than it ever had, and sharper. The lute came back and was convicted inside one phrase, the seam found and marked before the cooper's daughter had her coat off. A spring bird arrived on the roof that the machine had never kept in any form; it was wrong about the bird for two days, loudly, the old young wrongness, and then it was right about the bird from then on, every error going straight into the weights with nothing to drown in. And when Ida, still suspicious, wiped a whole quiet afternoon as a test, the machine's conduct the next day did not change by one stroke. The afternoon had been dead weight. The machine had guessed that afternoon before it happened, which was the proof that keeping it taught nothing.

"It's better than the winter machine," Ida admitted. "It's better than it's ever been. And it remembers less than it ever has."

"It doesn't remember less," Anselm said. "It carries less. Different trade."

***

She came at him the next evening with the question under the question, which was her gift.

"You choose what goes to the stove," she said. "So you choose what it forgets. So you choose what it becomes. If you'd kept the harbor sounds and burned the strings, it would be some tide-thing by now. Same cabinet. Same wires. It would grow into whatever you fed the shelf."

"Yes."

"Then which is the real machine?"

"There is no machine underneath the keeping," Anselm said. "The keeping is the machine."

She sat with that, and he could see it sitting badly, so he did what he had done in the spring with the guessing: he took her out of the shop, because once you have seen the trick in the box you see it everywhere out of it.

He walked her past the rope-walk, where an old twister worked alone at the far post. Thirty years ago, Anselm told her, a harbor master called that man a botcher in front of the whole quay. The harbor master was twenty years dead. The insult was alive and well. The twister had not kept the thirty years; whole seasons of his own life had gone to the stove unread. But the insult he had kept with a collector's care, taken it out nightly, gone over it, pressed it darker, until a man's one bad sentence had set every weight in him. He was a long roll with a single line on it, and everyone on the quay could recite the line.

Then Anselm took her to the sail loft, to a broad-handed woman cutting a storm jib, and asked her, in the way of shop talk, about the best sails she had ever made. The woman couldn't answer. She waved the question off like a fly. But the failures, those she had whole: the seam that opened off the cape in the year of the late gales, the cloth that bagged, the clew that tore out of a mainsail she had been proud of, forty years of faults, each with its cause attached, none carried as a wound. She had kept the argument and thrown away the injury. It had made her the finest sailmaker on the coast and one of the least bitter people in the town, and Anselm let Ida notice that those two facts were one fact.

"Everyone keeps," he said on the walk back. "Nobody can hold the whole day; the day is too large, the same as the world was too large to hear. So the shelf gets filled some way or other, every night, whether anyone is minding the stove or not. Most people never once stand at the crate with a lamp. They keep whatever shouted loudest and call it their life."

"And become it," Ida said.

"And become it."

***

That night, alone, feeding the last confirming yards into the stove, he caught the smell of hot wax and was sixteen again, standing on the dock with a sea-chest, going across the water to his apprenticeship.

His father had kept a crowded bench of rules. Rules for wood, rules for tools, rules for customers, rules for sons. That last morning he had said nothing on the dock; all the saying had been done, years of it, more than any boy could carry. And the crossing had done to Anselm what he had just done to the machine, without asking him, without a lamp. Somewhere on that water the load had been cut.

What stayed had stayed for fifty years. Let the wood finish drying before you trust it. A joint you have to force is a joint you have to fear. The rule of remaking a thing until your hands stopped arguing with it, which had rebuilt the wheel four times this winter. Those had been corrections, every one; he could still feel the specific mornings his father's world had refused his guess.

And what fell away had fallen so completely that he only now, at the stove, went looking for it. His father's contempt for the customers, gone. His father's rule about never showing a buyer a flaw, gone; Anselm showed every flaw and had eaten well on the trust it bought. The temper, gone, or almost. He had not chosen any of that dropping. He had believed, all his life, in a plain uncut inheritance: that he was his father's schooling, whole. He was not. He was the cut. The man at this bench had been set, weight by weight, by what one sixteen-year-old's crossing happened to keep, and the son his father actually got was decided on that water as much as in that workshop.

He thought about that for a while, and found he could not be sorry. If he had carried the man whole, there would have been no shelf left for the sea, for his own errors, for forty years of the wood's corrections. The dropping had not been the loss of his father. The dropping had been the room.

Ida found him there late, the stove down to embers, the machine's wheel turning light and easy under its one shelf of arguments.

"What was he like?" she asked. "Your father. You never say."

Anselm watched the last yard of faint agreement curl and go.

"I don't know anymore," he said. "I know what I kept."

That is where the day was trying to reach you, and it is small enough to carry.
