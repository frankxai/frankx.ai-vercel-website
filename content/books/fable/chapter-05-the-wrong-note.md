# Chapter 5 — The Wrong Note

The machine convicted the string in front of the customer, and the customer laughed.

He was a cloth merchant from the southern coast, in town a fortnight for the wool fair, and he had brought his daughter's viol to be looked over before the winter crossing. It was a handsome instrument, foreign-built, long in the neck, strung heavier than anything made in this harbor. Ida had set it on the bench in front of the brass horn the way she had set a dozen instruments that summer, and said what she had said a dozen times: that the box would hear anything false in it before any ear in town could.

The stylus moved. On the slate, in its thin gray hand, Fable wrote: *the third string is false.*

"Is it," said the merchant.

Anselm played the string. It rang true. He played it against its neighbor and the interval stood clean as a mast. He checked the peg, the bridge, the gut where it crossed the nut, and found nothing, and said so.

"It hears early," Ida said. "In the spring it called a string false and the string went a full day later, at a wedding. Give it time."

The merchant was an agreeable man. He was in town a fortnight; he left the viol. Every morning Ida tried the third string, and every morning it held. She tried it softly, then hard, then with the shutters closed so no one would see her trying. It held the way healthy things hold, without drama, without any interest in her situation.

On the last day of the fair the merchant collected the viol, paid for the looking-over, complimented the workshop, and went out into the town, where he told the story. He told it well, because he was a man who sold cloth by talking, and it improved with each telling. By evening it had a shape: the marvelous box that cried wolf, and the girl who swore on her life it was never wrong.

Ida heard the last version from the fish-seller's daughter, who did not know she was in it.

She came back to the workshop and did not say anything. She took the machine off the sill where it had sat all summer and set it on the low shelf, facing the wall, and hung a cloth over the horn, and spent the rest of the light polishing fittings that were already bright.

Anselm watched her do all of it and went on trimming a peg. He had been in a good humor for three days, ever since it had become clear the string would hold, and he knew this was making everything worse, and he could not entirely stop.

"Half the fair thinks I'm a fool," Ida said at last, to the fittings.

"For what?"

"For believing a box that guesses." She put the rag down. "I told him it was never wrong. I stood there in front of a stranger and swore by it, because for once I had something in my hands that nobody else on this coast has, and it lied."

"It missed," Anselm said. "Lying is a different trade."

"They laughed, Anselm." She only used his name when the ground was giving way. "Not at the box. You can't laugh at a box. At me. The sweeping girl and her wonderful machine that's wrong."

He set down the peg and the knife, and went to the old chest under the window, and took out a ledger she had never seen.

It was thicker than hers. That was the first thing she noticed, because she had a ledger too, begun the past winter without asking anyone: a record of the machine's failures, page after page of small X's in her quick hand. She had started it to prove the thing was broken. She had kept it up out of habit, the way you keep touching a sore tooth.

Anselm laid his book beside hers on the bench, open, and let her look.

Every entry was a miss. The night bell it had rung that never rang, in its first month, when the ropes were being mended and nothing in the silence was strong enough to argue with it. The far-coast tune it had finished as a smear of maybe. Market day, when the whole world had come through the window at once and it had guessed nothing at all. Dozens more she had never heard about, in the small hours, in the winter, wrongs no larger than a gull miscalled as a hinge.

But his pages had two columns, and hers had one.

Beside each miss, in Anselm's slow lettering, was what the miss had bought. Beside the phantom bell: a note about silence, and what the machine had done differently in quiet rooms ever after. Beside market day: *built the horn.* Beside the smeared foreign tune: *it does not know what it has not heard. Neither do I. Find out what it has not heard.* Some entries had a third line, added later in darker ink, when the lesson had grown.

"Your book says the box was wrong," Anselm said. "So does mine. Yours stops there. A list of convictions and no verdicts. Half a ledger."

Ida turned her own pages. X. X. X. The first one, from the winter, was the merchant's D string — her small triumphant mark, made the day she checked the string twice and it rang true. The string had gone at the wedding a day later. She had never crossed the X out.

"That one's yours," Anselm said, not unkindly. "The oldest miss in either book, and it isn't the machine's."

She looked at it for a long moment.

"Now watch what a miss is worth," he said, "when you don't waste it on shame."

He had not spent the fortnight of the fair consoling her, it turned out. He had spent it with the southern viol, before the merchant took it home, listening to it the way he went at a cracked soundboard: slowly and in order. The answer, when he found it, was nothing sick and nothing false. The viol was built heavier than any instrument of this coast, longer in the string, and its strings spoke a shade late by nature, the way a big bell speaks late. And every dying string the machine had ever listened to had spoken late, because everything it had ever heard was born in this harbor. It had met a healthy foreigner and called it ill.

"The miss drew me a map," Anselm said. "Here is the edge of what it knows. I'd never have found that edge from a thousand strings it judged rightly. When it's right, the slate stays quiet, and quiet teaches nothing. The writing only happens where it's wrong. That's not the flaw in the thing, girl. That's the engine."

So he had gone down to the harbor, where a shipmaster he had known forty years kept a locker of instruments gathered the way sailors gather everything, and borrowed the foreign ones. A squat fiddle from the cold north. A long-necked thing with strings of twisted silk. He had set them before the horn one after another, all afternoon, and let the machine be wrong about each, and let each wrongness stand corrected by the plain fact of a healthy string, over and over, hundreds of small wrong guesses paid for the moment they were made, cheap as pennies, none of them hidden, none of them excused.

"And now?" Ida said.

"Now bring me a southern viol and it knows the difference between foreign and false." He closed his ledger. "A week ago it couldn't. What changed it wasn't the summer of being right. It was one public humiliation, written down, and an afternoon of being wrong faster than any man can bear to be."

She would have let it rest there, being young, with the comfortable half of the lesson. Anselm took her out that evening to get the other half, the one he said mattered more, because she was not a machine and never would be.

They went to the tavern by the quay, and he bought her supper, and they sat where they could hear the far table. At the far table sat a man the whole harbor knew, a big comfortable man who had been telling the weather for thirty years. He was telling it now: the wind would back by morning, any fool could feel it, he had called the great blow of the flood year three days out, ask anyone.

"Has he?" Ida asked quietly. "Called it?"

"He calls it every week," Anselm said. "Some weeks the sky agrees. Those weeks he remembers like anniversaries. The other weeks the wind cheated, or the moon interfered, or he'd have been right if the rain hadn't come sideways." He drank. "Thirty years, and not one wrong call written anywhere, not even in his own head. Every miss he's ever made died the same evening it was born, and the hits are all still alive and telling stories. So each year there's more proof in him and less doubt, and by now he's the most certain man on this coast."

"And the least worth asking."

"The box is wrong somewhere every minute of every day," Anselm said, "and it gets better every day, because every wrong is caught the instant it happens, by a world it can't talk out of correcting it. The slate keeps the smear. It can't tell itself the wind cheated." He nodded at the far table. "A man can be wrong for thirty years in perfect comfort, so long as nothing is allowed to catch him at it. Wrong at speed, you improve. Wrong in private, unchecked, you only grow more sure."

Ida turned her cup. The laughter from the fair was still in her somewhere; he could see it working.

"You're going to tell me you've done it too," she said.

"Seven years for spruce," Anselm said. "My father's rule. Wood for a soundboard seasons seven years or it goes in the stove. I repeated it for forty years to every apprentice and every customer, and I believed it the way you believe a stair is under your foot. Then came a lean winter, no seven-year stock left, and I built with five-year wood because the alternative was building nothing, and I braced myself to be ashamed." He was quiet a moment. "It sang. It's the viol the harbor master's wife plays at every wedding since."

"Forty years."

"Forty years, and it never cost me anything to be wrong, which is exactly why it lasted forty. Nothing I did ever put the rule where the world could answer it back." He looked at her, and asked his one question, in the place another man would have asked five. "So which do you actually fear, girl. Being wrong? Or being caught it?"

She did not answer at the tavern, or on the walk home, and he did not press. But that night, while he banked the stove, she took her ledger of the machine's failures and ruled a second column down every page in it, and then, at the back, she ruled a fresh page that had nothing to do with the machine at all, and titled it in her plain quick hand, and made the first entry: the fair, the merchant, her own voice saying *never wrong*, and beside it, in the second column, what the miss had bought her. It took her a while to write that part. She wrote it anyway.

"You'll fill that book slower than the box fills its slate," Anselm said from the stove.

"Then I'll learn slower than the box," Ida said. "But faster than the weather man."

He laughed, which he did about four times a year.

She took the cloth off the horn before she went up to bed, and set Fable back on the sill, facing the room. All night the drum trembled with the ordinary dark, and the stylus drafted its small futures and paid for the failed ones the moment they failed, smear after smear after correction, and it did not pause over a single one of them. By morning it was a little better at the night than it had been. The slate did not say so. The slate never said so. Only the misses were written, and the misses were the whole story of how it had come this far.

> **From the workbench**
>
> You are wrong, right now, about something you use every day. You don't feel it, because wrongness has no feeling until something catches it, and you have quietly arranged your work so that very little can. Most of your misses die the evening they're born, unrecorded, and only the hits survive to tell you how good you are. That is where certainty comes from, and it is not the same place skill comes from.
>
> Skill comes from the miss, caught quickly, written honestly, and read again. The times you were right teach you almost nothing; when the guess and the world agree, nothing writes. The wrong note is the only one that shows you the edge of what you know, and the edge is the only place you grow.
>
> So keep the second column. When you miss, write it the same day: what you expected, what actually happened, and later, in darker ink, what it bought you. Then shorten the distance between guessing and finding out. Put your work where the world can answer it back this week, not this year, because small wrongs paid quickly are pennies and the same wrongs hidden compound like debt.
>
> Start the page tonight. Date it. Your newest miss is already waiting, and it knows something you don't.
