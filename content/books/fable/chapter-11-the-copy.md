# Chapter 11 — The Copy

The crate came up from the harbor on two poles, carried by four men who were being paid enough to be careful, and half the town walked behind it the way they would walk behind a wedding or a drowning.

Ida stood at the workshop window until the procession turned toward the inn. "He's back," she said. "The man from the capital."

Anselm went on fitting a peg. "He was always coming back."

An hour later the stylus moved before any boots sounded on the stairs. Ida read the slate and her mouth went thin, and then the Broker was in the doorway with his hat in his hands, courteous as weather in June.

"Master Anselm." He nodded to Ida as though she had ever once smiled at him. "I've brought something up from the ship that I would rather show you than describe. Describing it would sound like boasting, and I try never to boast where I can demonstrate. This evening, the long room at the inn. The harbormaster is coming, and the guild men. I would count it a favor if you came, and the favor would be mine, which is the best kind for you to hold."

"You had a man in here last autumn," Ida said. "The corn-factor. He asked questions for three days and bought nothing."

"He bought a great deal," the Broker said, "in a manner of speaking." And he smiled the way he smiled, which told you everything the sentence had left out.

When his boots had gone back down the stairs, Ida turned from the door. "You won't go."

"I'll go," Anselm said. "A man should see the thing itself. Rumor always improves it."

***

The long room smelled of tallow and wet wool and too many coats. The machine stood on trestles at the far end, and it was handsome. That was the first thing Anselm made himself notice, and he made himself notice it fairly: a rosewood case, a brass horn polished like a shaving mirror, joinery he would have been glad to sign at thirty. Fable was walnut and wax and eleven re-makings. This thing had been built once, correctly, by men who knew from the first morning what they were building. You could do that, when someone else had spent the winters finding out.

The Broker did not lecture. He let it work.

It wrote the room first. The slate filled in a fine steady hand: the gull on the ridgepole, the kettle in the back kitchen, the scrape of the benches, the harbormaster's cough sketched faintly a breath before it came, the man having coughed all evening at honest intervals. People laughed, and then heard what they were laughing at, and stopped.

Then a hired player stood up, a young woman with a fiddle. She played a reel from the near coast and stopped at the fourth bar, and the stylus wrote the fifth. She played a slow air from the far coast, the kind of tune that had once left Fable's stylus smeared with maybe, and stopped it halfway, and the stylus finished it. Confident as a signature.

"It has heard more than anyone in this room," the Broker said, to the room but facing Anselm. "Forty clerks in the capital have fed it for a year. Tunes, bells, gulls, sermons, arguments, the noise of three harbors and a city. It has been wrong more times than any made thing in the world, and every wrongness was written down and paid for by the piece. I learned the worth of a miss in this town." He gave a small bow toward the back bench. He never lied.

Someone called for a harder test, so the fiddler offered her own instrument, which rang true to every ear in the room, and the machine convicted the E string anyway. There was muttering. Anselm went up without being asked, took the fiddle, plucked the string, and set two fingers on the peg. He could not hear the top of that string and had not for years. But his hands had forty years in them, and the peg had the faint slack breath of a thing beginning to let go, a day out, maybe two. The box on the trestles had written it from across the room.

He handed the fiddle back and sat down. The fiddler stood a moment looking at her own instrument as though it had been talking about her behind her back, and then she retuned the E, which would not save it, and played on it anyway, which Anselm respected.

A grain merchant asked what such a thing would cost a town. "Less than a bad season," the Broker said, "and you have one of those every third year whether you pay for it or not." There was the laughter of men doing sums. Anselm listened to the rest, all of it, to the end.

Afterward the room emptied slowly, the way rooms empty when everyone wants to be last. The Broker came and stood beside him at the trestles. Behind them the stylus went on quietly writing the fire settling.

"Well, master Anselm."

Anselm looked at the machine a while longer. "It's good," he said.

The Broker was silent a moment, and for the first time in their acquaintance the pause did not sound like a price being worked. "From another man I'd take that for manners," he said. "From you I'll take it for an appraisal."

"It's neither. It's true."

"Then let me buy something with it." The Broker turned his hat once in his hands. "There will be one of these in every port by winter, and in every guild hall by the winter after. Harbors will pay gladly, and they'll be right to. I would rather the machines went out carrying your good word, and I'll pay for the word honestly. A tenth share, for 'good' said once in daylight."

"Who tells it when it's wrong," Anselm said, "now the clerks are done?"

"Whoever rents it. Every harbor will correct its own a hundred times a day, and once a season I'll gather what the corrections taught, from every machine, and pour it back into all of them. Each one feeding all. All of them feeding each." He let that sit, warm as a hearth. "It will only get better. Nothing on this coast improves at that speed. You built the first of a kind, and I'm offering you a share of the kind."

"You don't need it."

"No," the Broker agreed, and it was the plainest sentence Anselm had ever heard him spend. "But I'd have liked it."

He put his hat on, and at the door of the long room he paused. "You'll wonder how much my man saw. All of it, I should think. You keep no locks, and you wrote your method out plain enough that a stranger could work a bench from it. I was only ever the stranger it was plain enough for." He said it without triumph, the way he said everything, as a fact with a price on it. "Good evening, master Anselm."

Ida was waiting on the quay with her arms crossed against more than the cold. She held her tongue for ten steps, which for Ida was a fast.

"Why did you say it was good?"

"Because it was."

"He had it drawn in secret. He sat men in our shop with their ears open and their money folded, and you told him it was good."

"Nobody broke a lock," Anselm said. "The door has stood open forty years. I wasn't going to shut it for him."

"It finished the far-coast air." Her voice had the wobble she hated in it. "Fable never could. You told me so yourself, the first spring."

"No," Anselm said. "It never could."

"Then what do we have," she said, "that he doesn't now have handsomer, and forty clerks deep, and soon in every port?"

Anselm looked down the harbor, where the masts stood against the last light like a wall of instruments nobody had tuned yet.

"Ask me when I'm home," he said.

***

The road home went up through the town, and he took it slowly, the way he went at a cracked soundboard, in order.

The machine was good. Start there, and do not flinch from it. It would earn its rent in every port that took it. It would catch a rigging-line a day before it parted, a keg gone sour behind a wall of sound kegs, a fiddle's string before some other wedding lost its dance. The Broker was not wrong about the money, and he was not wrong about the good the money would ride in on. He had never once been wrong about money.

And it did not matter. Anselm had known it did not matter since the middle of the demonstration, known it the way you know a note is off before you can say which one, and he had spent the walk so far hunting for where the knowing lived.

He found it by the chandler's, where the street bent, remembering a viol.

Two years back, a wealthy man had asked for Anselm's work exactly, and Fable had produced it exactly, and the copy had been flawless and dead. He had opened that instrument like a body and found nothing missing. What made his viols his was nowhere in the wood. It was in forty years of small refusals the copyists never saw, and he had thought, then, that the lesson was about a man's hand. It was bigger than a hand.

Every choice in Fable was an answer to him. What it kept, it kept because he had taught it what was worth keeping, and he had taught it that the way he had learned it, by losing things. Where its narrow horn pointed, it pointed because his ears had stopped going there and something had to. The errors it was corrected on first were the errors that cost him, not the errors that cost everyone. Ten thousand corrections, and each one had come out of a particular evening in a particular room above a particular harbor, out of a dead father's dragging upbow, out of a register that had gone the way of a remembered face. He could have named a hundred of them outright: the week he hooded it with wool to learn what it did not need; the month he spent teaching it that cart wheels were not worth the slate; the winter of evenings he corrected it toward the top of the fiddle's E, over and over, not because the town needed that but because he did. Not one of those corrections could have been bought. Not because they were hidden. Because to buy them you would have to buy the failing ears and the forty years and the man.

The Broker's men had drawn everything. He was sure of that now and found he did not mind it. Every jig, the throat of the horn, the ledger's rulings, the little weights. And nothing they carried off was the machine, because the machine was the ten thousand answers, and the answers answered a question their machine had never been asked: what does this one man, going deaf in this one register, need to hear before winter?

They had copied everything it was. They had copied nothing of what it was for.

Even the pooling, he thought, the clerks and the harbors and the corrections poured together each season, all of it made the same trade at a larger size. Every harbor's misses stirred into every other's, so that no machine would ever come to belong to its own port. Broader every year, and no one's, ever.

It was not even the Broker's, he thought, not in the way that counted. The Broker would rent it out and gather it in and it would serve his ledger faithfully. But a ledger is not a pair of ears. A ledger cannot lose the top notes. There was no wound anywhere under that rosewood, no particular life the whole apparatus leaned toward, and that was not a flaw a clerk could correct, because it was not a flaw. It was an absence, and absences do not show up on slates.

He passed the peddler's shuttered stall, where in summer a man sold spectacles out of a basket, ground for no one, sold to anyone. Honest goods. Half the old men of the town read their letters through them, and Anselm owned a pair himself for coarse work. But when his eyes had first gone long, a grinder in the capital had made him a pair to his own two eyes, this one weaker, that one stubborn, and the difference was not that the basket pair failed. The basket pair worked. The difference was that no man who has had glass ground to his own eyes ever goes back to the basket. Both trades would live forever, and only one of them could be taken from a crate.

The Broker's machine would sit in every port by winter, ground for no one, sold to anyone, and it would be good, and he would say so again if asked. And above the harbor there was a plainer cabinet in walnut that was his the way his hands were his. Not because nobody could see it. Because nobody else could need it.

He had hidden nothing. They had taken everything. He had lost nothing.

The workshop was dark except for the lamp Ida had left burning, and the carved rule over the bench caught its light as he came in. On the slate, written while he was still on the stairs, was his own tread, unmistakable, and under it, unasked, a line about the small viol strung that afternoon: the new A going slack at the peg, slow, a morning's worth of slack, in the register he could not hear and the machine had been corrected toward for four springs because he could not hear it.

Small. Particular. His.

He stood in the lamplight and read it twice, an old man and a box, the harbor going dark below, and anyone looking in from the street would have seen nothing worth drawing. That was the answer he owed Ida, near enough. From the street, there had never been anything to see.

> **From the workbench**
>
> Someone can take what you have built. Assume it. Somewhere the likeness is being drawn now, and it will be a good likeness, and the copy, when it comes, will work. Grieve for an afternoon if you must. Then notice what never left the room.
>
> Everything you make is an answer, and a copy carries the answer away without the question. The question is the need you built it for: this reader, this customer, this failing, this one life's Tuesday problem. Whoever holds the question can make the next answer, and the one after that, each fitting closer. Whoever holds only the answer holds a thing that is finished.
>
> So spend your worry where loss is possible. Locks guard the part that was never the point. What cannot be carried off is being the one the work serves, and that needs no guarding, survives every theft, and deepens every time you correct the work against your own life instead of against everyone's at once.
>
> Tonight, once: take the piece of work you most fear seeing imitated, and write beneath it the name of the person it serves. A real name. If the first word that comes is everyone, you have found the theft, and you are the one committing it. Start there. The name is the one part no pencil reaches.
