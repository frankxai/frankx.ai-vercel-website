# Chapter 10 — The Second Workshop

The man from the upriver mill had rowed half the night to be first at the door, and he was not first.

The fishmonger's widow was there before him, wrapped against the cold, and behind him, by the time Anselm drew the bolt, stood a ropewalker from the yards and a boy sent by the harbor master of a town Anselm had never seen. They did not know each other. They had all come for the same thing.

They wanted a box that listened.

The widow no longer trusted her late husband's hull and wanted to hear it going before it went. The mill's main shaft had begun a groan the miller could feel in his teeth but could not place. The ropewalker had heard that the box caught a string a day before it slipped, and reasoned, correctly, that a rope is a string with more riding on it. Word had gone up the coast the way word does, faster than boats, and it had gone out wrong in only one particular: everyone believed there were more machines than one.

Ida kept the waiting book. That morning she added three names, then read him the count without being asked.

"Thirty-one."

Anselm said nothing.

"You build one machine in a season," she said. "Each one you take apart twice before it holds. I have done the sum. The harbor master's boy will be a harbor master himself before we reach the bottom of this page."

"I know the sum."

"The broker would have served all of them by summer." She was not arguing for the broker. She was doing what she did, naming the thing he circled. "You said no, and I understand the no. But the no has a bill, and these people are standing in our doorway holding it."

Anselm looked past her at the four of them waiting in the cold shop, patient as gulls, and felt the truth of it settle where truths settle, low and permanent. He had refused the tower. Nobody had refused the need.

He worked the rest of that day and said almost nothing. Then, for three days, he did not build at all. He walked the harbor. He sat in the loft where his father's old stock lay seasoning in the dark, boards racked forty years ago by hands he still missed, and he stayed up there long enough that Ida twice invented reasons to climb the ladder and check on him.

On the fourth morning he cleared the loft. He carried the good boards down, swept forty years of dust into the harbor wind, and began to build.

Not a machine. Benches.

***

Four of them, plain and heavy, set in a row under the loft's long window. Ida watched a week of the shop's dwindling time go into furniture and held her tongue longer than was natural to her.

Then he started on the strange things, and she couldn't.

He began with the horn. Every listening box needed one, the narrow brass throat that fed it the world one sound at a time, and the whole trick of the horn was an angle. Too wide and the box drowned; too tight and it starved. Anselm had found the true angle years ago the way such things are found, by ruining better wood than he liked to remember, and the angle lived nowhere but in his hands. He could set it in the dark. Nobody else on the coast could set it at all.

So he built a cradle. Maple, two fences and a stop, three days of fussing over a block whose only work would be to hold a piece of brass the way his hands would have held it. When it was done, the work dropped in and the file could meet it at one angle only. A man who had never heard of the angle could not miss it. The cradle did not know anything, the way the machine did not hear anything, and it was right every time.

Ida turned it over, looking for the cleverness, and found only wood.

"What are you making?"

"Fewer decisions," Anselm said. "The good ones, once."

She set the cradle down. She looked at the benches, at the racked patterns he had begun tracing from his own templates, at the empty pegs waiting along the wall, and he watched her see it, the way she saw things, all at once and slightly angry about it.

"You're not going to build the machines," she said. "You're building the thing that builds them."

"The shop downstairs makes instruments," he said. "This one makes the shop."

The days went to jigs and fixtures after that. A gauge for the drum's tension, so that *tight enough* stopped being a feeling and became a mark a stranger could read. A story-stick for the cabinet joints, every measure of the box notched into one ash lath, so that no rule need ever be read twice nor misread once. Patterns in thin cedar for each part, drilled to hang on their own pegs, each peg painted so that an empty peg accused the room. None of it was clever. All of it was judgment, forty years of it, coming out of his hands one decision at a time and setting hard in wood.

The nights went to the book.

He had thought the book would be the easy part. He was wrong by the second sentence. He ruled a fresh ledger, wrote *On seasoning the walnut* at the head of the first page, and then wrote, with the confidence of four decades, *season the boards until they are ready.*

He sat looking at that sentence for a long time.

Ready. He had never once asked what ready meant. His hands knew: some weight, some smell, some way a corner shaving curled. His mouth, invited to say it, stood empty. He went down to the stock in the dark and planed a corner off a board he knew was ready and one he knew was not, and watched what his hands were watching, and caught it at last like a thief taking his own coat: the ready board's shaving curled and held; the green board's shaving broke. He wrote that down. It took him most of an evening to write four sentences he could stand behind, and the whole time some part of him rose against each one, objecting, *not always, not quite, only when.* The book argued with him the way Ida argued with him, and it won the same way, by asking how he knew.

"If I cannot say it," he told her, when she found him at it past midnight, scratching out a page for the third time, "I do not know it."

"You've known it for forty years."

"I've done it for forty years." He turned the ruined page. "It turns out those are different, and I am finding out which parts were which."

The finding out was not kind. He hired the cooper's son for the season, a careful boy with no opinions about instruments, and set him at the first bench with page seven and the drum jig and an instruction to follow the page exactly and ask nothing. The boy followed exactly. The drum he made trembled at a breath and also at nothing, a drum with a nervous disposition, useless. The boy apologized to the floor.

"You did nothing wrong," Anselm said, reading the page again. "The page did."

That became the season's whole shape. Every failure at that bench was a letter addressed to him. The boy could not fail on his own; he had been given no room to. When the work went wrong, it was the jig, the page, the mumble in the master. Anselm had spent his life being the best pair of hands on the coast, and now he stood in a loft learning, one botched drum at a time, how much of what he called knowledge had been habit wearing knowledge's coat. He was sixty-six years old and being corrected daily by a ledger and a boy who smelled of oak staves, and his pride took it the way green wood takes a nail.

Some evenings, planing a fence true for the next fixture, he felt he was at a burial that would not end. Everything leaving his hands. The angle into the maple, the tension into the gauge, the seasons into the pages. He was making a room that would not miss him, and it hurt in the exact place he had kept his worth.

Ida saw. She let three weeks pass before she said it.

"You hate this."

"Yes."

"Then why?"

***

The answer had a smell, and the smell was bread.

The baker died that summer, the one up on the hill street, and it is fair to say the whole town's mornings died with him. His loaf was the best thing the coast made that wasn't a boat. Crust that broke like thin ice, a crumb that stayed sweet to the heel. Fishermen timed their going-out by his chimney. People who moved away described that bread to strangers in far ports the way other people describe a mother.

He had not been secretive. He was a generous man. Three apprentices had stood at his elbow across the years, good workers, welcome to watch everything. But the baker measured in his fists and proofed by smell and judged the oven by holding his arm in the door, and when they asked him how he knew, he told them the truth, which was *until it's right,* and the truth was useless. Nothing he knew had ever left his hands. It had one home its whole life.

Then his hands stopped.

The eldest apprentice took the ovens. He was diligent and honest and the bread he made was fine, and within a month the town understood that fine is a kind of grief. Nothing was wrong with the new loaf. It was simply ordinary, and the extraordinary one was nowhere now, not in the apprentice, not in a page, not in a fist-sized cup or a marked peel. Fifty years of judgment had gone into the ground still inside the only skull that held it.

Anselm bought a loaf from the new man, ate it walking, and finished it out on the mole with his coat snapping in the wind.

He had done this once already. That was the thought that came to him there, plain as the gulls. When the top notes had left his ears, he had not sat guarding the ruin of them. He had built a box to hold the listening outside his own skull, where failure could not reach it. It had felt like invention at the time. It was the same move he was making now in the loft, aimed the other way: not his ears this time, his hands. The baker had been a whole workshop, and the workshop had one door, and the door was him.

He told Ida that night, in the loft, by way of answering her three-week-old question.

"The baker was the best of us," he said. "And he is gone like a tune nobody wrote down."

***

By the turn of the season the loft had a name around the harbor, the second workshop, said half as a joke, and the joke stopped in the week the cooper's son built a horn.

Start to finish, alone. Brass in the cradle, file to the fence, throat checked against the gauge, every step off the pages Anselm had bled all summer. Anselm took it to the window and measured what did not need measuring, because his eye had already told him at the bench.

True. First try.

The boy had not found the angle. The boy did not know there was an angle, not the way a finder knows. The angle had been waiting for him in the maple, where it had lived since the third day of summer, and it would wait there for the next stranger, and the next, patient as a well.

"It took him a week," Ida said quietly, watching the boy sweep his bench with the self-importance of a man who has made one good thing.

"It took forty years and a week," Anselm said.

She smiled at that, and then did not. "The broker couldn't buy this either," she said, working it out as she said it. "He offered for Fable. He never offered for the loft. He doesn't know the loft is the thing."

Anselm hung the horn cradle back on its peg, among the jigs ranked along the wall like a new kind of instrument, the kind that plays makers instead of music.

Downstairs the waiting book still held thirty-one names. But the number had an ending now, and he could read it. Four benches. The pages getting truer by the failure. Hands he had never shaken would build listening boxes he would never touch, in this room, on mornings after he was done with mornings, and the boxes would hold the angle because the angle no longer needed him to hold it.

He put out the lamp and stood a moment in the dark loft, in the smell of maple and cut brass, in the room where his judgment would go on showing up for work without him.

Then he went down to supper.

> **From the workbench**
>
> Somewhere in your week is a problem you have solved before. You solve it again each time it comes, a little differently, from memory and mood, and you call that work. The third solving is a message. It says you have found a good decision and you are spending it over and over instead of keeping it. Keep it. Put the angle in a block. Put the check in a list. Put the reason on a page a stranger could follow, so the decision gets made once, well, and then goes on being made without you.
>
> The page will insult you. What runs easily through your hands will fight the sentence, and the parts you cannot say will turn out to be parts you never understood, only repeated. That sting is not the cost of writing it down. It is the finding out, and there is no other place to find it.
>
> So: the next time you finish a thing you have done three times, stop before the glow fades and write the way of it, plainly, as if for someone who is not you. Then give it to someone who is not you, and watch where they stumble. The stumble marks the spot where your knowledge was still only habit. Fix the page, not the person.
>
> One page, this week. The bench outlives the hands.
