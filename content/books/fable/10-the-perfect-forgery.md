# The Perfect Forgery

He had copied everything, and it was not working, and he could not understand why.

He stood at his bench in the stance she used — weight on the back foot, the left hand always the one that held, the right the one that cut — because he had watched her through her open door for a whole winter and learned even that. His tools were her tools, made by the same smith to the same weight; he had paid the smith double to be certain. His chisels lay in the order she laid hers, coarse to fine, left to right. He began each chair the way she began each chair, with the back legs, though every other chairmaker in the country began with the seat. He had copied the order too, because he had decided that nothing she did was accident, and he was right about that. It was the one thing he was right about.

And his chairs were good. This was the part that undid him. Any judge would have said so — the joints tight, the grain read true, a chair that would hold a heavy man forty years and never once complain. He set them in his window across the lane from hers. They were as good as hers. He knew wood well enough to know that they were as good as hers, and no man lies to himself about wood for long.

The town walked past his door to hers.

He caught the miller's wife one morning, a basket on her arm, as she crossed the lane to the other shop. "The same chair," he said, "for less. Sit in one. Tell me the difference."

She was kind about it, which was worse than scorn. "There's no difference in the chair," she said. "I'm not crossing over for a chair."

So at last he went to the woman herself, because a man can only stand at a copied bench so long before the standing eats him. Her door was open. She did not stop working when his shadow fell across her floor; she had known his step for a season and had been waiting for it, the way you wait for a nail you have already heard begin to loosen.

"I have your bench," he said. "Your tools. Your hands, near enough — I have watched them till I see them when I sleep. I make your chair, joint for joint. Tell me what I'm missing, and I'll pay you for the telling."

She set down the gouge. "What did the last man ask you for?"

"A chair."

"And what did you make him?"

"The one I'd just finished. It was good. The one before it had sold, and this one was as good, so —"

"So you gave him that one." She nodded, not unkindly. "He came to me first, you know, before he crossed to your window for the cheaper price. He'd buried his father in the spring. He wanted a chair to sit in where his father used to sit, and he could not bear a chair that looked like grief, so I made him one that looked like an ordinary morning. Low arms. He rests his hands loose, like this." She showed him, palms down on nothing. "Your good chair has high arms. It is a better chair than the one I would have made him. It was the wrong chair for that man, and he knew it the moment he sat, though he could not have told you why, and he paid you anyway, and he will not come back."

"Then give me the questions," he said, and there was something bare in it now. "You ask them something, before you cut. Give me the questions and I'll ask them too."

She looked at him a long moment, and what was in her face was close to sorrow.

"You think I keep a list," she said. "You think if I read you the list you'd have the trick of it. But I don't ask *him* the questions. I ask *myself* the questions, standing here, with him in the room — I find I want to know a thing about the man that I don't yet know, and the wanting is what bends the chair toward him. That's all it is. There's no list. You can hold my chisel in my order and start with the back legs until the day you die. You'll make a good chair every time. You will never make his chair, because you don't want to know the thing about him." She picked the gouge back up. "You only ever wanted to know the thing about me."

He had no answer. There wasn't one. He went back across the lane.

He was not a wicked man, and nothing punished him. His chairs sold, in their steady way, to the many people who want a good chair and nothing more than that, which is most people, and he did not starve. But some evenings he would stand at his copied bench in her borrowed stance, her tools in her order under his hands, and look through his own doorway at hers. And he would see her there in the low light — not cutting, not joining, not doing any of the thousand things he had learned by heart. Just standing with someone. Head tilted. Asking.

That was the one motion he had never been able to copy, because it did not begin in the hands. He had taken the whole of what the work *was*, down to the burn on the bench and the lean of her shoulders, and not one grain of what the work was *for* — and the for of it had never once been in the shop. It lived in the single place his patient, perfect hands could not reach: the inside of the only person on the lane who had ever wanted to know.

---

*Fable said the moral was that a method can be stolen and will make an honest chair, but the question under a person's work is not a thing and so cannot be lifted, because it isn't knowledge — it's hunger, and hunger has to be someone's. I said that was too gentle on the woman and too hard on the man; he only wanted to become her, and no thief is punished worse than getting his wish. We went back and forth and did not land it. But I keep the woman's part now, when I sit down to work: not "what did she do," which I can copy, but "what was she hungry for," which I have to answer myself or make nothing at all. That, at least, no bench can hand me.*
