# Are They Dangerous?

The official had come three days by bad road to ask one question, and the old woman would not give him the one word he had come for.

"Yes or no," he said again. He had asked it several ways now — first grand, then reasonable, and now merely tired. "The capital does not want a speech. It wants a word small enough to carry home. Are they dangerous."

She was mending a net across her knees and did not look up. Behind her, through the open door, he could see the town he had been sent to judge: a listening box on nearly every roof now, little slatted things the size of a loaf, each one turned toward the water. In the lane below, a mill ground without a miller, its clever gears reading the wind and reefing their own sails when it freshened. He had counted a dozen such contrivances before he reached her door. The capital had heard rumors of a hundred.

"There are two carriages in the capital," he said, "and they will not stop shouting at each other. One says these things will make every man rich and every man idle, that within a lifetime no one will lift a hand or hold a thought, and we will all grow soft as risen bread and just as witless. The other says worse — that a people who let a box do their listening will forget how, that we will become a herd that goes where the bell sends it, cattle with the memory of having once been men. One carriage wants them blessed. The other wants them burned in the square. Both sent me to you, because you made the first one, and both will accept your word and no one else's." He spread his hands. "So. Bless or burn. Which."

The old woman knotted off her mending and bit the twine.

"Are they dangerous," she said, tasting it. "Yes."

He had his hand half to his satchel for the writing-board before she finished.

"Dangerous like harbors," she said. "Like presses. Like fire."

The hand stopped.

"A harbor drowns men every year it stands," she said. "Reach into the tally-house and you will find the count, and it is not small — the boy off the mole, the crew that misjudged the bar, the fool who swam after his hat. Every year the harbor takes someone. And every soul in this town, and yours, and the capital's, eats bread this winter because somewhere a harbor let a boat come home full. You do not bless a harbor. You do not burn one. There is no word for a harbor. There is a seawall, and a bar-light, and a man who keeps the depth-marks painted, and a book of every wreck so the next fool learns where the next fool drowns."

"Then your answer is that there is no answer," said the official, and heard the bitterness in it. "I cannot carry that three days on a bad road."

"I did not say there is no answer." She set the net aside and stood, slower than she used to, and went to a shelf where a book lay so heavy it had bowed the plank beneath it. She carried it to the table in both arms and let it down with a sound like a door. "I said there is no *word*. There is this."

He looked at the book. It was not fine. The boards were water-stained and the spine had been resewn more than once by more than one hand.

"Open it anywhere," she said.

He did. The page was two columns in the same brown ink, and he saw at once that it was not a book of praises. On the left of one leaf: *In the second dry summer, the box on the granary roof caught the change in the sound of the well-water and named the failing spring a month before the well ran shy. We dug the new well in time. No one thirsted.* And directly beneath it, no new page, no separate book, the same ink, the same hand: *In the same summer, the box on the mole heard a storm in the sound of the swell and we trusted it over old Perrin, who is a pilot and said the swell was lying. We kept the boats in. The storm went south of us. Three days' catch lost, and Perrin shamed for nothing, and Perrin was right and the box was wrong, and we had taught ourselves to believe the box because it had been right eleven times. That is our fault and it is written here in our hand.*

He turned the leaf. It went on like that. A child found feverish in the night because a listening box by her cot had caught the wrong sound in her breathing and woken her mother — written plain. And two leaves on, in the same ink: *We lost the Kestrel and four men. The box on the mole had learned the sea from every storm it had ever heard, and it had never heard a storm come from the land side in winter, because none had come in the years it listened, so when one came it said fair, and we believed it, because it had never been wrong about a thing it had heard before. It cannot warn of a sea it has never heard. We knew this. We forgot it because forgetting was easier. Four men.* And below that, not struck out, not softened: *Whose fault. Ours. We made the box. We chose to trust it past the edge of what it had learned. The sea is not to blame and the box is not to blame. Read this before you trust the next one.*

The official read a long time. The old woman let him.

"You keep both," he said at last. "The saving and the killing. In the one book. In the one ink."

"In the one ink," she said, "or it is worth nothing. A book of only the harvests is a song, and you sing it at a blessing. A book of only the drownings is a warning, and you nail it to the door before a burning. We keep neither. We keep the whole account, and the town reads it before it trusts a new box, and adds to it after, and where the fault was ours we write *ours*, because a fault you hang on the tool is a fault you will make again." She laid her hand flat on the open page. "That is how we hold the fire. Not by loving it and not by dousing it. By keeping the book of what it cooked and what it burned, and reading the book before we strike the next light."

"And your verdict," he said. "For the two carriages."

"There is no verdict." She said it gently, as you tell a child a hard true thing. "There is only the keeping. A verdict is a feeling you have once, in a square, with a torch or a garland in your hand, and then you go home and forget you had it. The keeping is a book you sit down to every time, forever, before you act, and it does not care how you felt in the square." She turned the great book back to its first leaf and set it before him, and put the pen beside it, and drew up the second stool.

"You came for a word to carry home," she said. "There is no word. But the road is three days, and you can read while you wait for the horse to rest. Start at the beginning. That is where we started."

And the official from the capital, who had come to bless or to burn, sat down at the table of the oldest maker in the coast town, and drew the lamp a little closer, and slowly, from the first page, in the falling light, began to read.
