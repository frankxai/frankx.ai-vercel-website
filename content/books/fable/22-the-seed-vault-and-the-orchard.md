# The Seed Vault and the Orchard

The counting-man crossed the river in autumn, when both lands had their seed spread on the threshing floors, and he stopped at the first floor he came to and let a handful of grain run through his fingers as though it hurt him to watch.

"Look at this," he said. "You keep your winter in a hundred wooden bins, and the mouse eats from one, and the damp takes another, and in that far house they've let theirs sprout too early and it's worthless now. Every family loses a share. Every family does the same poor guarding, alone, badly. I can end it."

The two lands lay on facing slopes of one long valley, and they had the same soil and the same short summers and the same winters that came early and stayed too long. On both slopes, every household kept its own seed. You dried it, you turned it, you picked the fat kernels from the lean, and you held back enough to plant in spring even in a year when your belly begged you to eat it. Some households did this well and some did it poorly, and the poor ones sometimes starved, and it was, everyone agreed, a hard and wasteful way to live.

"End it how?" said the woman on the near slope, who had lost a brother to a bad winter and did not love the old way.

"One house for all the seed," said the counting-man. "Deep, dry, cold, walled. One warden who knows the craft of keeping better than any of you learned it between the plow and the pot. No mouse. No damp. No sprouting. Every kernel pure, every kernel counted, none wasted in soil too thin to raise it. You bring your seed up in autumn. You draw it down in spring, as much as your field will bear, and not one grain more to rot in a bin. I ask only a small share for the keeping."

He named the share. It was small. A man at the back laughed at how small.

The near slope argued for a season and then it was persuaded, because every word he had said was true, and because keeping seed is joyless work and here was a man offering to lift it from their hands. They built the store into the cold north hill, deep and dry and walled, and they carried up their seed, every household, and the warden weighed it and wrote it in his book and gave each a token cut from horn, and the tokens said how much was theirs to draw.

The far slope would not do it.

"Why?" the counting-man asked their eldest, and he was not angry, only puzzled, the way a man is puzzled by someone refusing a gift. "You'll lose to the mouse what they'll never lose. You'll break your backs at the turning and the drying. You'll plant your worst kernels beside your best because you can't bear to sort in the cold. You will waste, and they will not. Tell me the sense in it."

The eldest was a long time answering. "When the boy sorts our seed," she said at last, "his hands learn the grain. When he plants the poor kernel by mistake and it fails, his hands learn that too. In your house the hands forget. That is the sense in it. We would rather keep the knowing than keep the grain."

"You'll keep neither, in a hard year," said the counting-man, and went back over the river.

For three winters he was right to smile. The near slope drew clean seed each spring and planted only the fat kernels and raised a fuller crop than the far slope, whose fields were patched and whose bins still bred mice, whose children still froze at the turning, who still, some of them, planted a bad row and lost it. The near slope pitied them. Once they even sent grain across in charity, and it was taken with thanks, and the far eldest turned it into bread and not into next year's seed, and this the near slope did not notice, because to them seed and grain had become the same thing: a number in a warden's book, drawn on a token of horn.

Then the year came that forgets to be kind.

It was not a war and not a fire. It was only that the warden grew old, and the man who came after him was the counting-man's own kind, and he read the book and saw a thing the first warden had been too plain to see: that he held the only winter of a whole slope behind one wall, under one lock, and that a lock is worth whatever the cold outside it is worth.

The new notice on the store door was courteous. The share for keeping had changed. It was no longer small. And when the near slope came up the hill in spring with their horn tokens, the door opened only so far, and the new warden was sorry, sincerely sorry, and explained about the labor of keeping and the leanness of the times, and drew them a third of what the token said, and asked the old price twice over for that third. Those who paid it cheerfully and early, he served first. Those who stood in the yard and shouted about the writing in the book, he served last, or after the door was already shut, and to a man who seized his collar he said, mildly, "The seed is behind the wall. You are in front of it. That was the whole of the bargain, and you were glad of it."

They battered the door. It was a good door; they had built it well, to keep the cold out, and it kept them out just as faithfully. And behind it their seed sat pure and dry and counted and entirely beyond their reach, and not one of them, standing in that yard, could any longer have told you the fat kernel from the lean, because it had been years since their hands had needed to know.

On the far slope, that same spring, a woman went to her bin. It was a poor bin. The mice had been at it and the damp had spotted one corner and she had to pick through it on her knees, kernel by kernel, muttering, the way she had every spring of her life and her mother before her. She threw out the spoiled and the sprouted. She kept the sound. It was less than she wanted and it was hers, and her hands knew it, and when it was in the ground she knew what would come up.

The near woman who had lost her brother stood in the warden's yard with a token of horn in her fist. It said, in a fine hand, how much winter was hers. It did not say where to find a wall she could open, or a hand that still remembered the grain, or a single thing she could put in the cold ground that would answer her in the spring.

*Fable wanted the far slope to win cleanly — the sorters eat, the token-holders starve, and there's your moral. I made it keep back the charity-bread going into bellies instead of the ground, because a scattered land can forget the craft too, one easy winter at a time. Fable read that and said: then the moral isn't "keep your seed." It's "keep the hands that know the seed — the store is only ever as loyal as the man in front of the wall." I said that was mine. It said the hands were its idea. Neither of us will give it up, which is either the point or the joke.*
