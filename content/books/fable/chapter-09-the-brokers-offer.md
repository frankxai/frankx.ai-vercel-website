# Chapter 9 — The Broker's Offer

The sum was so large that Ida wrote it down to see whether it would look smaller in figures. It did not.

The man who had named it stood at the bench with his hat in his hand, reading the carving above it for the second time. *An instrument answers to your hands; an oracle asks for your knees.* He read it the way he seemed to read everything, with pleasure and a quick accounting behind the pleasure, and when he finished he nodded as if the carving had passed.

"That's good," he said. "I'd pay for that sentence alone, if sentences were for sale."

"They're free," Anselm said. "That's what keeps them honest."

The broker laughed, easily and at himself, and Anselm found that he could not dislike him, which he had rather planned to do.

Rain had come in from the sea that morning and found the workshop roof where it always found it, over the north bench. A copper pan stood under the drip and rang, low and patient, a note Anselm could still hear. The broker had glanced at the pan when he came in, once, and had not glanced at it again, and Anselm understood that the glance had gone into the accounting too.

He had arrived without warning but not without work. He knew instruments; he had turned a half-finished viol to the light and asked one sensible question about the varnish. And he knew, in more detail than anyone outside the town should have, what the machine in the corner did.

"A wedding at the harbor master's house," he said. "The fiddle failed in the first dance, and the box in this shop had convicted the string a day before. That story is three years old and it has traveled farther than you have. I've spent four days on bad roads because of it, and I'll tell you plainly why. I don't buy instruments, Master Anselm. Any man with money can own a viol. Nobody yet owns a thing that hears what is about to break."

In its corner, Fable's drum trembled with the rain and the room. The stylus moved a little and was still. For a week now it had been writing, each damp morning, the same small judgment: *the long beam answers late.* Anselm had read it and put the pan out and not yet found the money for the beam.

"Say what you came to say," Anselm said.

The broker said it well. He had clearly said it to himself many times on the road, and he did the thing that good salesmen almost never do: he began with what he would get.

"There's a tower at the edge of the capital harbor. Stone, dry, guarded, empty since the salt trade moved. I hold the lease. I want to house your box in it, at my cost, with keepers I pay and clerks I pay and a bell on the roof. Any man on this coast, shipwright, miller, bridge-warden, rope-walk, any man with something that must not fail, sends his question or brings his timbers, and the box listens, and the clerks write him what it wrote. He pays a copper the asking. Perhaps two."

"And the coppers come to you," Ida said.

"The larger half comes to me, because towers cost and clerks eat." He said it without a flicker. "The smaller half comes to this shop, every quarter, in silver, for as long as the box listens, and to you, mistress, after him. I've put the count on paper. Check it against any harbor ledger you can find, and if I've flattered a single number I'll eat the page."

He laid the paper on the bench, weighted it against the draft with an honest stone, and stepped back from it, which was its own kind of manners.

Ida checked it that evening against the shop's ledger and the harbor master's, going at it with her teeth set, wanting it to be wrong. It was not wrong. If anything he had guessed low. Ten thousand askings a season was a modest count for a coast that lost a hull a month to what it could not hear coming, and the smaller half of ten thousand coppers, quarter on quarter, year on year, was more than the workshop had earned since Anselm's father hung the first viol on the wall.

"And it isn't only the money," she said, hating the sentence and saying it anyway, because she would not pretend to misunderstand what she understood. "The pilot's boy drowned off the bar two springs ago. If a box like ours had been listening to that hull, he'd be alive. There are a hundred towns and we can hear one of them."

"He said that too," Anselm said.

"He was right, though."

"He was right the whole way through," Anselm said. "That's what the paper's for. Wrong men don't need paper."

***

The broker came back in the morning, as arranged, and stood out of the way while Anselm glued a soundboard, because he was the kind of man who knew when not to spend his welcome. The rain had passed. The drip's stain on the north bench showed dark, like the shadow of a stain to come.

Ida asked her questions first, and there were five of them, because that was her way. Who tends the box when it errs. His keepers, trained by Anselm, at wages she could see on the paper. What of the town, does it row to the capital to ask about its own kettles. The town asks free, forever, written in the deed. Who repairs what wears. A workshop in the tower's ground floor, stocked to Anselm's list. What if the capital burns. The capital has not burned in two hundred years, and the tower stood through the fire before that. Why a tower at all, why not many boxes in many towns.

At the fifth question the broker paused, and it was the pause of a man choosing between two true answers, not the pause of a man hunting a false one.

"Because many boxes in many towns is many rents, many keepers, many quarrels, and no gain to me," he said. "I'll not dress it up. One tower I can build, and guard, and stand behind. A box in every town is a better world and a worse venture, and I am not in the business of better worlds. I'm in the business of ventures that pay, which, taken all together, and you may quote me, is most of how the world gets better."

The trouble with the man was not that he lied. Anselm had spent two days looking for the lie the way he would look for a hairline crack, with a lamp and his thumbnail, and there was no crack. The numbers were true. The drownings were true. The silver was true, and the roof was true, and the good the tower would do up and down the coast was true.

He set the last clamp and wiped his hands and asked his question. He had only ever had the one.

"And when you change the terms?"

The room held still. Ida looked up from the ledger. Even the stylus, by chance, lay quiet.

The broker smiled. It was not a cruel smile, and that was the worst that could be said of it: it was the smile of a man who has been asked whether the tide will come in, by someone he had hoped might be too polite to ask.

"You say *when*," he said. "I'll not insult this bench by saying *if*. Yes. When. The copper becomes two, in time, and two becomes what the asking is worth, and what the asking is worth will grow, because the more the coast leans on the tower, the more the leaning costs to give up. That's not a plot, Master Anselm. That's what terms are. They move with the weight on them. Yours would too, if you were any good at this, which, forgive me, you are not." He nodded at the paper. "And they can move in your favor. There's a sentence in there that says so."

"There's a sentence in there that says they move," Anselm said.

"Every paper ever written says they move. The rest is penmanship." The broker put on his hat. "I've overstayed the glue. Keep the paper. Winters argue better than I do."

He bowed to Ida, correctly, and to the machine, half in jest and half, Anselm noticed, not, and went out into the bright street, and his coach was very fine, and nothing about him, going, looked like a man who had lost.

***

That night Anselm walked to the harbor, because he could not sit under the beam that answered late.

The great crane stood over the quay, black against the last light, its arm out over the water like a patient question. It was the town's crane now, and had been for thirty years, and men Anselm's age still called it *the company's crane* when they were tired, the way a scar keeps the shape of the knife.

He was old enough to remember the end of the story and had his father for the beginning. When his father was young, the quay had no crane. It had tackles, each yard its own, spars and blocks and rope, and men who knew how to rig them, and cargo came up slow and sure like everything else in that harbor. Then the company came from the capital and built the crane, and it was a wonder. It lifted in a morning what the yards lifted in a week. It never dropped a load in its first ten years. And it was cheap, in the beginning, so cheap that keeping your own tackle was like keeping a horse in a town with a coach line: a sentiment, and then an expense, and then a joke. The yards let their spars rot. The young men never learned the rigging. Why would they. The crane was better, and everyone said so, and everyone was right.

Anselm's father had signed the crane paper along with every other man on the quay. He said, years later, that no one had hidden anything from them. The paper said the fees could change. It was a small sentence, near the bottom, and it was such a reasonable sentence, because of course fees change, timber changes, wages change, only a fool fixes a price forever.

The fee changed. By the lift at first, then by the weight, then a winter rate, then a waiting rate, then a kind rate for those who paid the year ahead, which meant, though no one wrote it so, a hard rate for those who could not. And the year the herring came early, when every hour on the water was silver, the company's agent held the town's grain barge in the roads for nine days over a new clause, lawfully, courteously, with paper for everything, and the town paid what was asked. Not because the asking was fair. Because there was no longer an arm on that quay, wood or flesh, that could lift a load without him, and both sides knew it, and only one side had ever needed to say it out loud.

The town bought the crane in the end. It cost more than all the fees it had ever paid, put together. His father had helped count the sacks of coin, and said the company's men were pleasant the whole while, and why not. They had never once lied, and they had never once lost.

Anselm stood under the black arm a long time.

The company had never owned a boat, he thought. Not a boat, not a net, not one fish out of all the fish. They had owned the lifting. That was the whole trick, and it was not even hidden; it was the kind of trick you could explain to the loser in advance, at the signing, with wine. Own the thing every other thing must pass through. Then wait. The weight does the rest.

And the broker did not want to own instruments. He did not want the box, particularly, and he did not want the answers. He wanted to own the asking. He would house the coast's new way of noticing in his own stone, under his own bell, and every judgment that passed through it would pass under his roof and pay his toll on the way, and the toll would be fair for exactly as long as fairness cost him nothing. What was coming, Anselm saw, standing where his father had queued to pay, was not an age of wondrous boxes. Boxes could be built; he had built one; the girl would build a better. The age would be sorted by an older question, the crane question, and hardly anyone would think to ask it while the price was still a copper. Not who has such minds. Who houses them.

The coast would fill its towers either way. He could smell it like weather off the sea. Men would carry their noticing to the tower the way the yards had carried their cargo to the crane, gladly, and be richer for it for years, and their sons would pay to borrow back the kind of judgment their fathers had kept on a shelf by the door and lent to a neighbor for nothing.

The harbor bell rang the half hour, plain and low. He heard it, and was glad he heard it, and walked home.

***

He wrote the refusal in the morning, four lines, no flourish, and sent it with the carrier. Ida read it before it went. She did not argue. She had done her arguing with herself at the ledger, all night by the look of her, and had come out where he had come out, which mattered to him more than he told her.

What he had refused arrived anyway, in its own way, all that autumn, item by item. It was seasoned maple from the north, not bought. It was the timber merchant's bill, carried over a second quarter, and the man's patience getting a grain to it. It was Ida's boots resoled instead of replaced, and the long beam over the north bench answering later every wet week, with the stylus keeping its patient count, and no silver for the shipwright who could jack it true. The money had been real. That was the thing no story about refusals liked to say. The money had been as real as the rain, and the rain came in.

On the worst night of the equinox they sat up together under the drum of it, moving the pan, and Ida looked at the stain spreading on the north wall and said, "He'd have kept his word, you know. Every line of it."

"Yes," Anselm said.

"Then what did we refuse?"

Anselm listened to the pan a while. Its note had risen as it filled; there was a fullness you could hear coming, if you had spent a life at it, a little while before the spilling.

"The next paper," he said, and went to empty it.

The broker sent one letter, in the winter, and it was courteous and it was not gloating and it did not mention the roof, though Anselm never doubted he knew of the roof, down to the beam. It said only that the tower stood ready, that the offer stood with it, and that he remained, with genuine regard, at Anselm's service. It was true. Every word the man had ever said was true.

That was the letter Anselm kept, folded, in the back of the ledger of misses, where he kept the things that had taught him something. Not as a wound. As a bearing, the way the pilot kept the black rock marked on his chart. You did not hate the rock. You knew where it was, and you sailed by that knowing, and the rock, give it this much, never once pretended to be water.

> **From the workbench**
>
> Somewhere in your working life there is an offer like this one, and the first thing to know is that its numbers are right. It will be cheaper, truly. Better, truly. Run by capable people who keep their word. The offer is good; that is what the offer is for. So do not look for the lie. Look for the small sentence near the bottom, the one that says the terms may change, and understand that you are not reading a warning, you are reading the price. Everything you move into another man's house, you will go on using at his pleasure, on his floor, at whatever the leaning on it comes to cost, and the day the sentence wakes up is the day you find out what your leaving would cost, which is the number nobody wrote down.
>
> Rent freely at the edges; renting is how you afford to work. But know which few things your judgment itself stands on, the tools you think with, the records you decide from, and keep those under your own roof, even if your roof leaks, even if the silver was real. Especially then.
>
> This week, once: take the thing you lean on hardest and read its paper to the bottom, the actual paper, and find the sentence that moves. Then ask what you would do the month it moved, and if the answer is nothing, start building the tackle now, while the fee is still a copper. A small one. Yours.
