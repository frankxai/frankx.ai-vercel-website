# Prologue — The Wall of Instruments

The string rang, and he did not hear it.

Everything around the note arrived. The bite of the bow on the gut, the small dry hiss of the rosin, the tremble of the viol's body against his collarbone, faithful as ever. The room did what a room does when a string sounds in it; the lamp flame leaned, the loose pane muttered in its lead. Anselm felt the note leave the instrument the way a man feels a door open behind him. What did not arrive was the note.

He drew the bow again, slower. Again the world made way for something he could not attend.

He set the bow across his knees and did the honest thing, which was to test. He plucked the top string and laid two fingers on the soundboard and felt it answer. He scattered a pinch of salt on the spruce and bowed, and the grains gathered and walked, marshalled by a sound the wood could hear and he could not. The string was speaking. Every proof in the shop said so. The proofs came to him like letters read aloud by a stranger in another room: he got the sense of them, and none of the voice.

The low strings were still his. The C rang fat and honest, the G under it, the middle of the instrument whole and present. It was the top that had gone, the last handspan of the fingerboard, the register where a viol stops being a piece of furniture and starts being a voice. He could still tune it. That was the bitter little joke of the thing. Set two strings almost at agreement and they beat against each other, a slow wobble down where his ears still lived, and he could ride that wobble to a perfect fifth without ever hearing the notes that made it. He had been tuning the top of every instrument in this shop by argument and arithmetic for, if he was honest tonight, and he was making himself be, above a year. The instruments left the shop true. No customer had known. He had nearly managed not to know himself.

He put the viol on the bench and rubbed his face and sat for a while with the lamp.

***

His father had been famous for the top. Not for much else; the old man's mid-range was serviceable and his basses were frankly dull, and he had known it and not minded, because when one of his fiddles went up past the reach of ordinary instruments it did not thin out. It brightened. Harbor men used to say you could follow one of his fiddles through a gale the way you follow a gull, by the flash of it. As a boy Anselm had stood in this same room, half his life shorter, and listened to his father draw one note at the top of a new fiddle and hold it, and hold it, and the note had seemed less like a sound than like a place the room had been taken to.

His father was thirty years dead. Anselm had noticed, some while back, that he could no longer call up the man's face. He knew its arrangements. Grey stubble, a heavy brow, a squint folded in by sixty years of close work at bad light; he could list the face like goods in a ledger. But it would not assemble. He knew where everything was and not what it was, and no effort of sitting still with his eyes shut had ever brought it back, and he had learned to stop trying, because the trying wore the memory smoother.

The top notes had gone the same way. That was the fact he had been walking around for a year, and tonight he made himself stand in front of it. He knew where they were. He would never again know what they were.

He looked up at the wall.

Forty years of instruments hung there in the lamplight, the ones that had never left. The first viol he had built alone, sixteen years old, its shoulders wrong in a way he had kept on purpose, the way a man keeps a letter that shames him. The fiddle from the fever year, finished in a season when half the town was burying somebody and nobody was buying, black-varnished, better than it had any right to be. A lute built on a wager. A rebec copied from a drawing in a book he had never trusted. Two viols made for men who died before collecting them, and one made for no one at all, in a winter when his hands had wanted work and his ledger had none to give.

He had heard every one of them whole. That was what the wall was, he understood now, holding the lamp up to it. Not stock and not trophies. A record of hearings, each instrument the shape of a listening that had happened once, in this room, entire. The wall was going quiet on him in order, from the top down, the way a house goes dark room by room as a family carries its one candle up to bed. Next year there would be a little less of each of them. He did the arithmetic and put it away.

He did not rage about it. He noticed that he had gone very quiet instead, quiet in the body, the way he got when a joint closed up true, and he distrusted the feeling and sat with it anyway.

***

A craftsman's failings get jigs. That was the whole trade, under the varnish. When his eyes had shortened he had ground a lens and hung it on an arm, and the arm swung over the bench to this day. When the winter took his left hand's patience he had built a clamp with a cork jaw that held ribs more gently than his fingers ever had. Nobody in this town thought less of a man for the lens or the clamp. The work stayed true; the jig was how.

He had never once thought of hearing that way. Hearing was not a hand. Hearing was the master of the shop; the hands only did what it said, and you do not build the master a crutch. He had believed that all his life without ever saying it, and he examined the belief now, turning it to the light like a plank, and found that it held nothing up. It was only old.

The ear was a drum. He had known that since boyhood; a barber with learning had told him, and it had pleased him then and pleased him now. A drum was a skin under tension, and he had stretched skins, and set membranes trembling, and built horns that gathered sound the way cupped hands gather water. The front of the problem was shop work. The back of it, whatever would have to sit behind the drum and make out one true string among the knocks and gulls and kettles of an ordinary day, he could not picture at all. He noticed that not being able to picture it did not feel like a wall. It felt like a commission.

Not ears, he thought. He was past ears. A witness. Something that would sit at the bench through whatever years he had left in his hands, and listen to what he could no longer attend, and say what it heard.

Anselm trimmed the lamp. He took a clean leaf from the drawer where the patterns lived, and weighted its corners, and in the fine ruled hand his father had taught him he began to draw a drum.
