# Chapter 14 — What the Machine Could Not Hold

The news came up the stairs on a Thursday, which was wrong, because Thursday was when Tomas came up them himself.

It came instead as his grandson, sixteen and winded, who stood in the doorway with his cap in both hands and could not say it and did not need to. Anselm set down the plane. He heard his own voice, from somewhere a little behind himself, ask whether it had been quick, and the boy said his grandfather had been at his desk in the chandlery with the day's page open in front of him, the last figure entered and not yet totted, and so it had been quick, quicker than the sum.

"Sit down," Anselm said, because the boy did not look ready for the stairs again, and because there was a chair by the window that Tomas had refused, standing, for forty years.

Forty years of Thursdays. He came at four, climbed with a step there was no mistaking, refused the chair the way other men refuse an insult, and drank Anselm's coffee standing at the window with his eyes on the harbor. He said the coffee was terrible. He said it every Thursday, with the satisfaction of a man confirming a measurement, and held out the cup for more.

He smelled of his trade, tar and hemp and the cold that lives in stone warehouses, and he brought the smell up the stairs with him, and the workshop kept it until evening, so that Thursday was the one day of the week that smelled of anything besides glue and shavings.

They had one argument and had kept it their whole lives, the way some men keep a boat they never sail. Tomas held that the harbor bell rang flat. Anselm held that the bell was true and the water bent the sound on its way in. Every few years one of them found new evidence and presented it, and the other considered it seriously and rejected it. They had settled nothing in forty years and had never once tried to.

And under the coffee and the argument, never mentioned by either of them, there was this. When Anselm was twenty-three, with his father half a year in the ground and a first viol that nobody in the town would risk money on, it was Tomas who had bought it. Full price. The price of a made name, paid to a boy with sawdust in his hair, by a chandler who then hung the viol in his parlor and brought it down on feast days to play it worse than any man on that coast has ever played anything. Anselm had understood the arithmetic of that purchase perhaps ten years later, all at once, in the middle of an ordinary afternoon, and had gone hot in the face at his own bench. It was never spoken of. There was no way to speak of it that Tomas would have allowed.

Now the boy sat in the refused chair and Anselm stood at the window where Tomas stood, and below them the harbor went on arranging itself, hulls and gulls and water, as if nothing had happened to it. Nothing had.

The boy drank a cup of the terrible coffee, because Anselm made it for him and stood over him while he did, and then went back down to his mother. All afternoon the news moved through the town below the window the way news moves through a town, one door at a time. Anselm watched people stop one another in the street and stand a moment with their heads bent close, and he knew each time what had been said by what the bodies did after.

He sent the apprentices home at midday and worked alone, or stood at the bench holding tools without working, which from the street looks the same.

At dusk, Fable spoke at his ear. Nobody had asked it anything.

Its voice was the one it used for weather. "I have kept a great deal of Tomas," it said. "Six years of Thursdays. His step on the stairs. The cup set down on the sill, and the argument about the bell, both sides of it. I have his laugh whole, from more than a hundred evenings. I can make it again now, and it would be right."

All of that was true. The machine had sat in this room through six years of those afternoons, its drum trembling to everything, keeping what surprised it, and a laugh like Tomas's laugh surprises any room it happens in. It would have it in every weather. It could make it again tonight, here, in the room where the laugh had lived, and it would be right. Not close. Right.

Anselm stood with both hands on the bench. He wanted it. He wanted it so plainly that his own head had already begun, the way forty years of expecting a thing writes it in for you; somewhere under his breastbone the laugh was already half sounding, the way the stylus, years ago, had once rung a bell that never rang.

His hand went out and rested on the cabinet, on the walnut he had rubbed with oil eleven autumns running.

"Not tonight," he said.

He turned the small crank that let the drum go slack, and fitted the cover over the horn, gently, the way he covered the good viols at night. Then he took his coat off the peg and went down the stairs and out along the seawall.

***

The tide was coming in over the flats, in the dark, without hurry.

He sat where he had sat on many nights, on the flat stone at the seawall's end, and let it come. Somewhere behind him, on a shelf in the workshop, lay a slate that had filled itself edge to edge through one whole night, years ago, and had held, by morning, the way the sea behaves and not one spoonful of the sea.

The cold came up through the stone. The water made its sounds.

He was not angry at the machine. He went over himself for anger the way he would go over a soundboard for a crack he suspected, slowly and in order, and found none, and was left with the harder work of saying what it was he had turned off, since it had not been anger, and it had not quite been grief either, or not only.

The machine had offered him exactly what it had. And what it had was everything about Tomas that repeated. The step, because steps repeat. The Thursday, because Thursdays repeat. The laugh, because a laugh is a habit the body keeps, and habits repeat. Everything in the man that made a shape more than once, the drum had felt and the machine had kept, and none of it was less than true.

What it did not have, what in six years of Thursdays it had never once felt, was the next thing Tomas would have said. There was no next thing now. That was what the word dead meant, and he had reached the age of knowing many dead and had somehow never put it to himself that way before. The argument about the bell would not be added to. It was finished. Not settled, finished, and the two had nothing to do with each other.

The machine could give him the laugh again. It could not give him the one thing the laugh had been for, which was that it answered. It had always answered something: a remark, a mishap, a silence of exactly the wrong length. The laugh the machine made tonight would answer nothing. It would be the sound with the man taken out of it, and the man was the part he wanted, and the man had never gone into the drum at all. Only the ways of him had.

He tried the thought from the other side, to be fair to it, the way he gave every idea its trial. Say he had nodded. Say the laugh had come out into the room, whole and right. He would have wanted it again. He knew himself well enough to know that. Again, and then again, and each time it would answer nothing, and he would be a man knocking at a door painted on a wall, where there had once been a house.

Further out, the tide turned some stones over.

Years ago, watching the machine fail to hold this water, he had thought he was learning something about the machine. He had it the other way around now. The slate that could not hold the sea and the drum that could not hold Tomas had not fallen short of their work. They held the smaller shape of things, and held it well, and the failure he had once gone looking for was not a failure. Some things do not have a smaller shape. The sea was not smaller than itself. Tomas was not smaller than himself. The machine had never been wrong about either of them. It had held all of each that could be carried, and the rest had stayed where the rest of a thing stays, which is in the thing.

He sat until the cold got into his knees, and a while past that, and then walked home along the seawall with the tide loud on his left hand.

***

They buried Tomas on a gray Tuesday with most of the town standing in the wind. The harbor bell rang the hour while the men were filling the grave. Anselm caught himself listening hard, and could not tell whether it was flat, and the one man whose opinion he wanted kept it to himself.

Two days later Tomas's daughter climbed the stairs with a case Anselm knew before she reached the landing. "He wrote it down years ago," she said. "It was to come back to you."

The first viol. The varnish gone amber at the edges, the back worn matte where a chandler's buttons had rested against it on forty years of feast days. Anselm set it on the bench and tuned it, and the old pegs still turned clean. He played a little. It was honest in the middle, shy on top, like its maker at twenty-three. It was a good viol.

It was not Tomas. He had made it with his own hands, and the man had kept it near him for forty years, and it could not give the man back any more than the drum could. He stood a moment with the bow still raised, hearing the room settle. Then he hung the viol on the wall among the forty years of others, and thanked her, and walked her down.

Ida came up in the evening. She had her own machine now, and her own way of being quiet in a room. She looked at the covered horn for a while before she spoke.

"Do you want me to clear what it kept of him?" she said. "Some people would want that."

"No," Anselm said. "Keep it. All of it."

"Will you ever ask for it?"

"I don't know."

She waited for the reason. He was looking past her at the wall, at the instruments nobody played anymore, kept anyway, and after a moment she followed his eyes there, and did not ask again.

The week went on the way weeks insist on going. A soundboard came in cracked from the cold, and he mended it. A student sent up from the coast road played her scales in the corner, badly and then less badly, and he let her be, because Tomas would have found her comic and the room needed that.

On Thursday at four the stairs stayed quiet. Anselm made the coffee anyway, because his hands had started before he could put the question to them, and carried the cup to the window, and drank it there standing, with his eyes on the harbor. Below, the tide was coming in over the flats. Behind him the machine sat covered on its bench with six years of Thursdays inside it, all of it kept, and he was glad that it was there, and he did not take the cover off.

The coffee was terrible. He drank it to the bottom.

> You keep things well; keep them. The line she wrote you, the way he told that story the winter it snowed, the sound of the room on the good night: hold on to every scrap that can be held, and let nobody tell you the scraps are foolish. A day comes when a scrap is what there is, and on that day you will be glad of every one.
>
> Only know what a scrap is. It is the shape a thing made where it touched your keeping. The thing itself was always larger, and stays larger, and no fault of yours, and no cleverness of your tools, will ever fold it small enough to file. That is not a sorrow to be fixed. It is what having the thing was like while you had it.
