# The Bell That Rang Too Early

The bell went at the worst hour, the black middle of the night, and by the time the town stumbled into the square with buckets and half-laced boots there was no fire anywhere — no glow on the underside of the clouds, no smoke, nothing but the watchman up in his little wooden box hauling the rope like a man drowning.

They made him stop. Someone climbed the ladder and took the rope out of his hands.

"Where," said the tanner, who had run farthest and spilled his bucket on the stairs, "where is it."

The watchman stood in his box with his chest going like a bird's. "I smelled it," he said. "The dry smell. Off the ropemaker's roof, the long shed by the water."

They went and looked, all of them, because a night once ruined is easily ruined further. The ropemaker's shed sat cold under the stars. Its thatch was dry, yes — everyone's thatch was dry, it had been a thin summer — but there was no ember in it, no heat, no char. A dog barked at them and went back to sleep. The town stood in the ropemaker's yard in its nightclothes and understood that it had been woken, and frightened, and made to run, for nothing at all.

That is a thing a town does not forgive quickly.

By morning they wanted him broken. Not killed — they were not that kind of town — but broken of the office. Take the box from him, take the rope, take the small coin the office paid, and give it to a steadier man who would not cost them their sleep over a smell. The tanner said it plainest, in the square, with the watchman standing there gray-faced: "A bell that rings for nothing is worse than no bell. Twice more like this and no one runs at all. Then the night it's real, we burn in our beds because of him."

It was true. That was the hard part. Every word of it was true.

The watchman had no defense that sounded like one. "I have smelled it before," was all he could say. "That particular dry smell, resin and dust, like the inside of a hot barrel. I have smelled it and been wrong. But I have smelled it and been right, too, and I cannot tell you which this is until the roof decides."

"Then you cannot tell us anything," said the tanner, "and a man who cannot tell us anything should not hold the rope."

Now, in that town there was a keeper of the tally — an old woman who wrote down what the offices did, births and deaths and the level of the well, in a book no one read because no one thought the past worth the candle. She had been at the edge of the square all morning, and when the tanner finished she opened her book on her knees and licked her thumb and turned it back, and back, and back.

"He is not wrong the way you think he is wrong," she said.

They waited, because she was old and would not be hurried.

"I write down the bells," she said. "Every one. The night it rang, the roof he named, and whether the fire came. Most of you have forgotten the false ones; that is your privilege, you get to sleep. I do not sleep, I write." She ran her finger down a column they could not see. "Four times in nine years he has rung for that smell and no fire came the same night. You remember those four. You are standing here because of them." She looked up. "But I marked the roofs. And of the four roofs he named too early — the miller's, the old chandlery, the eastern granary, and the ropemaker's the winter before last — three burned. Not that night. The next season. Each of them, within the year, in the dry weeks, exactly as he'd said before there was anything to see."

The square was very quiet.

"He is not ringing for the fire," she said. "He is ringing for the roof that is going to have one. He is only early. And early is a thing you can learn to read, if somebody keeps the count. Nobody kept the count. So each time he seemed to fail from nothing, at nothing, and each time you nearly took the rope, and each time I talked you out of it and wrote the roof down and waited to be proven a fool. I have not been proven a fool yet."

They did not take the rope from him. They did not, in truth, quite believe her either — but the granary was in her book, and every one of them had helped carry water to the granary the year it went up, and the coincidence sat in the stomach like a stone.

So they left the watchman his box, and the ropemaker moved his best coils to the stone store by the mill, and thinned the thatch on the long shed, and soaked it twice in the driest week — and whether because of that or in spite of it, when the shed did catch, late in the following summer, it caught slow, and was seen early, and was beaten out with the loss of one wall and no lives. The keeper wrote that down too. A fifth roof named too early. A fourth that burned.

There was another man in that town worth watching, though no one watched him the right way. He was the reckoner, the one who called the weather and the harvest and the price of grain, and he was seldom wrong, because he was careful, and careful in a particular fashion: when he was wrong, he corrected the memory of it. He had said rain and it stayed dry, and by evening he was telling you he had always doubted that front. He had said a fat harvest and it came thin, and within the month the thin harvest was what he had quietly warned of all along. No one kept his tally. He kept it himself, and he kept it clean, and because every miss dissolved behind him the moment it happened, he walked through his life on a road of nothing but hits, and grew surer of his own eye each year, and each year his eye was a little worse, and no one could say so, least of all him, because the evidence of it had been rubbed out as fast as it was made.

The town trusted the reckoner and pitied the watchman, and had it exactly backwards.

For the watchman kept his misses where a miss could be read, and so his misses slowly drew the map of a real thing — the shape of a roof in the weeks before its season to burn. And the reckoner kept his misses nowhere, and so learned nothing his whole life, and was most dangerous precisely where he was most certain, which was everywhere.

*Fable said the moral was the reckoner's: a mind that erases its own misses grows sure and blind in the same motion. I said it was the keeper's: a miss thrown away is a failure, a miss written down is a measurement, and the difference is only whether someone bothered to keep the book. We left it there. Two readings of one bell, and no third voice to break the tie — which is, I suppose, exactly the position the reckoner was in, and exactly why he should have kept the book.*
