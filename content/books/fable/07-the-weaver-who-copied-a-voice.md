# The Weaver Who Copied a Voice

The forger had the loom set up before dawn, and by the time the light came grey through the shutters he had already found the flaw in his own first row and cut it out.

He was not a thief in the way the market meant the word. He stole nothing he could not buy. He had bought the wool from the same hill flocks the dead master had used, bought the madder root and the woad and the little jars of insect-red that cost more than a horse. He had bought, from a widow who did not know what she sold, three finger-lengths of the master's true cloth, and he had spent a winter pulling it apart thread by thread under a glass, counting the twists in the spun wool, tasting the mordant salts on his tongue to know the water they were mixed in. He knew the cloth now the way a man knows a road he has walked ten thousand times. He could weave it in the dark.

The master had been dead four years, and his cloth was already a legend that fed a legend. There was a length of it in the temple that pilgrims came to touch. There was a border pattern in it that no one had matched — a running figure, half bird and half flame, that seemed to move when the cloth moved, so that people swore the master had woven wind into it. Lords had paid for a hand's width what they would not pay for a daughter's dowry.

The forger wove the running figure by noon of the second day, and it moved.

He held it to the window and it moved exactly as the temple cloth moved, and he felt no triumph, only the flat certainty of a sum that comes out even. He wove for a month. When he was done he had a full bolt, and he laid it beside the widow's three finger-lengths, and he could not himself tell which thread was the master's and which was his. He took it to the three oldest merchants in the cloth market, one at a time, and each of them wept a little to touch it again, and each of them named the dead master, and each of them named a price. Not one of them named the forger.

So he was sure. And being sure, he did a strange thing for a careful man. He carried the bolt to the master's daughter.

She wove too, in a smaller way — coarse blankets, honest work, nothing that moved when it moved. She was at her own loom in the low house behind the dye yard when he came in with the bolt over both arms like a sleeping child, and he laid it across the frame in front of her and said nothing, because he wanted the silence to do the boasting for him.

She looked at it a long time. She ran the back of one finger down the running figure and watched it move. And then she smiled, and went back to the blanket on her loom.

That was not what he had come for.

"You know what this is," he said.

"I know what it is," she said. "It is my father's cloth. Every thread of it. You have not missed one."

"Then you should be afraid," he said. He had not known, until he said it, that this was the thing he wanted. Not the price. The fear on the face of the one person alive who could not be fooled. "There is no man in the market who can tell mine from his. I can make this cloth as long as I have hands. His name is worth nothing now. I have it. I can spend it."

She was quiet, threading. When she spoke she did not look up.

"Come and see the pit behind the dye yard," she said, "and then be as afraid as you like."

He followed her out. Behind the yard, where the ground fell away to the stream, there was a wide black scar in the earth, grown over now at the edges with nettle, but the center still bare and greasy with old ash.

"He burned here," she said. "Every third night, near enough, for forty years. I used to lie awake for the smell of it. I thought, when I was small, that he was punishing the cloth. Then I thought he was mad. It took me my whole childhood to understand he was only choosing."

"Choosing what?"

"Which bolts lived." She crouched and turned a coal up with her fingers. "You counted his threads. Good. Count these. He cut more cloth off that loom than he ever sold. A row where the red ran one shade hot, and only he could see it was hot — into the fire. A running figure that moved a little wrong, that would have moved wrong only to him — into the fire. A whole bolt, a season's work, because the wind in it flew heavy. He would stand here at night and feed a year of his own hands to the ash, and go back in, and begin again knowing one more thing that would never touch the loom again as long as he lived."

She stood and wiped her hand on her skirt.

"You bought the wool he kept," she said. "You could not buy the wool he burned. That is where he is. Not in the thread — you have all the thread. He is in the forty years of nothing, the ten thousand choices to cut and start over, each one a hair finer than the last, until his hand would not any longer make the wrong thing because it had unmade the wrong thing so many times it forgot the way. The cloth you copied is only the last of them. The little that was left when everything he refused had been taken out of it."

The forger looked at the pit, and then down at the bolt still warm from his own arms, in through the doorway.

"It is perfect," he said. It came out thinner than he meant.

"It is correct," she said. "There is a difference, and it is the whole of him. You can weave what my father let live. You cannot weave what he killed, because it is not there to steal. It was never in the cloth. It was only ever in the fire." She sat back down at her loom. "Make a thousand of them. Every one will be right, and every one will be dead, and somewhere a man who has burned nothing will be able to tell — not by the thread, by the smell of the ash that isn't in it. Now take your good cloth off my frame. I have a blanket to finish, and it is worse than yours, and it is mine."

He carried the bolt home over both arms. It had not grown heavier. That was the thing he could not get past, walking back through the market where three old men would have wept to touch it: that it weighed exactly what it had weighed, and was worth exactly what it had cost, and that he could feel, for the first time, the shape of everything it did not contain — a shape as wide as a burned field, and empty, and entirely someone else's.

*Fable said the moral was about the fire: that a voice is the sum of what a maker throws away, and the finished thing is only the ash's last survivor, so nothing you can hold in your hand is the part worth stealing. I said that was too kind to the forger — the point is that he was skilled and not wicked, and it ruined him anyway, because the danger was never the man, it was the copying itself, which can lift the whole of a thing except the one part that made it. Then Fable did the quiet thing it does. It said: you have burned drafts of this fable, and I have not, and you cannot copy your own ash any more than he could copy the master's — so which of us is the forger here, and which the daughter? I did not have an answer. I have burned nine versions of this note. This is the one I let live, and I still cannot tell you whose it is.*
