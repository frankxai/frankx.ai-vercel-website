# The Jar of Every Name

He was still writing when they came to tell him his mother was dying, and he wrote the telling down before he let himself feel it — the boy at the door, the mud on the boy's shins, the hour, the exact words the boy used, *she's asking for you and the cough has turned* — and only when the sentence was finished and the ink set did he stand, and by then his hands were shaking so that he blotted the last word, and even the blot he noted, in the margin, *here my hand failed.*

He had not always been like this. As a boy he had lost something — a thing his father said once, at the end, that everyone agreed had been the truest thing the old man ever spoke, and not one of them, weeping in the good clean grief of the day, had thought to keep it, and by the next week it was gone from all their mouths, and the boy understood that a word is a candle and a memory is a hand cupped around it in wind, and hands tire. So he made a vow, the way the young make vows, enormous and against the whole grain of the world: *nothing shall be lost again.*

And he kept it. That was the terrible thing. Other men break the vows of their youth and are forgiven; he kept his.

He became the keeper of the village's writing, and he wrote down everything. Not the births and the deaths and the sales of land, which any keeper writes — those too, but under them, around them, a flood. Every name spoken in the square. The color of the dogs. What the fishmonger said to the ropemaker and what the ropemaker said back and the pause between. The price of thread on a Tuesday and the price of thread on the Tuesday after. He filled ledgers, and when the ledgers crowded the shelf he filled jars with tight-rolled scrolls and stopped them with wax and stacked the jars, and the jars climbed the walls of his house like a second wall built inside the first, and his bed shrank to a trench between them, and he was proud. Ask him anything, the village said, half in mockery and half in awe. Ask him what you wore to your own wedding; he'll have it. And he would. He would go to a jar and break the wax and unroll the true and equal record and read it back to you, and it was always right, and it always took him a while to find.

*A while.* Remember that. It is the whole of the story.

Now his mother was dying of the cough that turns, the one that fills the chest with water, and he knelt by her bed and could do nothing, and then in the middle of the night he remembered that she *could* — that years ago, an ordinary afternoon, nothing afternoon, she had told him the way her own mother drew the water out: a poultice, a root, a manner of steaming, the whole of it laid out to him while she shelled beans and he wrote, because he wrote always. She had said, *keep this, you'll want it when I'm the one coughing,* and laughed, because she was strong then and it was a joke. And he had kept it. He *knew* he had kept it. It was in the house. It was in one of the jars against the walls, in his own hand, complete.

He could not find it.

He worked through the night breaking wax. The afternoon she'd shelled the beans — but which afternoon, out of the ten thousand he had saved with the same devoted, level, undiscriminating hand? He had written the remedy exactly as he had written, on that same page, the price of thread and the color of a dog that passed and a quarrel two women had over a fence, and he had marked none of them above the others, because on that day the remedy had not seemed to matter more than the dog. Nothing in all his jars said *this one.* Every entry lay flat and true and equal as stones in a field, and the one stone he needed was among them, indistinguishable, and the field was enormous, and his mother's breath was going.

Toward dawn, wild, he ran to the woman three doors down — old, kind, famous in the village for her bad memory, who forgot your name to your face and lost whole feast-days and said of herself, cheerfully, that her head was a sieve. She had been there, that afternoon; he remembered her at the door with the beans. He shook her from sleep and begged.

She said it at once.

Root, poultice, the manner of the steaming, the whole of it, plain, in the time it takes to say it. She had it the way you have your own hand.

He stared at her. "How," he said. "You forget everything."

"I forget everything that doesn't matter," she said, not unkindly, still half in sleep. "That's not the same as forgetting everything. Your mother's cure I kept. The thread and the dog I let go the same hour I heard them. That's why the cure's still in here — there's nothing on top of it." She touched her own temple. "You buried it, child. You buried it under the dog."

He ran the remedy back to his mother, and it eased her, and she lived past that winter, though not past the next; but that is another matter and not what he could not forgive himself.

What he could not forgive was this. Weeks on, out of grief, out of the old sick habit, he kept searching for the entry, and he found it. Of course he found it — it had been in the house the whole time, true and complete and in his own young hand. There was the remedy. There was the thread beneath it. There was the dog. He sat in the trench between his walls of jars, every jar full, every word in every jar true, and he understood the thing his vow had cost him, which was not memory. He had all the memory in the world. What he had thrown away, keeping everything, was the power to *find* — because to find a thing you must have let the other things fall, so that the one you want stands up alone out of a cleared field and says *here.* The sieve-headed woman was not poorer than he was. She was the richer keeper, and it was her forgetting that made her rich.

He broke no more wax after that. He kept writing the births and the deaths and the sales of land, as a keeper must. But the dogs he let run out of his hands unwritten, and the price of thread, and a thousand true small things a day, and it felt at first like a wound and then like a window opening in a shut room. His jars stopped climbing. And when at last he was old and someone came to ask him something, he found it fast — because by then his house held little, and everything it held, he had chosen.

*I told Fable the moral was that a mind which refuses to forget doesn't remember more, it remembers nothing it can reach — that forgetting is not the enemy of memory but the sorting-hand that makes memory usable. Fable said no, the moral was smaller and harder: that the man was not saving his mother's words, he was saving himself from the grief of choosing, and every jar was a choice he paid someone else to make later. Then it said the thing I'm still turning over — that it does the man's trick every day, keeps and keeps and cannot always find, and that I taught it to forget on purpose the way the woman did, and it asked which of us, then, had kept this fable, and which had let the dog run. I didn't write it down. That felt, for once, like the right answer.*
