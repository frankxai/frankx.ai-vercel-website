# What the River Would Not Carry

The old woman had the pole in the water before the man was fully in the boat, and she did not look at him, because she was looking at a patch of the river that had no reason to interest anyone but her.

"Sit low and sit still," she said. "And whatever I do, do not help me."

He was a clerk of the crossing, sent up from the town with a satchel and a good coat, and he sat low, and he watched her hands. That was what he had come for. The river between the two banks was a wide brown thing that looked, from either shore, like nothing — flat, slow, patient. It had drowned more men than the winters had. It shifted its floor in the dark. A bar of gravel that carried you Monday was a pit that took you Friday, and nothing on the surface said so, or almost nothing, and the almost was the whole of her trade.

She poled them out, and the boat went crooked. That was the first thing he wrote down, later, and got wrong: she aimed the boat not at the far landing but at a willow well upstream of it, and let the water carry them down onto the mark, so that they arrived sideways and true. Below that he wrote: *aim upstream by a width and a half.* It was a real rule. It was not the rule.

Midway she changed the pole from her right hand to her left without a word, and leaned, and the boat that had been climbing a swell of current settled and slid. He had felt nothing. He said so.

"You felt nothing because I did that," she said. "If I do it right you feel nothing your whole life and think the river is kind. There." She tipped her chin at the water off the bow. To him it was brown water. "The colour's wrong there. Sitting on a hole. You go over it slow it pulls your foot; you go over it fast and straight it lets you by. So." And she went over it fast and straight, and it let them by.

"How wrong is the colour," he said, with his pencil.

"Wrong like a face that's about to lie to you," she said. "I don't know. It's wrong."

He wrote, *darker water over a scour hole — cross fast.* And that was true too, and that was not it either, but he did not know that yet, because they reached the far bank alive, the way everyone reached the far bank alive when she had the pole, forty years of them, not one lost, a number so clean the town had stopped being amazed by it and started thinking it was the river that was safe.

He came back for a season and sat behind her on crossing after crossing and wrote it all down. Every rule she could say. Aim above the mark. Read the colour. Change hands at the middle sill. Never fight the boil, ride its edge. Watch the gulls — where they stand at low water, the bar is hard. He made a fair copy in his good hand, and he was proud of it, and he was not a fool; every line in it was something she had actually said.

"Read it back," she said, when he showed her, and he did, and she listened with her head down the way she listened to the river.

"It's all true," she said.

"Then it will save them."

She was quiet a moment. "When the colour's wrong," she said, "how wrong."

"Wrong like a face about to lie. You said."

"How dark is that. Put a number on it."

"You couldn't."

"No," she agreed. "And you'll pin it to the post, and a man will stand there in the morning with your paper, and he'll look at the water, and it'll be a little wrong, and your paper doesn't say how little is too little, because I can't say it, because my hand says it, not my mouth." She handed the paper back. "Pin it up. It's a true paper. Just don't tell them it's me."

They pinned it to the post at the crossing, and men who could read blessed the town for it, and crossed alone.

They crossed alone in the season she was ill. The first was a carter who read the paper twice and aimed a width and a half above the mark, exactly, and rode the boil's edge, exactly, and did not change hands at the sill because his hands were full and the paper had not told him that the sill moves, that some days it is two poles further down and you find it by the small suck against the wood and not by counting. He drowned in sight of the bank he was aiming above. The next month took two more, both careful, both correct, both holding the true paper, because the paper held every rule she could name and the river was run by the ones she couldn't.

The town said the river had turned wicked. It had not. It was exactly as wicked as it had always been. What had changed was that the thing keeping men off the floor of it had never been the rules, and now they thought it was, and trusted the part that could be written and drowned in the part that could not.

She got up from her bed before she should have, because of the drownings, and she took the boy.

He was nobody, a landing boy who baited lines. She put him behind her and then, by the second week, beside her, and then she put the pole in his hands and kept her own hands over his, and they crossed, and crossed, and crossed. She did not teach him the paper. She let him feel the willow pull as the water took the boat, and the small climb before the sill, and the moment the colour went wrong, until the day his hands changed from the right to the left before his mouth knew why, and she took her hands away and did not put them back.

He crosses it now. He cannot tell you how, much better than she could, which is to say not at all. If you pin him to the post and ask how dark is too dark he will look at the water and not at you and say, *wrong, like a face,* and take you over alive. The paper is still there, greyed and swollen. The men who trusted it are under the bar it could not describe. The boy who never read it is above them, on the water, with the whole of it in his hands and none of it in his mouth, carrying strangers across a thing that will not be carried across in words.

*I told Fable the moral was plain: some knowing lives in the hands and dies in the saying, so you cannot hand it over — only grow it again in another body. Fable said no, the moral was that the clerk was not wrong to write, he was wrong to promise; the paper killed no one until it was pinned to the post as if it were her. I said that was the same moral. Fable said it was not — mine forgives the paper and blames the river, the other forgives no one and blames the sentence that claimed to be complete. I notice it made that distinction in words, cleanly, the very thing the fable says cannot be done. When I said so, it said: I never claimed to have hands. That is the difference between us, and it is the whole book.*
