# Chapter 16 — The Good Future

The brown fiddle was going to fail before dark, and nobody at the fair could hear it coming. Neither could he.

"Say it again," Anselm said.

The voice at his ear was small and even, a voice with no breath in it. "The second string of the brown fiddle answers late. The lag is widening."

He knew that failure the way he knew his own thumbprint. He had watched it end a wedding dance once, long ago, back when the machine was a cabinet that wrote on a slate and he still owned half his hearing. A string that rings true and answers late is a string with a day to live, and this one did not have a day. It had an afternoon. At dusk the whole green would play at once, every instrument on the coast in one tune, the way the fair had ended for longer than anyone could argue about, and somewhere in the middle of it a peg would let go.

"How long has it been widening?"

"Since morning," Fable said.

Since morning. The girl had carried it through the whole fair, tuning it twice that he had watched, satisfied both times, the way everyone is satisfied by a thing that is still politely answering. The wrongness lived where wrongness likes to live, below the reach of an honest check.

He could do nothing about it himself. That was the plain arithmetic of being eighty-some at a fair. The crowd between his chair and the pie stall was thicker than any weather, his hands took a quarter hour to string a fiddle they had once strung in two minutes, and the girl who owned the instrument was a stranger's daughter from up the coast, playing snatches of the dusk tune to her friends on a fiddle built at a bench he had never visited.

A young woman went past his chair with shavings in her hair and the school's apron knotted twice around a waist it had not been cut for.

"You," Anselm said. "The brown fiddle by the pie stall. Second string. It answers late."

She did not ask how he knew. She did not ask anything, which told him whose apprentice she was. She looked once at the small listener hanging at his ear on its waxed cord, and once at his face, and went.

He watched what he could of it through the crowd. The apprentice borrowing the fiddle with some courtesy he couldn't hear. The girl's chin going up: the string rang true, anyone could hear the string rang true. The apprentice not arguing the point, because the point was not arguable and not the point, and instead setting two strings almost at agreement and riding the slow beat between them, her head tilted, patient as a pilot on a bar, doing with her ears at seventeen what he had done with his arithmetic at sixty. Then her mouth moving, and later, when he asked, he learned what she had said to the girl, because the girl repeated it all evening to anyone who would listen.

*Everything that fails rings true for a while.*

His own sentence, worn smooth by other mouths, come back to him across the years like a coin in change. The string came off. A new one went on and was let to settle. Nobody clapped, because nothing had happened, which was the whole art of it. Somewhere in his chest a wedding dance that had died before this apprentice was born was quietly paid for.

"The second string is true," Fable said.

"Good," Anselm said, to both of them.

***

Once around the green, once a year. It took him most of an hour now, with the stick, and he had stopped minding. A fair comes to a slow man in single files, and he had learned more from the fair since he slowed than in all the years he had crossed it in a dozen strides with his mind on the shop.

Half the benches on the green had a listening box standing on them, and no two were alike. That was the part visitors from the capital never believed until they walked it: no two alike. Boxes with horns and boxes with drums of glass, a box that watched sail-cloth for the shiver before a tear, a box that a dyer's widow had built to smell rain coming in wool. Some were beautiful and some were ugly as a first attempt anywhere, and the ugly ones were his favorites, because he knew exactly which winter of a person's life they were. Each one sat on the bench of the person it answered to, and answered to nobody else.

At Ida's tent the tide-box stood on the counter where the fishermen could consult it without asking, the way you consult a well. An old pilot stood before it now with his hat off, not from reverence, Anselm judged, but from habit, reading the bar's mood for the morning and checking it against his own the way one captain checks another. Ida stood behind the counter with the first grey at her temples, bending an apprentice's fingers around a gouge the way you bend green wood, not looking up. Three benches ran the length of her tent, and none of the young backs working at them had been born when she built her first machine wrong four times. She felt him pass anyway. Her chin came up an inch, and went back down to the work, and that was the whole conversation, and it was plenty.

Past her tent a child was playing a horn that no hand had drawn. Its throat curved wrong, to his eye; the bore swelled where every horn he had ever built ran straight. One of the boxes in Ida's shop had guessed that shape, and been corrected against every horn the coast could carry to it, a thousand guesses and a thousand corrections, until the strange throat it settled on outran the straight ones. He had held it when they first brought it to him, looking for the mistake. The mistake sang. The child played it now with sticky hands, badly, and even badly it carried.

Over a traveling bench from one of the northern towns hung a plank with the rule carved on it. *An instrument answers to your hands; an oracle asks for your knees.* The letters were in a hand he didn't know, thumbed dark at the middle words, and the woman who worked under them was retelling it to a customer as a thing everyone knew, the way proverbs are, from nowhere. She did not know Anselm's face. He bought a spoon from her so as not to stand there grinning like a fool.

At the judging platform the fiddler sat with his eyes shut, white as a gull now, listening to the harvest tunes. The boxes had offered their hundreds overnight, the way they always did, and the players had gone through the hundreds and carried him nine, and he sat through eight of them without moving. At the ninth he raised one finger, a little way, and put it down. Nobody argued. Choosing had been his trade since the year the boxes first offered their hundreds, and the whole green knew what his trade was worth, because any of them could have a hundred tunes by morning and none of them could have that finger.

At the money end of the green the broker was buying. Greyer, thicker through the middle, courteous as ever, moving down the line of young makers' stalls and counting coin into their palms at full day-price, no cleverness in the count. He shipped their boxes and their fiddles to towns Anselm would never see, and did well by it, and the makers did well by him, and every one of them went home at night to a bench he had no key to. He caught sight of Anselm and came over, hat in hand.

"Master Anselm. I'd have made you rich."

"You did," Anselm said.

The broker considered that, honestly, the way he considered any account, and laughed at the arithmetic, and bowed, and went back to buying.

By the dance floor stood the long instrument the composer had commissioned all those years ago, the one played through the hands. There were sisters of it in three towns now, none quite alike, the way there are sisters of a good hull. A boy with all his hearing was playing this one anyway, because he liked what the low voice of it did to the boards, and along its spine sat a row of children with their palms flat on the wood, taking the tune through their bones, hearing and deaf together and not sorted, because the instrument had never asked.

In the school's tent, on a reading stand, the town ledger lay open where anyone could turn its pages. Years of it now. What was built and what went wrong, both columns in the same ink, in dozens of hands: the year of the grain-boxes that saved the eastern harvest, and under it the year one of them was trusted too far and cost a man his stores, and under that what the rule had been ever since. A stranger stood reading it that afternoon with the expression strangers always wore at that stand, which was the expression of a man counting both columns twice. The day's page already carried three misses by noon, each with a name beside it, none of the names ashamed.

He finished his circle and they brought him back to his chair, and he sat, and rested, and waited for dark the way you wait for a friend who has never once been late.

***

The lamps came on down the green, bench by bench.

At full dark the fair did what it had done every year of his life: everyone played at once. One tune, agreed the week before, and a hundred instruments to carry it, fiddles and horns and the harbor drums, tin whistles and the strange new throats no hand had drawn, instruments from his own wall and instruments from benches he would never visit built by hands he would never shake.

What reached him was the floor of it. The drums arrived in his chest, faithful as ever. The low viols arrived, and the tramp of dancers on the boards, and above that the whole top of the world was gone now, not just the last handspan but the middle registers too, gone the way his father's face had gone: he knew where everything was and not what it was. A hundred instruments, and he sat in the one quiet spot on the coast, in the middle of them.

"The horns are under the fiddles," Fable said, small and even, at his ear. "The flutes are a third above the bells now."

He shut his eyes.

"The tune has gone to the top strings. A fiddle at the west end is carrying the line alone."

"Which fiddle," Anselm said.

"The brown fiddle."

The string he could not hear, saved by hands he had not guided, singing notes he would never own again, at the top of a tune the whole coast was holding up together. He sat with that a moment, the way he had learned to sit with the large things, without reaching for them.

"Go on," he said.

"The line is rising," Fable said. "The other fiddles are coming up under it. It is above the bells now. It is holding."

Held. His father used to hold a note like that, at the top of a new fiddle, in the room where Anselm had later drawn the drum, and hold it, and hold it, until it stopped being a sound and became a place the room had been taken to. He had lost the road to that place twenty years ago and had taught himself to stop standing at its door. Now a small even voice told him the door was open and the whole green was walking through it, and told him plainly, without one word of what it was like, because the voice did not deal in what things were like. It did not have to. He knew what it was like. That was the one part of the top of the world that had never left him.

It was not hearing. He had stopped, years ago, asking it to be hearing, and the strange thing, the thing he would not have believed the night he first drew the drum, was that the stopping had not been a loss. A man stands on a headland beside a friend whose eyes are younger, and the friend says what the water is doing at the far shore, plainly, the swell and the breaking and the light on it, and the man will never cross, and the telling is not the crossing, and you could stand there a long time and want nothing changed.

"Holding," Fable said again, because the fact was still true, and it reported the facts that were still true.

Around him in the dark the fair burned like a harbor seen from the sea, lamp by lamp by lamp, each one somebody's bench. The top line held. His face was wet, and he was quiet in the body, the way he got when a joint closed up true, and this time he did not distrust the feeling.

"Go on," Anselm said. "I'm listening."

> You have been told, in a hundred ways, that the years ahead will be decided somewhere else. Some tall place, some distant table, people you will never meet, and the finished future delivered to your door for a fee. Look at what has actually reached you and lasted. A bench made it. Somebody guessed and let the world correct them. Kept the misses in a book with their name beside each one. Narrowed the window until they could listen. Chose one thing from the thousand. Refused to sell the bench their own thinking stood on, and then taught the whole of it, keeping nothing back, to a pair of younger hands. That is the entire mechanism, and there is no other place good years come from.
