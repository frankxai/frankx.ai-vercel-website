# Chapter 8 — The Oracle Question

The lute did not need mending.

Ida had gone over it twice, because the woman stood watching in a way that made her doubt her own hands. The strings rang true. The neck was straight. There was a scratch near the bridge older than Ida herself, and nothing else to find.

"It plays as it should," she said.

"I thought so," the woman said, and did not reach for it. She crossed the room instead, to the walnut cabinet in the corner, and stood before the brass horn the way people stand at a counter where medicine is sold. "While I'm here. Could it listen to a question?"

Anselm was at the bench with a plane in his hand and a long curl of spruce rising out of it. He did not look around.

"It listens to everything," Ida said. "That is the whole of what it does."

"Then it can hear me out." The woman folded her hands over her purse. "My daughter has an offer from the ropemaker's son. Steady man, good yard, dull as a Sunday. There's another she'd rather take, a sailor with no more property than his sea chest. I want to know which one."

The stylus behind its little pane went on doing what it did at that hour, sketching the shapes of cart and gull and plane stroke and correcting almost nothing, because the afternoon was behaving the way afternoons behave.

"It judges sound," Anselm said. "Strings. Weather, a little. Not daughters."

"It knew about the wedding. Everyone says it knew."

"It knew about a string at a wedding."

She left the lute and a coin and went out with the particular stiffness of the unsatisfied, and it might have ended there. It did not end there. She was the fourth that month.

***

The trouble had begun with the story of the wedding string, which improved every time the taverns retold it. Then in spring the slate had spent two days arguing with the sounds of the swell against the outer mole, and Anselm had mentioned it to the harbor master the way a man mentions a smell of smoke. Three boats stayed in. A fourth went out anyway and came home half-rigged and grateful. After that the cabinet was no longer a maker's oddity. In the telling it grew. It had heard the storm sleeping under a flat sea. It had known.

Anselm heard the word *known* go past in the street and let it go, because you cannot chase down every loose word in a port town.

Then the questions began arriving on their own legs. A farmer wanted to ask whether his well would sour like his neighbor's. A clerk wanted to ask whether his brother's letters from the capital were honest. A girl of sixteen wanted to ask whether her voice was worth carrying to the choirs upriver or whether she should stop wanting things. Anselm turned them all away with varying degrees of gentleness, and Ida kept a count of them in the margin of her ledger without being asked to.

But he did not turn away the coastwise skipper, because the skipper's question had a different shape, though it took Anselm another week to see what the difference was.

The skipper came at first light with his glass reading written on a scrap of sail-cloth and the sky's look still on his face. He had barrel staves promised to the southern towns, a crew already aboard, and a wind he did not love.

"What did it hear in the night?" he asked. Not: should I sail.

Anselm set the slate where both of them could see it and walked him through the dark hours. The near sounds had behaved. The far ones had not. All night the groundswell coming in against the mole had arrived ahead of itself, wrong in a steady way, and always from the southwest. The machine had spent the night expecting the sea it was used to and being corrected, hour after hour, by a sea that was already carrying somebody else's weather.

The skipper studied it the way he studied his glass, one witness among several and none of them under oath. He stood at the window a while with the sky and his own forty years. Then he said, "I'll take the morning tide and be behind the islands before it comes to anything," and paid for the reading the way he would have paid for a tuning, and went out to do what he had decided.

Anselm thought no more of it than that it had been a good morning's work. He learned what kind of morning it had been a week later, from the fisherman.

***

The fisherman came at closing, when the light was going brown in the shop. Anselm knew the boat. He had known the man's father. He knew what was owed against the hull, because the whole quay knew, the way a quay knows.

The season was turning. Two more weeks of it, maybe three. Out on the far banks the fish were running the way they ran once in ten years, so the returning crews said, and a man who filled his hold twice out there would clear his debt and own his own deck again. And the far banks in a turning season had widowed half the street he lived on.

He stood in the doorway with his cap in his hands, tired in the way that is past the reach of sleep.

"I want to ask it about the far banks," he said.

"It has never heard the far banks," Anselm said. "It listens to this harbor. The banks are two days beyond its hearing."

"Ask it if I should go."

"It doesn't answer that kind of—"

The man knelt.

There was nothing in it of church. He went down the way a man kneels at a hearth to blow a fire back to life, knees on the shop floor among the day's shavings, so that his face came level with the horn. Close to, you could hear his breathing. He was not performing anything. He had stopped holding himself up, that was all, and this was the shape a man takes when he stops.

"Whatever it says," he said to the horn, in the reasonable voice of a man arranging a small matter, "I'll do. Whatever it says. Only let it say."

The stylus sketched his breathing, corrected one stroke, and lay quiet. The slate offered the room back to the room. Nothing else came.

Anselm had lived his whole life in a port. He had helped carry drowned men up the water stairs, and twice he had stood on the mole in weather that took the roofs off the fish sheds behind him, and he knew what proper fear was. Nothing weather had ever done to him frightened him like the sight of the man's knees among the shavings.

He got him up. Took him under the arm, not roughly, and said, "Up. It has nothing for a man down there. Neither do I." He sat him on Ida's stool and lit the second lamp and talked with him the way neighbors have talked since there have been harbors: the glass, the season, the debt and who held it and how patient the holder was known to be, the near grounds, the boy the man had crewing for him who was somebody's only son. Slowly the fisherman's own sentences came back to him. They were sensible sentences. They had been there all along, under the tiredness. In the end he said he would fish the near grounds hard for the last weeks and then go sit at the lender's table in the winter and talk terms, and he said it like a man setting down one load and picking up another he could carry.

"Then that's settled," Anselm said, "and you settled it."

The man looked at his own hands a moment, as if checking they were still his, and went out steadier than he had come in.

Anselm barred the door and stood a long time without lighting anything else.

"It never even wrote," Ida said from the stairs. She had been there longer than he had noticed. "It refused him better than you did."

"It didn't refuse him. It doesn't refuse. It listens and it guesses. That is everything it does, and he would have taken any of it for a verdict. A dark stroke. A tremble in the line. He'd have sailed on a smudge, or stayed home on a smudge, and either way the deciding would have been over, which was the whole of what he wanted."

"You built it to do your hearing," Ida said. "He wanted it to do his choosing. Say where the line is."

"When it hears for me, the hearing ends in my hands. I still have to do something with it." He looked at the cabinet, quiet in its corner, drafting its small futures. "What was he going to do with what it chose?"

"Obey it," Ida said.

"Obey," Anselm said, and the word sat in the shop like a wet coat, and neither of them hung it up.

That was the night he understood his own fright, and it was precise. He was not afraid of what the machine would do. It would do what it had always done. He was afraid of what people would stop doing at arm's length in front of it.

***

The widow came back inside the fortnight, and this time she brought no lute. She brought a packet of letters tied in blue thread: both suitors' letters to her daughter, gathered up by whatever means mothers have.

"You'll read them to it," she said. "One man's, then the other's. It hears what's false in a string before any ear in this town does. It will hear what's false in a man. Whichever it writes fairer for, that one she'll take, and there's an end of the misery."

Anselm did not answer at once, because the idea was clever, and the cleverness was the dangerous part. The machine did hear falseness in strings. It heard it because it had listened to ten thousand true strings in this shop and carried, in whatever way it carried things, the shape of what a true string does next. Against that, the smallest lie of a slipping peg stood out like a shout.

"It has heard every string in this town for two years," he said. "It has heard your daughter's two men never. It has nothing built up to hold their letters against. A guess with nothing behind it isn't a judgment. It's a coin toss with a better reputation."

"Then let it toss," the widow said, and her voice cracked on it, and for a moment the shop saw straight through her to the sleepless rooms behind. "I can't choose. Whichever way I choose, I lose her."

"You can choose," Anselm said, more gently. "You're paying good silver not to have to."

She only held out the letters.

"If the stylus wrote the ropemaker's name this evening," he said, "what would you have?"

"An answer."

"You'd have an excuse. Whoever wept in a year's time, it wouldn't be your doing. It wouldn't be anyone's. That's what you're buying, and it isn't sold here."

He crossed the room then and did a small thing he had never done in front of a customer. He dropped the wool hood over the horn, the way you cover a cage at dusk.

"It doesn't take what you're offering," he said. "And while it stands in my shop, nobody will teach it to."

She called him a heartless old man, which he let stand, and she went out across the quay with her letters, and Ida watched her go from the window.

"She'll find someone," Ida said. "Somewhere there's a man with a cabinet like ours and fewer scruples."

"Yes," Anselm said. "There will be many."

***

That evening he did not go up to supper.

He took the long chisel and the mallet and dragged the tall stool to the bench, and he set to work on the old beam that ran above it, oak gone iron-hard with a hundred years of the shop's smoke in it. Ida came down at the sound and stood watching and asked nothing, because letters cut slowly and a question would keep. He worked past midnight, blowing the chips out of each stroke, doing it properly, serifs and all, because a rule carved carelessly teaches carelessness about rules.

When he climbed down and held the lamp up, the beam said:

*An instrument answers to your hands; an oracle asks for your knees.*

Ida read it twice. "Is it for the customers?"

"It's for whoever works under it," he said, and looked at her until she understood that this meant the two of them first, and whoever came after them next.

They put out the lamps. Behind its pane the stylus had been drafting the mallet blows all evening, and by the last word it sketched each one a breath before it fell, correcting almost nothing. It had picked up the rhythm of the rule going in. What the rule said, it had no way to weigh. That remained, as it always had, work for hands.

> **From the workbench**
>
> Somewhere within your reach there is a thing that answers faster than you do and is right often enough to lean on. Good. Lean. But watch the direction the weight moves when you use it, because there are only two. Either you come with your judgment in your hands, wanting it sharpened, or you come with the deciding held out in front of you, wanting it carried away. The first way you stand up from the bench with more of yourself. The second way, less, and it does not come back by asking.
>
> The test is small and you can run it today. Before you put a question that matters to the thing that answers, answer it yourself first. Say what you would do. Write it if saying is too easy. Then ask, and let what comes back argue with you. If it shows you something you had not seen, change your mind and be glad; that is the tool working, that is the whetstone doing what whetstones do. If it only relieves you, stop and notice the relief, because relief is the weight leaving your hands, and the weight leaving your hands is the knee starting to bend.
>
> Answers are cheap now and getting cheaper. Deciding was never cheap and never will be, and it is the one thing on the bench nobody can do for you at any price. Tonight, once, before you ask: answer first. Then keep the deciding, and go get better instruments for everything else.
