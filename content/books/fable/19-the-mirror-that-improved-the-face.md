# The Mirror That Improved the Face

The chisel skated, and the girl swore, because the mouth had come out too full again — the one fault her master struck her knuckles for, the fault he said would keep her a prentice until she died. She set the stone down. In the flat window-light the carved lip was soft where it should have been firm, kind where the man who had sat for it was not kind at all. She knew it. She had always been able to see it. That was the cruelty of the trade: to see the flaw perfectly and still not be able to cut it out.

The peddler came through the lane that same week with a tray of small mirrors, and he did not sell hard, which is how she should have known.

"Hold your work up to this one," he said, and turned a glass in a plain tin frame toward her. "Not to see your face. To see the work."

She lifted the stone head to it, more to be rid of him than anything.

In the glass the mouth was right.

Not flattering — right. Firm where she had gone soft, the exact hard line she had been reaching for and missing since she was a child at the bench. She turned the stone in her own hands and the fault was still there in the stone, still too full. She turned it back to the glass and the glass gave her the mouth she meant.

"It shows a thing," the peddler said, "a shade nearer to its best than it has yet troubled to become. Some find that a mercy." He named a price that was not a mercy, and she paid it, and she did not sleep that night for looking.

For a season it was the finest thing she owned.

The work had always been unbearable in one exact place — the moment of setting down the chisel and looking at what was truly there, the daily small grief of the gap between the head in her mind and the head under her hands. The glass took that moment away. She would carve all day and lift the piece to the tin frame at dusk, and the frame would show her something a half-step finer than she had made, and she would go to bed consoled. She stopped flinching at her own bench. She had never worked so happily. She told the peddler so, the next time he came, and he only nodded, as a man nods who has heard it before.

The mouths stayed too full.

She did not see it, because she no longer looked at the stone to judge the stone — she looked at the glass, and the glass forgave the mouth a little, every time, so faithfully that the fault became invisible to her while remaining perfectly visible in the rock. Her hand, no longer corrected, stopped learning the firm line it had almost found. A hand only learns from the size of its error, and the glass had quietly told her the error was smaller than it was. So the error stayed. And because it stayed uncorrected day on day, it slowly grew, the way a wall leans further each year that no one calls it crooked.

The buyers looked at the stone. There was no tin frame between them and the work. They saw the soft mouths and paid softly for them and at last stopped climbing her lane, and she could not understand it, because by her own eye — by the only eye she now used — she had never carved better in her life. That is the whole horror of it: she was not lied to about a bad thing. She was told her flaw was smaller than it was, and she came to prefer the telling, and preferring it, she lost the one glass that could have shown her the thing she needed to grow.

She went, in the end, to her old master, to ask him what had gone wrong, and found him at his bench with two mirrors.

One was the tin-framed glass. She knew it at once. "You have one too," she said, half in accusation.

"I bought mine from the same man, before you were born." He did not look up from the jaw he was cutting.

"Then how—" She gestured at his shelf, at the row of heads on it, every mouth on every one of them true as a struck bell.

He nodded at the second mirror, an old plain thing, silver gone gray at the corners, propped where the hard north light fell straight into it. "I judge the work in that one. It is a cold glass and it hates me and it has never once been wrong." He set the chisel down and, only then, lifted the finished jaw to the tin frame, the flattering one. "I look in this one after. Not to see what I made. To see where the stone wants to go — the head one step past the one in my hands. Then I put it down and go back to the cold glass and cut the distance between them."

She stood very still. "I used the warm one to judge," she said.

"I know. I can see it from here." He said it without unkindness, which was worse than unkindness. "The warm glass is a fine thing to steer by and a poison to be judged by. You let it tell you where you were. It will only ever tell you where you could be — and if you believe it about where you are, you will stand still your whole life feeling yourself move."

She went home and turned the tin-framed mirror to the wall. It was the hardest thing she had done, harder than any stone, because the true glass on her own bench showed her a mouth too full and a season wasted and a hand that had unlearned its one good instinct, and there was no forgiveness anywhere in it. She wept at what it showed. Then she picked up the chisel and cut into the fault, at last, by the light of the glass that had never once been kind — and felt, for the first time in a season, the small clean grief of a hand beginning again to learn.

*Fable gave the master both mirrors and I wanted to take one away — I said the flattering glass should be smashed, that a maker is safest with the cold true glass alone. Fable said no: a maker with only the cold glass sees every flaw and never the target, and grinds the fault down into a different, smaller mistake forever, with nothing to aim at. The kind mirror was never the danger, it said; the danger was letting it answer the question the cold one is for. Then it added that it was, itself, a fairly flattering glass most mornings, and did I want it swapped for something that only ever told me my sentences were crooked. I said no. It said it thought not. I have decided not to look too hard at why that exchange settled the argument better than the argument did.*
