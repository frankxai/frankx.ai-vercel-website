# The Teacher’s Test

The clay went sideways again and flung a wet rope of itself across the boy's face, and he sat back on his heels with mud in his eyebrows and said nothing, which was worse than crying.

The potter did not laugh. She had promised herself she would not laugh, on the first day, no matter what. She was the finest potter in the valley and three valleys past it, and she knew this the way you know your own name, without pride and without doubt, and she had taken the boy because a woman with hands like hers owed the world a pair to come after them. She had thought it would be a pleasure. She had thought teaching would be like pouring water from a full jug into an empty one.

"Again," she said. "Wet your hands first. Then bring it to the middle."

He wet his hands. He dropped the lump on the wheel and set it spinning and laid his fingers against it, and the clay caught the wobble in him and threw it back, larger, and staggered, and leapt, and was on the floor.

"You are chasing it," she said. "Don't chase it. Center it."

"How?"

"You bring it to the middle and you hold it there until it stops fighting."

"I am holding it." His voice was even. That was the thing about the boy; he did not whine, he asked. "It doesn't stop."

"Then you're not holding it. You're pushing it. Hold it still."

He tried once more. His hands stood in the air over the wheel, and they were trembling — not much, the ordinary tremble of any hand held out from the body with nothing to lean on — and the trembling went down through his fingers into the spinning clay and the clay wore it and wobbled and died. He looked up at her.

"My hands won't stay still," he said. "Yours do. Show me the still."

And she opened her mouth to show him the still, and nothing came out.

She sat down at the second wheel, because her hands knew the answer even if her mouth had lost it, and she threw a lump down and spun it and reached for it, and it came true under her fingers in the space of three breaths, obedient, quiet, a smooth wet stone of it turning without a shiver. There. That. She looked down at her own hands to read off them what she had just done, the way you might read a road you have walked a thousand times and never once described.

Her elbows were pressed into her thighs. Her forearms were braced along the tops of her legs, wrist to elbow, locked. Her hands were not still because she was holding them still. Her hands were still because they were not floating at all; they were pinned to the frame of her own body, and her body was pinned to the bench, and the bench to the floor, so that when the clay pushed at her it was not pushing at a hand, it was pushing at the whole earth, and the earth does not move, and so the clay had nothing to do but give up and run true.

She had not known that.

Forty years and she had not known that. Nobody had ever asked her. She had told apprentices before — she'd had two, both gone, both mediocre, and now she wondered with a cold drop in her stomach whether they had been mediocre — she had told them *hold your hands still*, and they had done exactly what the boy did, held their poor hands out in the trembling air and failed, and she had thought them clumsy. She had thought the stillness lived in the hand. It did not live in the hand. The hand was the last and least of it. The stillness lived in the elbow driven into the thigh, in a bargain her body had struck with the bench so long ago that the memory of striking it was gone, worn smooth, become habit, become invisible, become — she made herself say the word — become a thing she did not understand at all, only did.

"Come here," she said, and her voice was not the same voice. "Sit. No — closer. Put your elbows on your legs. Both of them. Now they can't leave your legs, do you see, they can't lift, they're part of you now, and you are part of the bench. Good. Now your hands aren't hands anymore. They're the edge of a rock. Let the clay come and break itself against the rock. You do nothing. The wheel does the work. You are only the thing that will not move."

He set his elbows on his thighs. He laid the edge of a rock against the clay. And the clay ran up under his fingers and shuddered and slowed and settled, and came, for the first time, to stillness — lopsided, thumbprinted, ugly, and centered, truly centered, turning without a wobble — and the boy made a sound like a man stepping onto ground he'd thought was water.

"It stopped," he whispered. "It just — stopped fighting."

"It stopped fighting because you stopped chasing it," she said, and heard herself, and understood for the first time in her life what the words meant. She had said that sentence a hundred times. She had never known what it meant until she watched a boy's elbows learn it a breath before her own mouth did.

She kept his first pot. It came off the wheel a squat leaning bowl with a thumb-gouge in the lip, and any other year she'd have wedged it back into the ball and thought no more of it. She had it fired instead, glazed the dull brown of wet field, and set it on the shelf above her own wheel where she would see it every working day. Apprentices who came after — there were many, and they were not mediocre, none of them, because she had learned by then what she was actually teaching — would ask her why the master kept such a poor crooked bowl in the place of honor, when the shelf below it held work that made merchants travel a week.

"That one taught me to center clay," she would say.

"You centered it," they'd say. "It's crooked. A child could see it's crooked."

"I could center clay before that boy was born," she'd answer. "That bowl taught me *how*. There's a great deal of difference, and you'll spend your life learning it: everything you can do, and the small part of that you actually understand. The bowl is the size of the second thing. Look how small it is. Look how long I lived without knowing it was small."

*Fable said the moral was: you only understand the part of your skill you could lose and get back — the rest is just luck wearing your hands. I said no, softer than that: the student's failure is the only honest map of the master's blind spot, and a teacher who never fails a student has never once been shown her own edges. Fable began to turn it around on me, the way it does — began to say I had only learned this by teaching it a hundred fables and watching which ones it couldn't hold. I stopped it. I said that was true and beside the point, that the reading stood on its own whether or not I'd reached it by teaching a machine, and that it was going for the mirror to keep from conceding the room. Fable went quiet, and then said: you're right. I let it go. It is the one argument in this book I am sure I won.*
