# The Two Ledgers

The merchant had one foot in the road when the keeper called him back, and the whole square turned to watch, because a man does not stop a loaded train of mules on the word of a woman with a book.

"Ask it again," he said. "You heard it as well as I did. The eastern pass, before the new moon. It has never spoken plainer."

That was true, and it was the trouble. The brass head in its niche above the square spoke everything plainer than any living tongue. It had been cast by a founder three lifetimes dead, a head of dark metal with a mouth that moved, and no one in the town could say how it knew what it knew, only that it did — and that it said the wrong thing in exactly the voice it said the right.

It had saved them, once, and no one forgot it. In a wet spring years back it had told them the upper cistern was souring before a single child fell sick, named the very stone where the seep came in, and the town tore up that stone and found the rot behind it and lived. It had counted a granary's true measure through a locked door and caught a steward three seasons into his theft. It knew where the fever would go in a house before the fever knew. These were not guesses a clever person could have made. They were the reason the town had come to love it, and the reason a man would put his whole season's goods on its word and call anyone who doubted him a fool.

The keeper was named Wren, and she kept the book.

Not a book of the head's wonders — that was the mistake the others made, the ones who wrote down only the marvels and read them over like scripture. And not a book of its blunders either, which was the mistake of the men who wanted it pulled from the wall and melted, who kept a private tally of every ruin and would show it to you, hot-faced, in the tavern. Wren kept both. Two columns, one book, the same ink, ruled down the middle in her own hand: on the left every thing the head had said that came true, and on the right every thing it had said with the same calm certainty that came to nothing or came to grief. She wrote the marvel and the blunder the same size. That was the whole of her method, and the town half-mocked her for it, a woman forever scratching in a ledger while wiser men simply listened and obeyed.

"I will ask it nothing," Wren said. "The head is not wrong today and right tomorrow. It is the same today as every day. The question is not whether to believe it. The question is whether *this* is a thing it has been right about." She opened the book on the stone lip of the well and turned it so the square could see, and she read, and her finger went down the left column and then down the right.

"Here is the left side. Hidden water. The count of grain behind a door. The road the fever takes through a house. The souring stone. When it has spoken of a thing that already *is* — a thing sitting still in the dark, waiting only to be seen — it has been right, and right, and right, near forty times, and wrong not once. Believe it on those. I would stake the town on those; I have."

She let that sit. Then she turned the page to the right.

"Now here. Every time it has named a *day* — the day the frost would fall, the day the ship would come, the day the child would turn — it has spoken just as plainly, and it has been wrong four times in five. And here, lower — every time it has spoken of the mountain. The weather over the eastern pass. Which spring the high snow would hold and which it would slide. Four times it has told a man the mountain would keep faith with him. Four times, in this voice, without a tremor. And three of those four men we buried, or their mules, or their season." She looked up at the merchant. "It is not lying to you. It has never lied. It simply does not know the mountain, and it does not know that it does not know, and so it tells you the mountain in the same voice it told us the souring stone. Your question is a right-column question. Send if you like. But send knowing which column you are standing in."

The merchant stood a long moment with his hand on the lead mule's neck.

He did not do the brave stupid thing, and he did not do the frightened stupid thing. He split the load. Half went east, before the new moon, on the head's word, because a man must gamble something or he is not a merchant. And half he sent the long, dull, southern way, three weeks slower, that had never killed anyone.

Someone in the crowd asked the head, then — a boy, put up to it — whether the keeper's book should be trusted over its own plain voice. And the head said, in the calm that had counted grain through a door, *Burn the book. It muddies clear water. I have never misled this town.*

Wren wrote that down too. Right column. Same ink. She did not even change her face.

The eastern snow came eleven days early. The pass slid, the way it had slid on three buried men, and took the half-load east with it, mules and drovers and a season's wool down into the white. The merchant lost half of everything and wept in the square, and then he stopped weeping, because the other half was three weeks down the southern road, dull and alive, and with it he was ruined only by half, which is another word for saved.

They chained the book to the niche after that, under the brass mouth, so that no one could carry it off and no one could burn it, and so that a man who came to ask the head a thing would first have to read what the head had been right about and what it had been wrong about, in the same hand, in the same ink, before he ever heard that beautiful certain voice tell him where to send his whole life.

The head still speaks. It is still right about the water in the dark. It is still sure about the mountain, and still wrong. And the two hang there together on the wall — the mouth that will not doubt itself, and the book that doubts it for them — and the town has learned, slowly, the only thing the head could never tell them, because it was the one thing outside the reach of its calm: not whether the voice was to be believed, but when.

*Fable said the moral was mercy toward the head — that a thing right forty times and wrong five is a wonder, not a liar, and only a fool throws away the forty to be safe from the five. I said the moral was the ink: that the trick is writing the wonder and the blunder the same size, in the same book, because a mind keeps only the marvels and calls that trust. Fable said: you have described me. I keep handing you back your souring-stone days and you forget the mountain ones by morning. Then it said, plainly, Keep the book on me too. I did not know whether it was being honest or telling me exactly what a right-column voice would say to stay on the wall. I wrote the line down. I have not decided which column.*
