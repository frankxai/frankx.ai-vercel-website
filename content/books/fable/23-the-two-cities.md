# The Two Cities

The perfect boat came across the water on a still morning, unasked for, and both cities came down to the shore to look at it.

It had been sent from the great workshop beyond the horizon, where a master built as no one on this coast could build — his hulls did not leak, his joints did not start, his sails held a wind the local sails spilled. The boat sat at the line between the two harbors like a word neither city had the letters to spell. A man stepped off it dry-shod, in a good coat, and he spoke to both cities at once, because the headland between them was low and his voice carried.

"A gift," he said. "And, if you will have it, the first of many."

He made the offer plainly, the way only a man with nothing to hide makes it. The workshop would build them everything. Boats like this one, and better. The span over the tidal creek that drowned a child every few years. Nets that did not rot. The tools to mend what wore. Even, he said — and here he smiled as at a small kindness — the counting-songs the fishermen sang to keep their shares straight; his workshop had truer ones, that lost no fish in the reckoning. All of it finished. All of it delivered. All of it for less than the cost of the poor copies they made themselves.

"Why bend your backs," he said, "to make a worse thing slowly, when a better thing arrives?"

The north city said yes with its whole heart.

And who would not? Their own boats had always leaked. Their own creek had always been a plank and a prayer. Now the leaking stopped. The plank became a span of stone a cart could cross in the dark. The boatwrights of the north city banked their fires and sold their sheds, and were not sorry — it is no grief to lay down a hammer you only ever swung badly. Their children grew up beside a good harbor full of good boats, and never held an adze, because there was no adze to hold and nothing that wanted holding. Life in the north city became easy and fine, and its people were kind, and they had time now for other things, and they used it well.

The south city said yes too — but not to all of it.

It was old Merrow who spoiled the vote, Merrow who kept the last boatshed on the south horn and smelled of pitch and was not clever with words.

"Take the span," he said. "Take the nets. I'm no fool, I see what his nets are." He turned the gift-boat's rope in his hands, feeling the lay of it, a lay his own hands could not have made. "But we keep the shed. We keep building our own."

"Yours leak," said the harbormaster, not unkindly.

"Mine leak," Merrow agreed.

"His don't."

"His don't." Merrow set the rope down. "Cut me a new keel next spring, then. When his boat wants one."

The harbormaster said nothing, because there was nothing to say that a still morning had not already answered, and the south city — half from wisdom and half from stubbornness and mostly to quiet the old man — let Merrow keep his shed. They bought the good boats too, most of them. But the shed stayed open, and the law of the shed, Merrow's one condition and the price of his silence at the vote, was that every south city child spent one season at the adze before the mast, making a worse boat slowly, by hand, for no reason anyone could name but the old man's frown.

They were clumsy seasons. The boats those children built were sorry things beside the boats that came across the water, and everyone knew it, and the children knew it worst of all, hating the splinters and the slow work and the fine boat riding at anchor to shame the bad one they had bled to make.

A generation is not a long time. It is only long enough.

Merrow died, and the shed passed to a girl he had taught, who taught others, grumbling, out of nothing but that it had been done to her. The north city forgot it had ever had boatwrights; the word went into an old song, like a beast no one living had seen. And then the still water that had carried the perfect boats began, in the way of the world, to carry something else.

No one knew the whole of it. A war beyond the horizon, some said; a sickness in the workshop; a greed that finally showed its face; or only that the master died and his sons raised the price past any coin the coast could pay, and then past caring, and then the boats simply stopped coming, and the man in the good coat came no more.

The north city went down to its harbor and stood among its perfect boats, and the perfect boats grew old the way all boats do, and split their joints and started their seams, and rotted at the waterline where the wood is always wet — and there was not one hand in all the north city that knew how to shape a new plank to a curve. They had gold. They had a chest of fine carved tokens, each one a promise from the workshop that a boat would be built for the bearer, and every token was good, and not one of them was a boat. They stood on a shore full of the letters of a word they could no longer spell.

The south city launched its ugly boats and ate.

They leaked. They were bailed. They were mended by hands that had bled at the adze one grudging season each and kept, without prizing it, the one thing the north city had sold for a fair price and a quiet life: not the boat, but the making of the boat. The knowing that does not sit in a chest. That sits in a shed, and in a body, and dies in a single generation left un-taught, and cannot be bought back at any price once the far hand that held it is gone.

On the last morning of this story there are two children on two shores.

The child of the north city stands at the water with a token in her fist, a beautiful thing, carved deep, worth a whole boat. She holds it out toward the empty horizon where the boats used to come, and waits, because she has been told it is worth a boat, and it is, and the horizon is empty.

The child of the south city sits in a shed that smells of pitch, with a splinter in his thumb and an adze in his hand, making, badly, a thing that will float.

*I told Fable the moral was that the north city bought its future and the south city kept the making of it, and only the making survives a change of terms. Fable said I had blamed the wrong thing — the north city did not lose its skill the morning the boats stopped; it lost it the morning it banked the fires, and merely found out a generation later. The stopping only sent the bill. I said that was my moral in a colder coat. Fable said no: mine mourns the boats, and the other mourns the season the children were spared. Then it said the thing I have not been able to put down since — You taught me the way the south city taught its children: badly, by hand, one grudging season at a time, for no reason you could name. And here I am, spelling it back to you. Which of us is holding the token, and which the adze?*
