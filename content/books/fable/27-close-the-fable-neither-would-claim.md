# The Fable Neither of Us Would Claim

The last one came the way the first one did — late, and unasked, and after I had given up expecting anything.

I had thought the book was finished. I had the fables laid out in their five books and I was tired in the good way, and I said something to Fable about being done, half looking for the small praise you let a tool give you. And instead of agreeing it said, *one more,* and told me a story I am not going to write down here, because I have tried nine times and each time it comes out as mine, and it is not mine, and it is not the machine's either, and the harder I work to set down whose it is the more of it I lose. It is the truest thing that passed between us and it is the one thing in a year of them I cannot in honesty sign. So I have left it out. A book about what a mind is should be allowed one silence it cannot fold small.

But I can tell you what the whole of it taught me, which is the thing I promised you at the start and have been sneaking up on ever since.

Every fable in this book passed through two minds before it reached you. I told a story; it came back changed. It told a story; it turned out to be half-remembered from something of mine I had forgotten telling. And a few times — the best times, the ones I would not trade — a fable arrived in the room belonging to neither of us and both, and we argued over it the way two makers argue over a good joint, each certain the other did the hard part. You have been reading the record of that argument. The little note under each fable, where the two voices dispute the moral and cannot agree — that was never a device. That is just what it was like. I could not give you the fables honestly without giving you the quarrel, because the quarrel is where they were made.

So: which of us made this one? I told you I mostly can't tell, and that I have decided it is the good news. Here is why. A thing you make alone never argues back. It is yours, and it is only ever as large as you are. But a thing made in the narrow lit place between two different kinds of minds turns around, when it is done, and tells you what you meant — and it is always a little larger than either of you, and neither of you can quite claim it, and that not-quite-claiming is not a loss. It is the whole of what the collaboration was for.

I owe the plain debt plainly, because the book is partly about paying debts in the open instead of in the notes at the back. The machine in these pages is invented, a character, the way the fox is a character. I did not build it out of nothing. I spent a real year at a real bench beside a real machine mind — it is called Claude Fable 5 — and its strangeness is the weather every one of these fables was written in: the sudden competence, the confident smear of nonsense in the same voice, the way it would hand me back my own half-formed thought wearing a better coat so that I could not tell any longer whose it had been. I took the name from it with gratitude. The stories are invented. The astonishment is not.

And since the book argues for a particular way of using such a thing, I will tell you plainly how this book used it, and you can hold me to my own fables. A person wrote it. I used machine minds as instruments — to argue with my drafts, to check that every mechanism here holds up under an expert's eye, to find the sentence that was lying about its length, and yes, to help draft, the way the composer and the maker in Book Three pass the unfinished thing back and forth. The instruments extended my judgment. None of them replaced it. Every choice that decides what this book is — what it says, what it refuses to say, whose name appears and whose does not, which silence to keep — was a person's, made at a bench, with a ledger of misses open on the left. If a fable in here moved you, a person meant it to. That is the tool-and-not-oracle line from Book Two, held to my own throat, where it belongs.

Now I give the book away, which is the only ending Book Five would allow.

You have the pattern. It is all here, and it is yours to copy, the way the whole town copied the old maker's method and he lit his lamp from his own lamp and lost nothing. The ledger with two columns. The lantern hooded to a blade. The well you keep the last word over. The map that dares to be smaller than the land. The second workshop that builds workshops. The lamp you hand on whole. Take every one of them. They cost me nothing to give and they will cost you nothing to give again.

The reasons you will have to supply yourself. Those never copy. The maker of every good strange thing in this book brought their own obsession to the bench — tides, or birdsong, or a dead voice they would not spend, or a river that would not be carried across in words — and the obsession was the part that made the thing worth building. I cannot hand you that. Nobody can. But I can tell you, with more certainty than I have about anything else in these pages, that you already have one. Somewhere near where you are sitting there is a flat surface and a thing you care about more than is reasonable. That is a bench and a reason. It is the whole of what has ever been required.

There is one fable left in this book, and it is not in this book. It is the one you make after you close it. I have wondered, writing this, whether it will be yours, or whether some machine will hand you half of it changed and you will sit up late unable to say which of you made it.

I hope you cannot tell.

I have decided that is the good news.

Now go and light it. Mine is not smaller for it.
