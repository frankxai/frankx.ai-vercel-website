# Chapter 7 — The Grain of the Voice

The customer said it the way men say things they have rehearsed on the road.

"I do not want a viol from you. I want your viol. The one you keep."

He had come up the stairs with two servants and a coat worth more than the workbench, and he stood in the shop the way rich men stand in shops, kindly, as if visiting the past. He was not unpleasant. He knew instruments the way he knew horses, which is to say by their reputations, and Anselm's viol had one: the viol he had built in his fortieth year and never sold, the one he took down when the shop was closed and the harbor quiet.

"It is not for sale," Anselm said.

"I know. That is why I am not asking you to make me one." The customer's eyes went, politely, to the walnut cabinet in the corner. "I am told your machine has listened to that viol for years. That it forgets nothing that matters. I am told it can hold a thing the way a chart holds a coast." He smiled. "Let it render your viol, and let some competent pair of hands cut what it renders. I will pay for the wood, the hands, and the fortnight. You need not touch it. In fact I would prefer you didn't. I want the thing itself, exact, without the moods of a maker in it."

Ida, at the glue pot, made a sound she turned into a cough.

Anselm should have said no, and knew it, and did not, because under the insult was a question he had been circling for a year and here was a man offering to pay for the answer. What was in the viol? If the box could hold the sea's behavior in something smaller than the sea, could it hold his?

"A fortnight," he said. "And you take what you get."

For two weeks the viol lived under Fable's horn. They put it through everything a viol can do. Ida bowed long notes on every string at every strength while the stylus wrote. She tapped the belly and the back the way Anselm had taught her, knuckle and fingertip, and the box heard in each tap what a sounding line hears when it finds bottom: how deep, just there. Anselm played the old dances on it in the evenings, and scales in the mornings, and once, when he thought no one was listening, the slow air his father used to play, and the stylus wrote all of it without comment, because commenting was not a thing it did.

At the end of the fortnight the machine had covered forty sheets. The outline of the body, true to a hair. The rise of the arching, drawn in curves like the lines on a chart. And a map of the plates that made Ida whistle: the thickness of the wood at every point, found from the outside by asking, in taps and tones, a thousand small questions and writing down every answer. Anselm checked ten of the figures with his calipers through the sound holes. He could not fault one.

A joiner from the boatyard cut it. He was a careful man with no opinions about music, which the customer counted a virtue. Ida checked each piece against the sheets. Anselm kept his word and kept his hands in his pockets, and it cost him more than he let anyone see.

***

The copy was finished on a Thursday, strung on a Friday, and by the following month, when the varnish had settled enough to be honest, it was, by every measure in the shop, the same viol twice.

Anselm hung a sheet across the middle of the workshop and had the fiddler play behind it. Half the town seemed to have found a reason to be in the shop that evening; word travels along a harbor faster than weather. The fiddler played the same dance on each instrument, twice over, in an order only he knew.

The copy was good. Anselm heard it and made himself say it inwardly, honestly, the way he would measure a rival's work: even across the strings, quick to speak, nothing false in it anywhere. It filled the room the way a well-made thing fills a room.

Then the fiddler crossed to the other instrument, and an old woman by the door, who had been minding her knitting through everything, stopped knitting.

That was the whole difference, and it did not show up on any gauge. People nodded along with one viol. They turned toward the other. When the sheet came down, nobody could say what they had heard, only which one they had heard it from, and they were right eighteen times in twenty.

The customer, to his credit, laughed. He had picked wrong both times.

"Then I have lost nothing," he said, and it was true, and he knew things of that kind precisely. "In my hall it will do everything I will ever ask of it. It is your ears that are expensive, not my evenings." He paid in full, and paid the joiner well, and left the copy to cure another week, and went down the stairs a satisfied man, having bought, at a fair price, everything the machine had heard.

Ida waited until the door was shut.

"It's dead," she said. "It's perfect and it's dead, and I checked every measure myself. So the machine missed something."

"Maybe," Anselm said. He was looking at the two instruments lying side by side on the bench like a man and his portrait. "Or maybe it didn't."

***

They opened the shutters for the sea air and worked by lamp, the way he went at a cracked soundboard, slowly and in order. If the machine had missed something, it would be findable. A wrong thickness. A wrong curve. Some whisper of a difference between the sheets and the wood.

They measured until midnight and found the difference nowhere. The copy was not almost his viol. Feature by feature, it was his viol. That was when the search turned strange, because everything they found had been copied faithfully, and the more faithfully a thing had been copied, the worse Anselm felt looking at it.

He put the calipers on the bass shoulder of each belly, where every rule and every master says to thin the plate, and both read thick. On his viol he knew why. The plank he had cut that belly from, forty years ago, had a run of soft grain through that shoulder where the tree had healed some old wound, and he had left the wood heavy there to carry it, against the rule, against his own gauge, one afternoon's argument between his thumb and the timber that the timber won. The copy's plank had no soft grain. Its shoulder was sound. And there the thickness sat anyway, exact to the sheet, a bandage on unbroken skin.

He turned the copy to the light and looked at the varnish, and the varnish was his, the slow oil he had ground and cooked the way he always did, months to cure, amber going toward brown. That too had been copied, because his recipes were no secret. What was not in the varnish, because it had never been anywhere the machine could hear, was the winter he was twenty-six and broke, when a dealer from the capital offered him double his year for work finished in hard spirit varnish, bright as sugar glaze, dry in a week and choking the wood by year ten. He had seen a choked instrument once, opened it, smelled it. He said no to the dealer, and ate badly that winter, and said no again the next. The copy wore the varnish. It had not eaten badly for it.

And inside, near the tail block, where he could just reach with two fingers and a lamp angled right, he found the scrape. His viol carried one there, a rough stroke he had never smoothed, and he had stopped smoothing it on purpose: as a young man he had chased a scratch like it through a belly, thinning and thinning to erase it, until the plate went papery and the whole month's work was kindling. Since then he left the tolerable flaw where it lay, to remember what erasing costs. The machine had heard Ida's taps ring a shade different over that spot, and the sheets had recorded it, and the joiner, honest man, had carved a scrape to match. It sat in the copy in exactly the right place. A scar, without the wound.

Ida had been quiet a long time, following his fingers from feature to feature. Now she asked the question the whole night had been walking toward.

"Then what did it copy?"

Anselm laid the sheets out on the bench, all forty, the outline and the arching and the thousand-answered map of the plates, the most complete account of a viol anyone had ever made.

"Everything I did," he said.

She waited. He was a while answering, because he was counting, and the count was long. The varnish not bought. The fat commissions turned away in the years when refusing them hurt. The fortnights not saved. The scratch not chased. The rule not followed, that one afternoon, because the wood asked him not to. Forty years of noes, most of them small, none of them written down anywhere, each one made against a temptation the machine had never heard and paid for out of a life it had never lived. What was left after all that subtraction was the viol. The viol was the remainder.

"It never saw what I didn't do," he said.

Ida looked at the copy, and he watched her understand it, the way she understood things, all at once and slightly angry. "The numbers were answers," she said. "It kept the answers and lost the questions. And then it put the answers on wood that was asking different ones."

"So nothing is missing from it," Anselm said. "Everything is missing from it. Both of those are true, and I have been up half the night learning that they can be."

***

He showed her the same trick outside the shop a week later, because by then he was seeing it everywhere, and had begun to suspect it was one of the world's regular jokes.

The old harbor pilot had retired that spring, and had left his chart to the young man who took his place: thirty years of soundings and crossings marked in the old man's own hand, every shoal, every note. The young pilot had the chart, and studied it, and was diligent, and in his first month he put a hull on the bar in weather the old man would have read from his kitchen window. Nothing on the chart was wrong. The chart held every mark the old man's years had made. It did not hold the years. The marks were where his judgment had landed; the judgment was the ten thousand crossings that told him, on a given gray morning, which of his own marks to trust and which to doubt. The young pilot would get it. He would get it the only way it comes, crossing by crossing, and no faster for holding the finest chart on the coast.

"So the customer bought a dead man's chart," Ida said.

"The customer bought a good viol," Anselm said, because fairness mattered to him and the copy was good, and would be loved in that far hall by people it would never disappoint. "He got everything my viol is. Every measure of it."

They were passing the shop window, and his own viol hung inside in the dusk, the heavy-shouldered one, the scarred one, the remainder of everything he had declined, and he thought, but did not say, because it was still only the size of a doubt: the machine held it for a fortnight and gave back all of what it is. What it is for was never in the room. It goes home under my chin. It would take the machine forty years to hear the difference, and it would have to spend them the way I spent them.

He did not say it. He noted where the thought lay, the way he noted soft grain, as a thing the coming years would ask about.

> **From the workbench**
>
> The work you admire is a remainder. What you can see of it, all of it, every measure, is what was left after someone spent years saying no: to the faster finish, to the paying shortcut, to the rule that didn't fit the wood in front of them. You can study the thing they made and take every answer it holds, and you should, and you will still be holding answers to questions your own work has not asked you yet. Nobody hands you your voice. You subtract your way to it, one refusal at a time, and each refusal has to cost you something or it teaches you nothing.
>
> So start the ledger the machine can't hear. Tonight, write down one thing you refused in your work this year, the shortcut, the easy client, the flaw you chased too far, and one line on what it cost and why you paid. Keep it where you work. Add to it. That page is the part of your work that will never be in the work, and it is the only part that was ever yours to build.
