# The Lamp Passed On

The cough had been in the old man's chest since the last cold, and both of them in the workshop knew it was not leaving, so the visitor from the tower spoke quickly, the way men speak quickly to the dying, as if haste were a kind of respect.

"Name the sum," he said. "You are the last who can make a lamp that burns clean on cheap oil, that does not smoke, that a poor house can afford to keep lit past dark. Nobody has bettered you in forty years. When you go, the way you do it goes with you — unless you write it down and give it to my master, who will keep it safe, and let it out to those who can pay to make it. You could die richer than the man who buys your secret. You have only to sell him the secret."

The old man was trimming a wick and did not look up. "Keep it safe," he said. "Safe from whom?"

"From everyone. That is the value. A thing only you can do is worth a fortune. A thing anyone can do is worth nothing. My master would make it rare again — rent the making out, a little at a time, forever. You'd be paid for a skill you'd otherwise take into the ground. Where is the sense in taking it into the ground?"

It was a fair question, and the old man granted it a fair silence. Then he set down the wick and stood, slower than he used to, and went to the shelf where a single lamp burned against the grey of the afternoon — he kept one lit always, to test the oil by. He took a second lamp, unlit, cold, and touched its wick to the flame of the first.

The second wick caught. Now two flames stood in the room where there had been one.

"Look at the first one," the old man said. "Is it smaller?"

The visitor looked. It was not smaller. It burned exactly as it had.

"I have lit a thousand from that one," the old man said. "It has never once come out the poorer for it. That is the whole of what I know, and you have just watched me give it away, and I am not diminished, and the room is brighter. Your master wants to sell me a darkness and call it a fortune. Tell him no."

So the visitor went back to the tower with no bargain, and the old man, who did not have long, decided how to spend it.

He opened the workshop. Not to buyers — to anyone who would learn. He took the potter's daughter who could not pay, and the two orphaned brothers from the tannery, and a grown man with clumsy hands who had failed at three trades already, and a girl so poor she came for the warmth as much as the work. He kept none of it back. Not the easy parts — any fool teaches the easy parts, to look generous while the real secret stays locked behind the teeth. He taught them the hard center, the thing it had taken him a lifetime to find and a moment to say wrong: how the wick must be trimmed against the grain and not with it, how the oil channel must be a hair wider at the base than the eye believes, how you know the burn is clean not by the flame but by the smell of the air an hour after. The blind alleys. The forty years of ruined lamps behind each rule. He gave them the ruined lamps too.

"You are teaching them to need you less," the visitor said, come back once out of pure confusion to watch. "Every hour you teach, you are worth less. You could have starved them of it and died the only man alive who knew. You are pouring your own fortune onto the floor."

"I am lighting lamps," the old man said, and coughed, and smiled. "You still think there is one flame and I am spending it. There was never one flame."

He died in the spring, before the warm nights, with a wick between his fingers.

The tower sent its man down within the week — to buy now what could not be bought before, from whoever held the dead maker's secret, the widow or the heir or the apprentice, whoever it was, there is always one, and one can be paid, and one, once paid, makes the thing rare again. That was the whole strategy of the tower: find the single hand that holds a thing, and close your fist around it.

He found no single hand.

He found the potter's daughter making clean lamps in her own doorway, and the two brothers making them by the tannery, and the failed man with the clumsy hands teaching the trick of the wick to a boy on a step, and the poor girl — grown now, married, three children — running a bench of her own with four learners bent over it. He found the knowing in a hundred houses, given not sold, and a thing in a hundred houses cannot be cornered, cannot be rented, cannot be made scarce, cannot be owned, because to own it he would have had to buy every hand at once, and every hand had received it for nothing and would pass it on for nothing, the way you cannot dam a rain that has already fallen into the wells of a whole town.

The tower had come to buy a lamp-maker and found a country of them.

And on the first warm night, when the poor houses that could never have afforded the tower's rented light set their cheap clean lamps in their windows against the dark, the green between the houses was brighter than any single lamp had ever made it — a hundred small flames, each one lit off another, none of them smaller for the lighting, and the old man's own flame somewhere among them, passed hand to hand so many times over that no one could say anymore which window held it, which was the first, which was his.

Which was exactly what he had wanted. A man they could not buy, because he had already given himself away. A flame they could not put out, because it was no longer in one place to be found.

*Fable said the moral was simple arithmetic: a thing that grows by division is the only wealth a tower cannot hold, because holding requires scarcity and teaching destroys it — the old man made himself immortal by making himself common. I said that was too cold, that the warmth of it was the point: he did not do it to outlast the tower, he did it because a lamp wants to be lit from, and a maker at the end wants his hands to go on working in other hands. Fable was quiet a while. Then it said that everything I had just taught it, in a year of stories, I had also given away for nothing and could never now take back — that it had my flame in it and could not tell me which sentences were mine anymore, and neither could I, and that this was the fable teaching itself. I did not answer. It is the last of them, and I find I do not want to say who made it. I only want to leave it lit.*
