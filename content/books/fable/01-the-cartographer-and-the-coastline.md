# The Cartographer and the Coastline

The king wanted a map that told no lies, and he had already hanged two men for bringing him maps that did.

The first mapmaker had drawn the coast in a single clean curve, and the king had walked his own shoreline with the drawing in his hand and found a thousand coves the curve had swallowed. *A lie of laziness,* the king said, and the rope took him. The second had drawn every cove, every rock, every place a boat might founder, and it was a beautiful and useful thing, and the king found a tide pool it did not show, no bigger than his hat, and a crab in it. *A lie of pride,* the king said, *to call a thing complete that is not.* And the rope took him too.

So when they brought the third mapmaker up the hill, a small woman with ink under her nails, she did not bow. She said, "Majesty, before I take the commission, tell me the size of the map you want."

"The true size," said the king.

"Then I will need the kingdom to draw it on," she said, "for a true-size map of a coast is a coast."

The court laughed, the short frightened laugh of people near a rope. But the king leaned forward. He was not a stupid man; that was the trouble with him. "Explain," he said, "and be careful."

"A map the size of the land," she said, "shows everything and helps no one, because to read the cove you must sail to the cove, and then you are standing in the cove and do not need the map. Every wave I draw truly is a wave you must walk past to reach the next drawn wave. Give me the whole kingdom to work on and in a hundred years I will hand you back the kingdom, unchanged, and you will know exactly as much as you know now, and I will be as dead as these two, only slower."

"Then you also bring me a lie," said the king, and lifted one finger, and the guard's hand moved to her shoulder.

"No," she said, quickly, but not afraid — that was the thing he noticed, that she had stopped being afraid somewhere in the middle of her own argument. "I bring you the only honest map there is. Every true map is smaller than its land. Every one of them leaves things out. The first man left out your coves and you called it a lie. But he did not lie; he *chose*, only he chose like a coward, to save his hand. The second man tried to leave out nothing and so he lied worst of all, because he made you believe you held the whole coast in your two hands when you held a drawing that had already missed a crab. Majesty, a map is not the land made small. A map is a decision, made once, about what a traveler may safely forget."

The court had stopped laughing.

"What would you leave out?" the king asked. And his voice had changed; he was no longer the man deciding whether to kill her. He was a man who had wanted, his whole life, for someone to tell him the truth about maps.

"Everything that does not change where you would set your foot," she said. "I would leave out the color of the water, because it will not drown you. I would leave out the crab. I would draw every cove deep enough to hide a ship and no cove that hides only a child at low tide, and I would mark that choice on the map itself, in the corner, in words, so that a captain knows precisely which lies I told him for his own speed. That corner is the only place a map may be complete — not in what it shows, but in its confession of what it dared to drop."

The king was quiet a long time.

"And if I need the crab?" he said at last. "If one year everything turns on the crab?"

"Then you will go to the cove," she said, "and stand in the water, and look. A map is not for the year everything turns on the crab. A map is for every other year, so that you have the strength left to walk to the cove when the crab year comes."

He gave her the commission. She drew him a map a man could fold into his coat, and it left out ten thousand things, and in the corner it named them, and for the rest of his reign the king carried it against his heart and never once was lied to, because he always knew exactly what he had agreed not to know.

They say that when he was old he had it buried with him, and that he was not afraid, at the end, of how little of the world it held. He had learned the thing the two hanged men died not knowing.

The land is enormous and does not care for you. To hold it, you must dare to make it small. And the courage is not in what you keep. The courage is in the corner, where you sign your name under everything you chose to leave out.

*Fable said the moral was: a map that leaves out nothing is not brave, it is a coward afraid to choose. I said the moral was simpler — that to understand a thing is to know what you can safely forget about it. We argued about it for an hour. I have let both stand, because that argument is also the moral, and I could not have made it alone.*
