# Building in Public

> "Generosity of spirit is a business strategy disguised as a character trait."
> — Unknown

---

I open-sourced ACOS on a Tuesday. By Thursday, someone in Singapore had forked it, adapted it for their creative practice, and sent me a message thanking me. By the following week, three people had mentioned the framework in blog posts. By the end of the month, frankx.ai had more traffic than it had received in the previous quarter combined.

I had given away my best work. And the return was immediate, compound, and completely asymmetric to anything I had achieved by keeping work private.

This chapter is about building in public — what it is, why it works, and how to do it without accidentally undermining yourself in the process.

---

## I. The Counterintuitive Decision

When I decided to make ACOS publicly available — the full framework, 250+ pages of configuration, 75 skills, 38 agent definitions, every CLAUDE.md file, every SKILL.md document — several people in my network thought it was a mistake. You've spent hundreds of hours on this. Anyone can just copy it. Why would you give that away?

The question assumes scarcity. It assumes that if someone else has your framework, you have less of it. That if a competitor can read your approach, they can replicate your advantage.

This is the wrong mental model for knowledge work in 2026.

The ACOS framework has no marginal cost to copy. Anyone who wants to screenshot it, read it, fork it, or build from it can do so regardless of whether I publish it. The information either reaches the people who can benefit from it, or it sits on my hard drive and helps no one. There is no middle option where I keep it private and maintain a sustainable competitive advantage.

What I actually have — and what cannot be copied — is the depth behind the framework. The 12,000 hours of AI production experience that informed each decision. The specific configuration choices I made and why. The ability to debug, extend, and evolve the system as AI capabilities change. The accumulated context that lives in my head and my trajectory files, not in any single document.

Giving away the framework makes me the most credible authority on it. Because I built it. And everyone who reads it, uses it, or references it amplifies that signal.

## II. GitHub as Portfolio

My GitHub is not a repository of code projects. It is a portfolio of thinking.

The ACOS repository contains 250+ pages of documentation. Each SKILL.md file is a specification — precise, structured, detailed. Each CLAUDE.md is a configuration document that demonstrates systems thinking applied to a creative practice. Each agent definition shows how I decompose complex work into specialized roles with defined capabilities and boundaries.

A hiring manager at an AI company who reads three pages of ACOS documentation knows something about how I think that no resume can communicate. A founder considering a coaching engagement who reads the full framework knows exactly what level of thinking they would be paying for. A prospective collaborator who explores the agent architecture understands the methodology before the first meeting.

This is what a GitHub portfolio does for technical work. It is a working demonstration, not a description of a demonstration. The difference matters.

The conventional wisdom says: don't show your process, only show finished products. The better wisdom, for anyone building at the frontier of AI systems design, is the opposite. Your process *is* the product. The thinking behind the architecture *is* the work. Showing that thinking — in detail, publicly, with all the decisions visible — is the most credible form of self-presentation available.

My 75 skills are not just tools. They are evidence of a design methodology: structured prompting, domain-scoped activation, layered expertise. My 38 agents are not just configurations. They are an argument for how specialized roles outperform generalist AI use. Every file in the public repository makes that argument more concrete.

## III. The Compound Returns of Transparency

Trust accumulates in public. It does not accumulate in private.

When I publish a SKILL.md file, someone reads it and decides I understand AI systems design. When I publish the full ACOS documentation, they decide I understand it at depth. When they find that the framework actually works — when they implement a skill and it behaves as described, when they apply the six-pillar architecture and their creative output improves — the trust compounds. They recommend it to someone else. That person reads more. The network effect begins.

This is qualitatively different from the trust signals that private work can generate. A portfolio of client testimonials says: other people found this person useful. A public framework that works says: this person built something real, and you can verify it yourself, right now, for free, with no commitment.

The transparency dividend extends beyond individual trust. Community contributions emerge from public work. In the months following ACOS's public release, I received: framework extensions I hadn't thought of, integrations with tools I don't use, corrections to edge cases I had missed, and translations into approaches for domains beyond my own. The community became a quality improvement mechanism that I could never replicate alone.

And there is the hiring signal. Every senior AI role I have been approached for in the last eighteen months has come with a reference to something public — an article, the framework, a GitHub file. The visibility of public work is a durable asset. It compounds over time in ways that private reputation, built through referrals and relationships alone, cannot match.

## IV. Building in Public Is Not Self-Promotion

This distinction is precise and important: building in public means showing your work. Self-promotion means claiming results.

Showing your work: Here is the SKILL.md file I wrote for SEO content writing. Here is the agent definition I use for music production. Here is the CLAUDE.md that governs my content governance system. Here is how the six-pillar architecture maps to personal use.

Claiming results: I am the best AI architect. My framework is more advanced than anything else available. My system outperforms everything in the market.

The first earns trust. The second repels it. The difference is evidence.

When I publish a blog post titled "How I Built a Personal AI CoE for $50/month," the title makes a specific, verifiable claim. The article walks through every component: the tools, the costs, the configurations, the tradeoffs. Readers can verify every number. They can replicate the setup. The claim is earned by the evidence that follows it.

Self-promotion substitutes assertion for evidence. Building in public provides the evidence and lets readers draw their own conclusions. Readers who draw their own conclusions trust those conclusions far more than conclusions they are told to reach.

The discipline of building in public is to resist the impulse to editorialize your own work. Let the framework speak. Let the configurations demonstrate. Let the outputs — 12,000+ songs, 90+ published articles, 250 pages of working documentation — make the case that something here is worth paying attention to.

## V. Documentation as Marketing

The most underrated marketing asset in technical work is documentation.

My CLAUDE.md files are not internal configuration documents. They are discovery surfaces. When someone searches for how to configure Claude Code for a content workflow, they find articles that reference CLAUDE.md patterns. When someone explores GitHub for AI workflow documentation, they find files that explain the approach in detail. When someone reads about the Personal AI CoE and wants to understand how it is implemented, they find documentation that shows, not just describes.

The SKILL.md format is similarly both technical and marketing. A SKILL.md specifies how an AI skill should activate and behave — it is a precise technical document. It is also a demonstration of thinking that creates authority in the domain it covers. Every skill I have published — seo-content-writer, frankx-brand, music-producer, frontend-designer — is simultaneously a tool and a signal about the level of craft involved in building these systems.

README files, configuration examples, documented decision logs — all of these function as content marketing without requiring the framing of content marketing. They are documentation first. Marketing is the side effect of documentation being useful and visible.

The pattern: write documentation as if it will be read by a technically sophisticated stranger who is evaluating whether your thinking is worth their time. Because that is exactly who reads it.

## VI. What to Share and What to Keep Private

The decision framework is simple, but it requires honesty about what actually constitutes competitive advantage.

**Share:** Code. Frameworks. Configuration patterns. Methodology. Architecture decisions and the reasoning behind them. Technical approaches that others can adapt. Documentation of systems that work.

**Share:** Strategy (the general approach). Philosophy (why you make the choices you make). Process (how you work through problems). Lessons from failure.

**Keep private:** Revenue figures. Client details and identifying information. Proprietary business intelligence that a competitor could directly act on. Personal information that belongs to others.

The test: does sharing this help other people and demonstrate your capability? Then share it. Does sharing this expose someone else's private information or give a direct commercial advantage to a competitor without any corresponding benefit to your audience? Then keep it private.

Most people invert this. They keep methodology private because it feels like the crown jewel, and they share surface-level results because those feel safe. The actual dynamic is opposite. Methodology demonstrated publicly builds authority. Results claimed without methodology are easy to dismiss.

The ACOS framework is my methodology. It is the most valuable thing I could publish because it shows how I think. Anyone can read it, but the only people who can replicate the depth behind it are people who have done the equivalent volume of work to develop the same intuitions. The framework is a map, not the territory. The territory is experience, and experience is not copyable.

## VII. The 1000 True Fans Applied to Open Source

Kevin Kelly's 1000 True Fans essay describes a model for independent creative sustainability: 1,000 people who love what you do and will pay for it. At $100 per fan per year, that is $100,000 annually — enough to sustain a creative practice.

The essay is usually discussed in terms of audience building. But it applies with full force to open-source technical work.

Open-sourcing ACOS does not require 1,000 true fans. It requires a smaller number of people who understand the framework deeply enough to value consulting, coaching, or extended products built on top of it. Every person who reads the documentation, implements a skill, or adapts the architecture for their own practice is a potential true fan — not because they will necessarily buy anything, but because they now have the context to understand the depth of what is available if they want to go further.

The funnel: public documentation creates awareness. Working implementations create trust. Trust creates the conditions for true fans to emerge. True fans generate revenue through products, coaching, and direct collaboration.

ACOS as a public framework drives traffic to frankx.ai. That traffic converts into newsletter subscribers. Subscribers see the book. Readers who finish the book see the coaching program. Coaching clients are, by the time they apply, already deep believers — they have read the documentation, tested the framework, followed the thinking. The conversation starts with trust fully established.

This is the difference between open-source as charity and open-source as strategy. The free work is genuine — I publish it because it is useful and because getting these frameworks to more people matters. But it also functions as the top of a funnel that is entirely self-qualifying. Only people serious about implementing enterprise-grade AI architecture for their personal practice reach the bottom of that funnel. Those are exactly the people worth working with.

## VIII. Closing the Loop: Public Work and the Personal AI CoE

Here is what building in public has to do with the framework that runs through this entire book.

The Personal AI CoE is not a private system. It is, by its nature, a demonstration system. The six pillars — Strategy, Governance, Talent, Technology, Data, Ethics — are most powerful when they are visible. When the strategy is documented and public, it forces clarity. When the governance is transparent, it creates accountability. When the talent architecture is openly described, it invites others to build on it.

The open-source strategy is not a separate decision from the CoE architecture. It is an expression of the Data pillar: your published work is data that compounds. It is an expression of the Governance pillar: public documentation creates a standard you are accountable to. It is an expression of the Talent pillar: a public agent library attracts contributions from others who extend your capability.

Most importantly, building in public is an expression of the Ethics pillar. The enterprise AI CoE has ethics embedded in its governance because powerful systems need transparency to remain trustworthy. The personal AI CoE has the same requirement. When you publish your framework openly — when anyone can read how your system makes decisions, what values it encodes, what constraints it operates under — you are doing the most honest thing a builder can do.

You are letting the work speak.

Not what you claim about the work. Not a description of the work. The work itself, available for inspection, replication, criticism, and improvement.

That is the strategy. And it is the most powerful one available.
