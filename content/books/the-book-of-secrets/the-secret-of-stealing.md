# The Secret of Stealing

The gallery opens at ten, and by half past the young man has already been there an hour, because he talked the guard into letting him in early and the guard, who had seen this kind of thing before and knew roughly where it led, decided not to mind.

He is copying a painting. Not photographing it, not sketching a loose impression of it to take home, but copying it the slow way, the old way, the way an apprentice would have three hundred years ago — a small canvas of his own propped on a folding easel, a mirror of the master's picture growing on it inch by inch, one matched color at a time. He has been at this particular painting for four mornings. He is not enjoying it the way he thought he would. What he is discovering, which no book ever quite told him, is that copying a great picture is not flattery and is not laziness. It is closer to surgery. To lay down the same stroke the master laid down, you have to reconstruct the decision behind it: why the light breaks there and not a thumb's width over, why that shadow is warm where every instinct says it should be cool, why a whole edge has been left deliberately unfinished so the eye finishes it and believes it did the work itself. You cannot copy a choice you do not understand. So you are forced, stroke by stroke, to understand it. The hand teaches the eye what the eye was too polite to notice.

And here is the thing that troubles him, the small shame he would not admit to the guard. He is twenty-three and he wants, more than he wants almost anything, to be *original*. To make a picture that is his and no one else's, that could not be mistaken for any other painter living or dead. And instead he is spending his best morning hours making, as exactly as his skill allows, a copy of a dead man's work. It feels like a betrayal of the very thing he came here to become. It feels like cheating in reverse — like stealing, and then, worse, learning to steal well.

He does not yet know that the copying is not the opposite of becoming original. It is the road to it, and there is no other road. He does not know that every painter he reveres stood, at twenty-three, in front of someone else's picture doing exactly this, and that the ones who refused — the ones who guarded their precious blank originality and would not stoop to copy anyone — are not hanging on these walls. They are not hanging anywhere. Their fear of imitation kept them from the one apprenticeship that could have made them worth imitating.

He thinks he is stealing. He is right. He does not yet know that this is the secret.

## The Pattern

Here is the secret, said as plainly as it can be said. Nothing you will ever make is original in the way you fear you have to be original. There is no such thing as a thing made from nothing. Every maker is a remix of what moved them, and the work you admire most — the work that seems to have arrived from some private planet no one else can visit — is not the exception to this. It is the fullest example of it.

The mechanism is not mystical, and once you see it you cannot unsee it. Originality is not spontaneous generation. It is combination. You take the things that struck you — a writer's sentence rhythm, a photographer's way with shadow, a way your grandmother told a story, a chord change that stopped you in a car park eleven years ago — and you run all of it through the single narrow channel of your particular self, and what comes out the other side carries the specific collision of your influences that no one else on earth is carrying. That collision is your voice. Not the influences themselves; anyone can love the same painters you love. The voice is in the *proportions* — this much of that, filtered through your history, your ear, your limitations, your obsessions — and the proportions are as unrepeatable as a fingerprint because they are made of a life only you have lived.

So the raw material of originality is theft. This is not a scandal. It is the mechanics of the thing.

Now watch what the fear does, because the fear is the whole tragedy of the beginner. The young maker hears "be original" and takes it to mean "owe nothing to anyone." So they try to purge their influences. They hear an echo of a hero in their own work and they flinch and delete it. They avoid the artists they love most, in case they catch something. They try to build a house with no bricks, because every brick came from somewhere and they want the house to be theirs alone. And the result is not originality. The result is thin, reactive, weak work — work that has cut itself off from the entire history of everything good, that stands against its influences instead of on them, and therefore stands for nothing. The purest way to guarantee you sound like no one is also, it turns out, the purest way to guarantee you sound like nothing. Generic is not what happens when you steal too much. Generic is what happens when you are too frightened to steal at all.

The mature maker does the opposite, and does it without guilt. There is an old line, worn smooth by a century of repeating, usually laid at T.S. Eliot's feet, that puts the border exactly where it belongs: the immature artist imitates, and the mature one steals. The writer Austin Kleon gave the same idea its bluntest modern name and built a small book around it — steal like an artist — and the whole trick is folded inside that phrase if you read it slowly. *Like an artist.* Not like a forger. The difference is not whether you take. Everyone takes. The difference is how many places you take from, and what your own hands do to the loot on the way through.

Because here is the part the fearful beginner never gets told. Steal from one source and everyone sees it; you are not a maker, you are a photocopier, and the shame you feel is correct. But steal from twenty sources, from thirty, from a lifetime of them, and something changes in kind, not just in degree. No single theft shows anymore, because each one is diluted by all the others and transformed by the particular self they are all passing through. The critic who could catch you aping one hero cannot catch you when you have swallowed a hundred, because there is no longer a single thread to pull. The theft has become digestion. And digestion is just the honest word for craft.

Which leaves one last turn, and it is the one that should set you free. If everything is stolen, then the only thing that is truly yours is *what you choose to steal.* Your taste in influences — the strange, specific, personal set of things that struck you hard enough to keep — that selection is nobody else's, and it was doing the work of making you original the whole time you were afraid you had none. You do not have to invent your voice out of the void. You have to notice what you already love, gather more of it than you think is decent, and put it all through your own hands until it comes out changed. Copying one master is theft. Digesting many is how every voice you have ever admired was actually made.

## A Second Proof

Consider two young musicians who started the same year, in the same city, playing the same small rooms for the same forty people.

The first was, by every early measure, the more gifted. He had the better ear and the quicker hands, and he had, above all, a fierce and defining commitment to being nobody's imitator. He refused to learn other people's songs — covers were for tribute acts, he said, for people with no ideas of their own. He would not study the players he was compared to, because the comparison offended him; he wanted his sound to owe nothing to anyone. When a friend played him something wonderful, he listened for about a minute and then turned it off, half-afraid it would leak into him. He was going to invent himself from scratch. And for a while the purity of the project was even a little thrilling to watch. He was so determined not to sound like anyone that he genuinely did not sound like anyone.

The trouble was that not sounding like anyone turned out to be very close to not sounding like anything. His songs had no ancestors, and so they had no bones. They wandered. They reached for feelings they had never studied hard enough to earn, and so they gestured at emotion instead of producing it. Every year he got a little more technically able and a little more locked inside his own small vocabulary, because he had cut himself off from the only supply of new moves there is, which is other people's moves. He mistook his isolation for integrity. He would still tell you, now, that he never copied anyone. It is the truest and saddest thing he can say about his work.

The second musician stole everything that was not nailed down.

She learned other people's songs by the hundred, first to play them, then to take them apart — to find out how a particular bridge lifted, how a certain singer sat a hair behind the beat and made a whole room lean in. She stole a guitarist's left hand and a soul singer's phrasing and the drum feel off a genre she was supposedly too cool to like and a lyric habit from a poet who never touched a stage. She stole from the dead and the living and from records her own scene would have sneered at. She kept a running theft — melodies, phrases, tones, whole approaches — and she was not remotely ashamed of it, because she understood something the first musician's pride would never let him learn: that she was not hoarding these things to hand them back. She was running them through herself.

And by the time all of it had passed through her — her voice, her history, her particular set of limitations, the specific accident of which things had struck her and which had not — it came out the far side unmistakably, unarguably hers. You can sit with her records and, if you know the history, hear the three or four bloodlines running underneath. And you can hear, at the very same time, that the result is not any of them, that it belongs to no one who came before, that it could only have been made by exactly one person. The theft did not make her a copyist. The sheer volume and variety of the theft, transformed by the one channel it all had to fit through, is *precisely* what made her original. There was so much of it, from so many places, so thoroughly digested, that no single source could claim her — and a voice that no single source can claim is the only kind of voice there is.

The difference between them was never talent. The first man had more. The difference was that one treated influence as a contamination to be kept out, and the other treated it as food to be taken in. And the one who guarded his purity most fiercely was the one who stayed, to the end, most generic — while the shameless thief became the only thing either of them ever wanted to be, which was someone you could not mistake for anyone else.

## The Secret, Named

> You are not going to make something out of nothing. No one ever has. Put down that impossible burden; it was never yours to carry, and it has been crushing you.

> What you will make instead is a combination — your influences, run through the one narrow channel of you, colliding in proportions no one else alive is carrying. That collision is your voice. It was never going to arrive from a void. It was always going to be built out of what you love.

> So collect. Steal openly and steal widely. Take the sentence, the shadow, the chord, the way a stranger told a story on a train. Gather far more than feels respectable, from far more places than feels safe.

> Because theft from one source is copying, and everyone can see it, and the shame you would feel is right. But theft from a hundred sources, digested through your own hands, is the honest name for craft — and no single theft shows, because there is no longer a single thread to pull.

> What you choose to steal is the one thing that is truly yours. Your taste in influences is your originality, doing its work quietly while you were busy being afraid you had none.

> The fear of imitation does not protect your voice. It starves it. The maker who guards against all influence ends up standing for nothing, because they are standing on nothing.

> Copy the masters until their moves live in your hands. Then steal from so many of them, so completely, that what comes out could only have come from you.

## The Practice

This week, start stealing on purpose, and keep a record of it.

Take the maker whose work you love most and are most afraid of sounding like — the one you have been quietly avoiding in case it rubs off. Stop avoiding them. Go straight at them. Copy something of theirs by hand, the slow way, the whole way through: type out the chapter, replay the passage until your fingers know it, redraw the frame, rebuild the paragraph stroke by stroke. You are not trying to keep the copy. You are trying to reconstruct the decisions inside it, because you cannot copy a choice you do not understand, and the copying is what forces the understanding. Throw the copy away when you are done. Keep what it taught your hands.

Then widen the theft so no single source can own you. Make a list — a real one, on paper — of ten makers you have stolen from or would like to. Not one hero. Ten, and let them clash: put the ones your own scene would sneer at right next to the ones it worships, put a dead master next to a stranger you overheard. The clash is the point. That specific, personal, slightly embarrassing collection is not a confession of how unoriginal you are. It is the raw signature of how original you already are, because no one else on earth would have assembled exactly that ten.

Now make one small thing this week that runs several of them through you at once. Not a copy of any one of them — a combination, where the guitarist's hand meets the poet's habit meets the accident of your own voice, and something happens in the collision that none of them would have made alone. It will feel like cheating. It is not. It is the only way anything good has ever been made.

The blank page was never asking you to invent the world. It was asking you what you love, and whether you are finally brave enough to take it.
