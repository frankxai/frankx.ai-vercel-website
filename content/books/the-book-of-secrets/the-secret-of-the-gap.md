# The Secret of the Gap

A young woman sits in a dark editing room at two in the morning, watching the worst thing she has ever made.

It is her first short film. Seven minutes long, shot over three weekends with borrowed equipment and friends who worked for pizza. She has just finished the final cut, the one she was so certain of a week ago, and now she is playing it through from the top with her hand over her mouth, and she can see everything that is wrong with it. The cut on the second line lands a beat too late and kills the joke. The lighting in the kitchen scene is flat and televisual when it should have been close and warm. The lead performance she was so proud of on the day now reads, on the screen, as a person acting rather than a person living. The score swells exactly where a truer film would have gone silent. She sees all of it. She sees it with a precision that is almost cruel, the way you can see a stranger's bad haircut and be unable to say anything.

And here is the part that undoes her. She cannot fix any of it.

She knows the cut is late. She does not yet know, in her hands, how to make it land. She can feel that the kitchen wanted a different light, but she could not have lit it that way on the day if her life depended on it, because knowing that a thing is wrong and knowing how to make it right are two different kinds of knowledge, and she has only the first. She has, in fact, an enormous amount of the first. She has spent her whole life absorbing it — a thousand films watched, some of them ten times, the good cuts and the brave silences and the performances that breathe all quietly filed away in her without her ever deciding to file them. Her taste is extraordinary. It is, if anything, the most developed thing about her.

Her taste is precisely what is torturing her.

Because the film in her head — the one assembled out of everything she has ever loved and understood about films — is magnificent. And the film on the screen is the one her hands could actually make this year. The distance between those two films is the whole of her suffering. She turns off the monitor and sits in the dark and arrives, as almost everyone arrives at this moment, at the wrong conclusion.

*I don't have it. I thought I did. I don't.*

## The Pattern

She has it. The disappointment she is feeling is not the absence of talent. It is one of the surest signs of talent there is, and almost no one is told this in time, and so a staggering number of genuinely gifted people quit at exactly the moment the gift was announcing itself.

Here is the mechanism, and it is worth understanding exactly, because misunderstanding it ends careers before they start.

Making anything well requires two separate faculties, and they are built by two separate activities, on two completely different schedules. The first faculty is *taste* — the ability to tell good work from bad, to feel when something is true and when it is false, to know what you are aiming at. The second is *skill* — the ability to actually produce the thing your taste is pointing at. And here is the trap folded into the machinery of it: taste is built by *consuming*, and skill is built by *making*, and you spent years consuming before you ever made a single thing.

Think about how much film that young woman had watched before she ever picked up a camera. Twenty years of it. Twenty years of quietly training the most sophisticated instrument for judging films that a person can own — and all of it, every hour, building taste and not one minute of it building skill, because watching a film teaches you nothing about how to light a kitchen. So when she finally makes something, her taste walks into the room fully grown, a full adult with decades of education, and stands next to her skill, which is a newborn, and the two of them look at the same seven-minute film, and the newborn made it and the adult has to watch.

Of course it disappoints her. It could not fail to disappoint her. Her taste is doing exactly what a fine, well-trained taste is supposed to do: it is recognizing that the work is not yet good. The taste is not broken. The taste is working perfectly. The taste is *right.* The work really isn't good yet. That is not a delusion to argue her out of; it is an accurate report, and its accuracy is the whole point, because the very faculty that is making her miserable is the faculty that will eventually make her excellent, and a person who could not see that the film was bad would have no hope of ever making it good.

This is the thing to hold onto. The gap between your taste and your skill is not a sign you should stop. It is the exact opposite. Only people with real taste feel the gap at all. The people who make bad work and are perfectly happy with it — who show you the thing and beam — are not ahead of you. They are the ones missing the instrument. Your disappointment is that instrument, running. The pain is the taste doing its job. If you did not have the taste, you would not have the pain, and you would also never improve, because you would have no idea which direction improvement was in.

This is very close to something the radio producer Ira Glass has said, and said better than most, in the version of this idea that has traveled the furthest. He put it roughly like this: everybody who does creative work gets into it because they have good taste, but for the first years the work you make is not as good as your taste, and your taste is good enough that you can tell your own work is falling short. Most people, he observed, quit right there. And the only way through it is to make a large volume of work — to put in the years, to close the distance by producing, not by waiting.

That is the honest mechanism, and it explains the cruelty of the beginning. The gap is widest at the start, because that is the moment when your taste is most fully formed relative to your skill — you arrive with twenty years of taste and zero years of skill, and the ratio never looks worse than it does on that first night in the dark editing room. It gets better from there, but only one way. Skill is built by making, so skill climbs only when you make things. It does not climb while you read about making things, or plan to make things, or wait to feel ready to make things. It climbs while your hands are moving and producing work that your taste, standing over your shoulder, keeps judging as not good enough — and that judgment, which feels like a verdict against you, is actually the ladder. Each thing you finish teaches your hands a little of what your taste already knows. The taste waits, holding its position, and the skill climbs, year over year, toward it. The gap closes from below. And it closes only for the people who keep making things through the exact stretch where making things feels most like proof they should stop.

## A Second Proof

Two students enroll in the same film program in the same September. Put them side by side on the first day and you could not tell them apart. Same age, same hunger, same shelf of films they love, same first attempts — two short films, both genuinely bad in the way all first short films are bad, and bad to almost precisely the same degree. If you screened their two films back to back you would struggle to say which showed more promise. They are, for all practical purposes, the same person at the same starting line.

Then each of them watches their own film, and feels the same thing — that lurch of disappointment, that gap opening up between the film they could see in their heads and the film they actually made. And here the two of them, still identical in every measurable way, do something different. Not with their hands. With their reading of the feeling.

The first one reads the disappointment as a verdict. *If I were really talented, my first film would have been good, and it wasn't, so I'm not.* It is a reasonable-sounding thought. It has the shape of honesty, even humility — she is not deluding herself, she is facing facts, she is being realistic about her limits. She tells herself she is simply being mature about it. And on that basis she makes fewer films the next year, and fewer the year after, because why pour yourself into something you've already proven you don't have the gift for, and each film she doesn't make is a rung she doesn't climb, and slowly the not-making becomes the truth she thought she was merely observing. She talks about film for the rest of her life. She has wonderful taste. Everyone agrees she has wonderful taste. She made three films, all before she was twenty-three, and quit.

The second one reads the same disappointment completely differently. She feels the identical gap — she is not braver, not more confident, not spared the two-in-the-morning misery — but she has been told, or she guesses, the one thing that changes everything: that the gap is not a measure of her ceiling but a measure of how far her taste has run ahead of her hands, and that the only thing wrong with her is that her hands are young. So the disappointment, for her, is not a stop sign. It is a distance to be walked. She makes another film. It is also bad, but bad in new places, which is the only evidence of progress that exists at the beginning. She makes another. By the tenth she is doing on purpose small things she used to do by accident. By the thirtieth the gap has closed enough that a stranger, watching, feels something. She is not more gifted than the woman who quit. She was never more gifted. She simply read the same signal as an instruction instead of a verdict, and kept her hands moving through the years when moving them felt pointless.

That is the whole difference between them, and it decided everything, and it was not talent. Both had the taste — that was never in question, they had *identical* taste, that is the entire cruelty and the entire point. The one who made it was not the one who felt less disappointment. She felt exactly as much. She was the one who understood what the disappointment meant, and refused to accept its false confession, and outlasted it.

The tragedy is how much talent quits at this exact spot, convinced by its own good taste that it has none.

## The Secret, Named

> Your work disappoints you because your taste is better than your skill. That is the whole of it, and it is not a flaw in you. It is the natural result of having spent years learning to love good work before you ever tried to make any.

> Taste is built by taking things in. Skill is built by putting things out. You did years of the first before you began the second, so your taste arrived full-grown and your skill arrived a newborn, and now the grown one has to stand and watch the newborn work. Of course it aches. The ache is the two of them being so far apart.

> But hear what the ache actually is. It is your taste, functioning perfectly, correctly reporting that the work is not yet good. Only a real taste can file that report. The people who make poor work and love it are not ahead of you; they are the ones without the instrument. Your disappointment is the instrument, and it is running, and it is right.

> So the gap is not the proof that you cannot. It is the proof that you can — that you have the eye, and only lack, for now, the hand. The eye is the rare thing and you already have it. The hand is the slow thing, and there is exactly one way to build it.

> Make a large volume of work. Close the gap from below, by producing, through all the years it feels like producing is only more evidence against you. The taste will hold its position and wait. The skill will climb. Where they meet is the only place good work has ever come from.

> Most quit here, at the widest part, mistaking the gap for a verdict. Do not. The gap is the beginning of everything. Outlast it.

## The Practice

This week, make something and let it be bad.

Not carelessly bad — you are not trying to fail, you are aiming as high as your taste demands, and your taste will demand a great deal. What you are practicing is finishing a thing your own eye can see falls short, and starting the next one anyway, without holding a private trial in between. The trial is the enemy. The trial is where the talented quit.

So set yourself a volume, not a masterpiece. Some number of small finished things — ten photographs printed, or a short story a week for a month, or one song demoed every few days until you have a dozen, whatever a unit of your work is. The point of the number is that it takes the pressure off any single piece. No one of them has to be good. They only have to be *made*, because each one is a rung, and you do not get the rungs by planning them.

When the disappointment comes — and it will come, right on schedule, the moment you look at what you actually made against what you could see in your head — practice naming it correctly. Say the true sentence out loud if you have to: *this is not as good as I can see that it should be, and that is because my taste is ahead of my hands, and the only thing wrong is that my hands are still young.* That sentence is not a consolation. It is the literal mechanism, stated plainly. Reach for it deliberately every time the false sentence — *I don't have it* — tries to arrive first.

And notice, over the weeks, the one form of progress that is actually available to a beginner. Your early things will be bad in the same few ways. Your later things will be bad in new ways. New badness is not failure. New badness is the sound of the skill climbing — you have solved the old problems well enough that you have earned harder ones. A maker who is failing in more interesting ways every month is a maker on the way up, no matter what the two-in-the-morning voice insists.

Keep your hands moving. The eye is already yours. The hand is coming. All it has ever asked of anyone is the years, and the years are only frightening from the near end of them.
