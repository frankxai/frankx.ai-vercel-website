# The Secret of Volume

On the first day, the ceramics teacher did something the students did not expect. He split the room down the middle.

Everyone on the left, he said, would be graded on quantity, and quantity alone. He would bring his bathroom scale to the final class and weigh the pots they had made across the semester. Fifty pounds of pots would earn an A, forty pounds a B, thirty a C, and so on down. He would not look at a single one of them. He did not care if they were beautiful or hideous, whole or collapsed. He cared only what the scale said.

Everyone on the right would be graded on quality, and quality alone. They needed to make one pot. Just one. But it had to be excellent. If, at the end of the semester, they produced a single pot of genuine quality, they would earn their A. The whole grade rested on one object, made as perfectly as they could make it.

Then he let them go to their wheels, and for a semester the two halves of the room lived in two different worlds.

The quantity half made pots the way you would expect people to make pots when nothing is at stake except tonnage. They made bad ones. They made lopsided, thick-walled, badly centered ones that slumped in the kiln. They made a great many of them, because the only way to add weight to the pile was to keep throwing clay, and so they kept throwing clay. And because they were making so many, they were also, without particularly meaning to, learning. Each collapsed pot taught the hands something the next pot remembered. They learned where the clay wanted to go and where it did not. They learned by the fortieth pot what the fourth pot could not have told them. Their skill climbed, quietly, pot by ruined pot.

The quality half sat and thought about excellence. They studied what makes a pot great. They planned. They deliberated over the perfect pot they were eventually going to make, and protected themselves from the risk of making a bad one by mostly not making anything at all.

At the end of the semester the teacher weighed the pile, and something had happened that he had known would happen. The best pots — the ones with real quality, the ones you would want to keep — came almost entirely from the quantity half of the room. The students graded on volume had, in the course of chasing sheer weight, made the finest work in the class. The students graded on quality had produced a stack of grand theories and a few timid, precious, mediocre pots.

The story is real. It comes from a book called *Art & Fear*, by David Bayles and Ted Orland, and it is the most useful thing ever written about the fear you feel standing over a lump of clay you might waste.

## The Pattern

Here is the secret the quantity students learned in their hands and the quality students never learned at all.

You cannot think your way to good work. You can only make your way to it.

This is not a motivational slogan. It is a description of how skill physically arrives, and it works the way the ceramics class worked, by a chain so plain it is almost dull. Volume gives you repetitions. Repetitions give you feedback — the pot slumps, the sentence clunks, the photo is dead, and each failure hands you one specific piece of information you did not have before. Feedback, absorbed enough times, becomes skill: the hands and the ear and the eye start to know, before the conscious mind can explain why, what will hold and what will fall. There is no shortcut across this. You do not get the feedback without the reps, and you do not get the reps without the volume, and you do not get the volume if you are spending the semester protecting one precious pot.

The quality students were not lazy. That is the part worth sitting with. They were, if anything, more serious than the others. They took the work so seriously that they could not bear to do it badly, and so they mostly did not do it, and by not doing it badly they arranged never to do it well. Their seriousness was the disease. They had confused thinking about quality with producing it, and those are not the same activity. One of them can be done in a chair. The other can only be done at the wheel, with wet clay ruining your sleeves.

There is a second thing volume does, quieter than the first and just as decisive. It breaks the grip of preciousness.

When you believe you are making one thing — the song, the essay, the photograph, the launch — every choice inside it carries an unbearable weight, because there is no other one to carry it. This single object has to justify the whole enterprise. It has to be the good one, because it is the only one, and under that pressure your hand tightens and your judgment curdles and you begin to strangle the very thing you are trying to make. Good work almost never happens under that pressure. Good work happens in a kind of looseness, a willingness to try the strange chord and cut the safe verse, and that looseness is only available to someone who knows there will be a next one.

Volume grants the looseness. When you have decided in advance to make a hundred, no single one of them has to be The One. If this song is bad, there are ninety-nine more, and the badness of this one costs you almost nothing except the clay. That lightness is not a minor comfort. It is the actual working condition under which good things get made. The maker who has freed himself to be bad ninety times is the maker who is loose enough to be great on the ninety-first — and the maker guarding a single precious pot never gets loose at all, and so never gets great.

Consider the photographer, because the camera makes this arithmetic visible. A photographer does not take ten good pictures. A photographer takes ten thousand frames to earn the ten that matter. The ten are not chosen in advance and executed; they are discovered afterward, pulled out of the flood, recognized as alive once they already exist. Nobody stands at the shutter knowing which frame will be the one. You get the one by shooting the thousand and then noticing, later, which few came alive. The volume is not wasted motion around the real work. The volume is how the real work is found.

This is the part that dismantles the whole fantasy of the masterpiece. The strong piece is not planned. It is found. You do not design the great song at your desk and then go execute it flawlessly; you write the hundred songs and then look back and see which three of them, for reasons you could not have predicted, came alive. The masterpiece was hiding inside the pile the entire time. It was in there with all the failures, indistinguishable from them until you had made enough of them to tell the difference. The only way to reach it was to make the pile you were afraid to make.

Which means perfectionism and low volume are not two problems. They are one problem wearing two faces. The perfectionist believes he is protecting quality by refusing to produce until he can produce something worthy. He is doing the exact opposite. He is starving himself of the only thing that produces quality, which is a large quantity of attempts, and calling the starvation a standard.

## A Second Proof

Two songwriters started the same year, in the same city, with roughly the same gift.

The first had a rule she had been given by an older musician, and the rule sounded almost insulting the first time she heard it: *write a hundred songs before you let yourself judge whether you are any good.* Not one hundred good songs. One hundred songs, full stop, of any quality, finished enough to count. She was not allowed to conclude anything about her talent, or quit, or despair, until the counter reached a hundred. Until then her only job was to keep the number climbing.

So she wrote badly, on purpose, at volume. She wrote a song about her landlord. She wrote three songs that were the same song. She wrote one in an afternoon that embarrassed her so much she never played it for anyone, and then she wrote the next one. Song eleven was worse than song nine. Song thirty was the first one that did not make her wince. Somewhere around song fifty she noticed she had stopped struggling with things that used to stop her cold — a bridge that would not resolve, a melody that sat too high — because she had solved those particular problems so many times that her hands now solved them without asking her. She was not writing better songs because she had gotten wiser about songwriting. She was writing better songs because she had written a great many songs, and the writing had taught her what the thinking never could.

The second songwriter had one song. It was, everyone agreed, a beautiful song, and he had been refining it for two years. He knew it was good, and precisely because he knew it was good, he could not stop working on it, because anything that good had to be made perfect, and perfect was always one more revision away. He changed the second verse and then changed it back. He rerecorded the vocal because a single note had a fray in it. He did not write new songs, because new songs would be worse than this one, and why would a serious artist spend his time making things worse than the best thing he had already made. He was protecting his one excellent pot.

Five years on, the difference between them was no longer a difference of degree. The first songwriter had a catalog, a sound, an ease at the instrument that let her follow an idea wherever it went, and a handful of songs that were genuinely, surprisingly good — songs she could not have written at the start and could not fully explain now, songs that had emerged somewhere in the pile without her planning them. She had also, along the way, become the kind of maker other makers wanted to work with, because she actually made things, reliably, and you could hand her a fragment on Monday and hear a finished thing by Friday.

The second songwriter still had the one beautiful song. It was still beautiful. It was, if anything, slightly worse than it had been three years earlier, sanded down in places where it should have been left alone, because a thing revised two hundred times loses the life that a thing made freshly has. And he had not grown at all, because growth comes from feedback and feedback comes from finishing things and meeting the next problem, and he had spent five years meeting the same problem over and over, learning nothing new because he was never anywhere new.

The cruelty of his position is that from the inside it felt like the higher standard. He looked at the first songwriter's pile of finished, imperfect, uneven songs and felt a quiet superiority: he would never release something that raw, that unfinished, that *much*. He mistook his own paralysis for discernment. But discernment is a thing you develop by making many pots and learning to tell them apart, and he had made one pot, and so the only thing he could truly discern was that pot, endlessly, forever. His taste had nothing to feed on but itself.

## The Secret, Named

> You cannot think your way to good work. You can only make your way to it, badly at first, and in quantity.
>
> The pile is not the enemy of the masterpiece. The pile is where the masterpiece is hiding. It is in there, indistinguishable from the failures, and the only way to find it is to make enough failures that it finally stands out from them.
>
> Volume is not the opposite of quality. Volume is how quality is manufactured. Every rep hands you a piece of feedback, every piece of feedback becomes a grain of skill, and there is no way to collect the skill except to spend the reps.
>
> And volume does one more thing, harder to see. It takes the unbearable weight off any single attempt. When you have decided to make a hundred, no one of them has to be the good one, and that lightness — that permission to be bad this time — is the exact condition under which you finally make something good.
>
> The strong piece is found, not planned. You will not design it in advance. You will make many, and look back, and see which few came alive.
>
> So make many. The person guarding one precious thing and the person too afraid to begin are not two people. They are the same person, starving the work of the only thing that ever feeds it.

## The Practice

This week, pick the smallest complete unit of your craft — one song, one page, one sketch, one recorded take, one shipped small thing — and commit, in advance, to a number. Not a masterpiece. A number.

The number should be uncomfortably high. High enough that no single one of them can possibly matter, which is the entire point. If you write, thirty pages this week, not one good one. If you photograph, a thousand frames. If you make music, ten sketches, none of which has to become anything. Whatever the unit is, set the count somewhere past the line where preciousness can survive, because preciousness is exactly the thing you are trying to kill, and it dies only in volume.

Then hold two rules while you make them. The first: finish each one enough to count. A song abandoned at the second verse does not add weight to the pile; it just becomes another thing you didn't finish. The bar is not good, but it is done. The second rule: do not judge any of them until you reach the number. You are forbidden from concluding anything — that you have talent, that you don't, that this one is the good one, that you should stop — until the counter is full. Judgment is a later task. This week's task is only to make the pile.

You will feel the pull, around attempt six or seven, to slow down and start protecting one of them, to sense a good one coming and hoard your care for it. That pull is the disease. It is the quality half of the room, sitting in its chair, theorizing about the perfect pot. Do not obey it. Keep the number climbing. The good one, if it comes, will come out of the volume on its own, and you will recognize it afterward, and you would never have reached it by aiming.

Make the pile you have been afraid to make. Everything you are trying to protect is already inside it, waiting for you to make enough to find it.
