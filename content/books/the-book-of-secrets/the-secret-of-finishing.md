# The Secret of Finishing

The hard drive had a folder called *Almost*, and the man had named it as a joke, years ago, before it stopped being funny.

Inside it were forty-one things. A novel that stopped at the second-to-last chapter, because he had solved the interesting problems and all that remained was the un-interesting one of typing the ending he already knew. An album mixed to the point where only the mastering was left, the tedious pass where you make a hundred inaudible decisions and none of them are fun. A woodworking piece, photographed at the stage where it looked finished from across the room and was in fact three evenings of sanding and one coat of finish away from being a thing a person could actually own. A course, ninety percent scripted. An app that ran perfectly on his own machine and had never once been given to anyone else, because giving it to anyone else meant the error messages and the sign-up flow and the small ugly work of making it survive contact with a stranger.

None of them had died at the start. That was the thing he could not explain to himself on the nights he opened the folder. He was good at starting. He was, if anything, addicted to starting — the first sketch, the first draft, the first week when the whole shape of the thing arrives at once and the making feels like receiving. His friends knew him as the one with the ideas. *You always have something going,* they said, and meant it kindly, and he heard it each time as a small verdict, because a thing that is always *going* is a thing that never *arrives.*

Each abandonment had felt reasonable in the moment. That was the quietest part of it. He had never once sat down and decided to quit something. He had simply reached the stretch where the thrill was spent and the work turned to grinding, and right then — always right then, with a timing so consistent it should have warned him — a new idea would arrive, bright and whole and unspoiled by any of the boring middle he had not yet had to do to it. And the new idea was *better.* It was always better. Of course it was better. It had no ending to type yet, no mastering pass, no sanding. It was all beginning, and beginning was the part he was good at, and so he would open a new folder with real excitement and leave the old one at ninety percent, and the *Almost* folder would gain one more resident who had come so close.

He was, by any fair measure, one of the most talented people his friends knew. And he had, in fifteen years of making, finished almost nothing — while a slower, plainer woman he'd gone to school with, who had less of everything he had more of, had somehow put four actual books into the actual world.

## The Pattern

We talk about making as though it were one skill. It is at least two, and they are not close relatives.

Starting is one skill. Finishing is another, and it is the rarer of the two, and almost no one is told this at the outset, because at the outset all anyone can see is the starting. The blank page, the empty track, the untouched block of wood — these are what a beginner fears, and so all the encouragement a beginner receives is aimed at the beginning: *just start, start badly, start anyway.* It is good advice. It is also only the first half of the problem, and the half that turns out, over a life, to be the easier one to solve.

Here is the mechanism, and it is not flattering, which is part of why it stays hidden.

Every project has a shape, and the shape is not flat. The first stretch is downhill and lit from the sky — the vision is fresh, the possibilities are infinite, and the work feels less like labor than like discovery. But every project has a final tenth, and the final tenth is uphill in the dark. It is where the interesting problems have all been solved and only the boring ones remain. The ending you already know but have to actually render. The bug that isn't conceptually hard, just tedious to find. The formatting, the export, the last coat, the second proofread, the hundred small unglamorous decisions that no one will ever praise and everyone would notice the absence of. The final tenth is not where the genius lives. It is where the janitor lives. And most work does not die at the start, where everyone is watching for it to die. It dies here, in the last tenth, in what we might as well call the ninety-percent graveyard — the vast, silent field of things that were *almost.*

What kills them is not laziness and not lack of talent. What kills them is that the final tenth is unfun in exactly the moment a new project's first tenth is maximally fun, and the maker, feeling the pull of the fresh thing, mistakes that pull for inspiration when it is really just avoidance wearing inspiration's clothes. The new idea is not better. It only *feels* better, because you are comparing a beginning to an ending, a first date to a Tuesday, and beginnings always win that comparison. They win it every time. That is why the folder fills.

Now here is the part that turns this from a lament into a secret worth the price of the book.

Finishing is a skill, which means it is *built*, which means it is built the only way skills are built — by doing the thing, repeatedly, including the parts that are not fun. And it is built by nothing else. You cannot learn to finish by starting more. You cannot learn it by reading about it. You learn to finish by finishing, and every completed thing hands you the one lesson that lives only at the end, the lesson that cannot be extracted any other way: how a whole thing actually behaves when it is whole. How the ending changes the beginning in ways you could not have predicted from inside the beginning. How the piece resists in the last stretch, and what it costs to push it over the line anyway, and — this is the reward — how it feels on the other side, which is the feeling that teaches your body the shape of the entire arc so that next time you can see the finish from the middle.

An abandoned thing teaches you almost none of this. It teaches you the first tenth, which you already knew, and then it teaches you a subtler and more poisonous thing without your consent: it trains the habit of abandoning. Each thing you quit at ninety percent makes quitting at ninety percent slightly more natural, slightly more your default, until abandoning is not a failure you occasionally suffer but a groove you reliably fall into. The folder is not just a record of unfinished work. It is a training program, and what it has been training, patiently, for years, is the reflex to leave.

Which is why the finisher pulls ahead of the more talented non-finisher, and keeps pulling ahead, and eventually is not even in the same race. Not because the finisher is smarter — often they are not — but because each finished thing is one complete lesson, the entire arc from start to end, and the finisher is accumulating whole lessons while the more gifted starter accumulates only openings. Ten years on, the starter has had a thousand brilliant beginnings and learned each of them a thousand times. The finisher has completed forty modest things and learned forty complete arcs, and the fortieth is very good, because they have been all the way around the loop thirty-nine times and know every step of the road, including the dark uphill part at the end that the starter has never once walked to its finish.

And often — this is the practical hinge the whole thing turns on — the way to finish is not to work harder. It is to *want less* of this particular thing. To lower the scope, not the effort. The final tenth so often stays unreachable because the maker keeps enlarging the target while trying to hit it — the novel that keeps growing another subplot, the app that keeps sprouting another feature, the piece whose ambition creeps upward faster than the work advances, so that it is forever ninety percent of a target that keeps receding. Shrink the target. Decide what the thing actually needs to be in order to be *done*, make it that, and let the rest be the next thing. Scope is the dial you turn to reach the finish line. Effort is not.

## A Second Proof

Two people took the same drawing class, the way people do, in the evenings, after work.

The first was plainly gifted. You could see it in the first week — a loose, confident line, a natural sense of where the weight of a thing sat. She started drawings constantly, in the margins of everything, on napkins, on the backs of receipts, and the starts were often beautiful. But she almost never carried one to a finished piece. She would get the drawing to the stage where its bones were right and its promise was obvious, and then, facing the long unglamorous labor of rendering — the shadows, the corrections, the tightening, the part where you stop discovering and start grinding — she would feel a new subject call to her, and she would turn the page. Her sketchbook was a gallery of magnificent beginnings. Ask her for a finished drawing and she would flip through it, a little embarrassed, and find nothing she could hand you.

The second had a heavier hand and knew it. His lines were stiff, his proportions off, his instincts nowhere near hers. But he had one rule, arrived at not through wisdom but through stubbornness: he finished every drawing he began. Every one. Even the bad ones. *Especially* the bad ones, because those were the ones most tempting to abandon and therefore the ones where the rule did the most work. When a drawing turned ugly halfway through — and his turned ugly often — he did not turn the page. He pushed it all the way to done, ugly and complete, and signed the bottom, and only then began the next.

Watch what the finishing did to him, because it did not announce itself and it did not happen fast. In the labor of rendering the drawings he would rather have abandoned, he learned the things that live only in the last half of a drawing. He learned how a shadow he'd placed early was wrong in a way he could not have seen until the whole thing was nearly done — a lesson the gifted woman, who never got there, never once received. He learned how to rescue a piece that had gone wrong at the halfway mark instead of fleeing it, so over time fewer of his pieces went wrong. He learned, most of all, the plain animal endurance of the final tenth — how to keep his hand moving through the boring dark part — and endurance, it turns out, is a muscle, and his was growing while hers was not being used at all.

A year in, his finished drawings were merely competent, and hers, on the rare occasions she finished one, were still more beautiful in their bones. Three years in, the gap had closed. Five years in, it had reversed, and it kept reversing, because he was completing whole arcs and she was completing openings, and whole arcs compound while openings only repeat. He was not more talented at year five than at year one. He had simply been all the way around the loop a few hundred times, and each full loop had taught him the entire thing, including the ending, while she had been learning the same gorgeous first move over and over and calling the collection a body of work.

And here is the part that should unsettle anyone who has ever comforted themselves with an unfinished masterpiece. Her best abandoned drawing — genuinely her best, a thing of real promise — taught her nothing, could be shown to no one, and freed her to begin nothing, because it was still sitting on her, unfinished, a debt she carried into every new start. His worst *finished* drawing did all three. It taught him a full lesson. It could be set on a table and looked at and reacted to. And it was *over*, which meant he was free — light, unburdened, clear to begin the next thing clean. A finished mediocre thing beats an unfinished masterpiece, not as a consolation and not as an excuse for shoddiness, but as a plain matter of what each one can do. Only the finished thing can teach you. Only the finished thing can be shown. Only the finished thing lets go of your ankle so you can walk to the next one. The unfinished masterpiece does none of that. It is not a masterpiece. It is a hostage, and you are the one it is holding.

## The Practice

This week, finish one thing. Not the best thing. The nearest thing.

Go to your own *Almost* folder — you have one, everyone who makes has one, even if it lives in a drawer or a corner of a hard drive you avoid opening — and find the resident closest to the line. Not the most ambitious. The most nearly *done.* The one where finishing is least about inspiration and most about simply walking the last stretch of uphill dark. That is the one. Its very unglamorousness is the point, because the skill you are building this week is not starting, which you already have, and not brilliance, which is not on offer in a final tenth. The skill is finishing, and you build it on the thing that needs finishing least dramatically and most literally.

Before you touch it, do the one move that makes finishing possible: lower the scope. Look hard at what remains and ask what this thing actually needs to be *done* — not perfect, not everything it could have been, done — and cut the target down to that. The subplot you were going to add: not this thing. The feature you were dreaming of: the next thing. Draw the finish line where you can actually reach it, then reach it. You are not lowering your standards. You are refusing to let a receding target keep a real thing perpetually *almost.*

Then walk the last stretch. It will be boring. That is not a sign you have chosen wrong; it is a sign you have finally reached the part where finishers are made. Type the ending you already know. Do the tedious pass. Sign the bottom. Push it over the line while it is ugly and you are tired, because that specific act — completing a thing you would rather have left — is the rep that builds the muscle none of your beginnings ever will.

And when it is done, notice what you are holding. Not a masterpiece. Something better, for now: a *finished* thing, which can teach you, be shown, and free you — three things the drawer full of *almost* has never once done. Then raise the bar, and aim it at the next one. That is the only place a raised bar can ever land: on work you will actually finish. The unfinished never gets better. Only the next thing does.
